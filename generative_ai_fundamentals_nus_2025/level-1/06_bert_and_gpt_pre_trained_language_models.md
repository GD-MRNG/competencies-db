# L1-06 · BERT and GPT: Pre-trained Language Models

The most important decision in a language model's life is made before it sees a single downstream task. It is the choice of pre-training objective — the made-up game the model plays for billions of tokens — and that choice determines what the model can and cannot do for the rest of its existence. BERT and GPT, which appeared within months of each other in 2018, are the two canonical answers to the question of what game to play. They use the same underlying transformer machinery. They differ in one decision, and that decision propagates through everything: what each model is good at, what it cannot do, how you fine-tune it, how you prompt it, and ultimately why one lineage gave us classifiers and the other gave us ChatGPT.

The shared insight underneath both is that you do not need labelled data to teach a transformer something useful about language. The web is full of text. If you can invent a task whose answer is already implicit in the text — predict a missing word, predict the next word — you can train enormous models on essentially free supervision. The model that emerges has absorbed an extraordinary amount of structure: syntax, factual associations, stylistic patterns, weak forms of reasoning. This is pre-training. What you do afterwards — fine-tuning on a labelled task, or simply prompting the model with an example — is a comparatively small adjustment on top of that foundation. The pre-train-then-adapt paradigm is the central organising idea, and BERT and GPT are the two ways it was first made to work at scale.

BERT plays a fill-in-the-blank game called masked language modelling. You take a sentence, hide fifteen percent of the tokens, and ask the model to predict what was hidden using everything else in the sentence — the words to the left, the words to the right, all of it. The consequence is that BERT's internal representation of any given token is informed by the full bidirectional context. This is exactly what you want when your task is to understand a piece of text: classify its sentiment, extract named entities, decide whether two sentences entail each other, answer a question by pointing at a span. BERT representations became, almost overnight, the standard input to nearly every text understanding system that mattered, because a sentence vector that has seen the whole sentence in both directions is simply a richer thing than one that has only seen the prefix.

GPT plays the opposite game. It reads left to right and predicts the next token, with no peeking. This is causal language modelling, and the constraint — that the model only ever sees what comes before — is not a limitation to be apologised for. It is what makes generation possible. A model trained to predict the next token can, at inference time, generate text by predicting one token, appending it, and predicting the next. BERT cannot do this; its bidirectional training assumed it would always have the full context, so it has no coherent procedure for producing text from nothing. GPT can. The same constraint that makes GPT representations weaker for some understanding tasks is the constraint that makes GPT a generator, and the entire lineage of fluent text generation — InstructGPT, ChatGPT, Claude, the rest — descends from this single architectural commitment.

Once you have a pre-trained model, you have to adapt it to do something specific, and here the two paradigms diverge again. BERT's natural adaptation mode is fine-tuning: you bolt a small task-specific head onto the encoder, run a few epochs of supervised training on labelled examples, and you have a classifier. This works extraordinarily well, but it requires labelled data and produces a model that does one thing. GPT, especially at scale, opened a different door. If the model is large enough and has seen enough text, you can sometimes get it to do a new task simply by describing the task in the prompt and showing a few examples — no weight updates, no training, no labelled dataset. This is in-context learning, and it was not predicted by anyone; it emerged from scaling causal language models and has become the dominant way people interact with GPT-family systems. The tradeoffs between fine-tuning and prompting — data cost, latency, generality, controllability — are now a core deployment decision, not an academic curiosity.

The lineages have proliferated and the distinctions blur in places. RoBERTa is BERT trained better. DistilBERT is BERT compressed. T5 reframes everything as text-to-text using an encoder-decoder. The GPT line scaled from GPT-2 to GPT-3 to GPT-4, accumulating capabilities — few-shot learning, chain-of-thought reasoning, instruction following — that were not visible at smaller scale. But the underlying split is still the right way to read the field. When someone says they are using a language model, the first useful question is whether the objective was masked or causal, because that tells you most of what you need to know about what the model can do. Encoder-only models are for understanding. Decoder-only models are for generation. Encoder-decoder models try to do both and pay for it in complexity.

The skill this topic builds is the ability to read a new model's release announcement and predict, before running a single benchmark, what it will be good at. If it is decoder-only and pre-trained on next-token prediction, it will generate fluently and you will adapt it through prompting. If it is encoder-only and pre-trained with masking, it will produce excellent representations and you will adapt it by fine-tuning. The pre-training objective is the model's character, and almost everything else is a footnote.

## Level 2 candidates

**Masked language modelling (MLM)** — Covers BERT's specific pre-training objective: the masking ratio, what gets masked, and the [MASK] token's role in training versus inference. Worth deeper study because the design choices around masking (whole-word masking, span masking, the mismatch between training and inference distributions) are subtle and explain why successor models like RoBERTa and SpanBERT outperformed the original.

**Causal language modelling (CLM)** — Covers the autoregressive objective and the attention masking that enforces it. Worth deeper study because understanding why a single, almost trivially simple objective scaled into general capability — and why bidirectional models did not scale the same way — is one of the most important empirical puzzles in the field.

**Fine-tuning vs prompting** — Covers the practical question of how to get a pre-trained model to do your specific task: gradient-based adaptation versus input-based adaptation. Worth deeper study because the tradeoffs (data requirements, latency, cost, controllability, generalisation) drive almost every real deployment decision and are not obvious without working through concrete cases.

**BERT applications: classification, NER, QA** — Covers the canonical downstream tasks where BERT-style encoders are still the right tool, including how task-specific heads attach to the encoder. Worth deeper study because seeing exactly where bidirectional representations excel — and where they hit walls — is what calibrates the intuition for when to reach for an encoder versus a decoder.

**GPT scaling behaviour and emergence** — Covers the empirical phenomenon that GPT-family capabilities (few-shot learning, instruction following, chain-of-thought) appear non-linearly with scale rather than smoothly. Worth deeper study because emergence is poorly understood theoretically but has direct practical consequences for what you can expect from a model of a given size.

**Model lineages: RoBERTa, DistilBERT, T5** — Covers the most influential descendants of BERT and GPT and what specific weakness each was designed to address. Worth deeper study because tracing these lineages is how you learn to read a new model paper critically — to see what claim is being made and what prior limitation it targets.
