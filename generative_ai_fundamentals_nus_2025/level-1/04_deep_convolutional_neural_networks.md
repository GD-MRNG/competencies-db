# L1-04 · Deep Convolutional Neural Networks

For about a decade before 2012, computer vision was a craft. You built features by hand — SIFT, HOG, colour histograms — and fed them to a classifier, and the quality of your system was largely the quality of your feature engineering. Then AlexNet won ImageNet by roughly ten percentage points over the next-best entry, and the assumption that humans should design the features collapsed. The model had learned them. What replaced the craft was an architecture that, on the surface, looks like a small set of operations stacked many times — convolutions, nonlinearities, pooling, repeat — but which encodes a specific and consequential bet about what images are and how to learn from them.

The bet has two parts, and getting them clear is the whole point of studying DCNNs. The first part is spatial locality: pixels close to each other in an image are correlated, and the structure that matters at the lowest level (edges, textures, small shapes) is local. You do not need to look at the whole image to detect an edge; you need to look at a small neighbourhood. The second part is parameter sharing: an edge detector that works in the top-left corner of the image should also work in the bottom-right. So instead of learning a separate set of weights for every position in the image, you learn one small filter and slide it across every position. A convolutional layer is, mechanically, exactly this: a learned filter applied at every spatial location, producing a feature map that says "here is where this pattern appears in the image."

Stacking these layers is what produces the depth in deep convolutional networks, and the stacking is doing real work. Early layers learn filters that respond to edges and colour gradients. Middle layers compose those into textures and parts — corners, curves, eye-like shapes. Late layers compose parts into objects. This hierarchical representation was not engineered into the network; it emerged from training a stack of convolutions on enough labelled images. That is the deep insight of the architecture: by enforcing the right inductive biases (locality, sharing, hierarchy) and giving the model enough data, you get a feature hierarchy for free that no human team could have hand-designed.

Pooling sits between the convolutional layers and does something that looks crude at first: it takes a region of the feature map and reduces it to a single value, typically the maximum. This deliberately discards information. The reason it helps is that exact spatial position is usually not what you care about — you care that an eye is somewhere in the upper region of a face, not that it is at pixel (143, 87). Pooling builds in a tolerance to small translations and reduces the size of the representation as you go deeper, which keeps computation tractable. It is a tradeoff: you lose precise localisation in exchange for invariance and efficiency. This same tradeoff returns in different form when you study vision transformers, where the question of how much spatial structure to preserve becomes a design decision rather than a baked-in assumption.

The reason DCNNs are still relevant after the transformer revolution is that the inductive biases they encode are genuinely useful for images, and useful inductive biases mean you can train with less data. A vision transformer treats an image as a sequence of patches and learns the spatial relationships from scratch; a CNN has those relationships wired in. When you have hundreds of millions of labelled images, the transformer's flexibility wins. When you have ten thousand, the CNN's bias is a feature. This is why CNNs remain in production for medical imaging, satellite analysis, and many industrial vision tasks where data is finite. It is also why pre-trained CNNs became one of the field's most reused assets: the features a network learns on ImageNet transfer remarkably well to unrelated vision tasks, and fine-tuning a pre-trained CNN on a small dataset routinely outperforms training from scratch. This same logic — pre-train on a large general corpus, fine-tune on a small specific one — is the template that BERT and GPT later applied to language.

The flip side, and the reason the curriculum eventually leaves CNNs behind for transformers, is that the locality bias that helps with images actively hurts in domains where long-range structure matters. Language is the obvious example. The relationship between a pronoun and its antecedent can span dozens of words; the meaning of a sentence depends on tokens that are nowhere near each other. A convolution with a small receptive field cannot see this relationship; you would have to stack convolutions deep enough that the receptive field covers the whole sequence, and even then the model is forced to compose long-range information through many local steps. This is not a minor inefficiency — it is the wrong shape of bias for the problem. Self-attention, which lets every token directly attend to every other token, is the right shape. Understanding why convolutions fail at this is exactly what makes the design of the transformer feel inevitable rather than arbitrary.

Applying convolutions to non-spatial data is the same category error in another guise. Tabular data has no spatial structure: the column ordering is arbitrary, and there is no meaningful sense in which "nearby" columns are correlated. Running a convolutional architecture over tabular data produces a model that is doing the wrong thing — imposing a locality bias on data that has none. It will sometimes work, in the way that any flexible model will fit something, but it is leaving information on the table and adding constraints that hurt rather than help. Knowing when an architecture's inductive biases match the problem is one of the most consequential modelling skills you can develop, and DCNNs are the cleanest place to learn it. The architecture is principled when the problem has spatial locality; it is a category error when the problem does not.

**Convolutional layers and receptive fields** — How a single learned filter is applied across an image to produce a feature map, and how stacking layers expands the effective receptive field to capture larger structures. Worth deeper study because the receptive field calculation explains both why CNNs work on images and why they struggle with long-range dependencies — the same insight that motivates self-attention.

**Pooling and translation invariance** — The mechanics of max and average pooling, and the deliberate tradeoff between spatial precision and invariance that pooling introduces. Worth a deeper look because the same information-discarding tradeoff reappears in patch tokenisation in vision transformers, and understanding it once carries forward.

**Transfer learning with pre-trained CNNs** — The empirical finding that features learned on ImageNet transfer to unrelated vision tasks, and the practical recipe of freezing early layers and fine-tuning later ones. Deserves attention because this is the historical template for the pre-train-then-adapt paradigm that now dominates language modelling.

**DCNN for tabular vs image data** — The diagnostic question of when convolutional inductive biases match the data and when they constitute a category error. Worth deeper treatment because it generalises into a broader skill: reasoning about whether a given architecture's biases fit a given problem, which recurs every time you choose a model.

**AlexNet and the historical inflection point** — The specific architectural and training choices (ReLU, dropout, GPU training, data augmentation) that let AlexNet win ImageNet by an unprecedented margin. Worth understanding because the same choices, in modified form, are still load-bearing in modern architectures, and the historical context explains why the field shifted so abruptly.
