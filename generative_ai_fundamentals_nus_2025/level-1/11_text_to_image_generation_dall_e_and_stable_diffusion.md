# L1-11 · Text-to-Image Generation: DALL-E and Stable Diffusion

The interesting thing about text-to-image systems is not that they generate images. It is that they generate images from text — and text and images are radically different kinds of data. A sentence is a short, discrete sequence of tokens drawn from a finite vocabulary. An image is a dense grid of millions of continuous-valued pixels with no inherent ordering. Bridging these two is the actual engineering problem, and the two systems that defined the field — DALL-E and Stable Diffusion — solved it in opposite ways. Studying them together is what stops their design choices from looking arbitrary.

DALL-E's approach, in its original 2021 form, was to refuse to treat images as fundamentally different from text. If a transformer can generate language one token at a time, why not generate an image one token at a time? The trick is that you first need to convert images into discrete tokens (via a learned codebook), at which point an image becomes a long sequence of integers and the same autoregressive machinery that powers GPT can predict it. The architectural simplicity is the appeal: one model, one objective, one inference loop. The cost is paid at sampling time. Generating an image means generating thousands of tokens sequentially, and resolution scales the sequence length quadratically. This is why pure autoregressive image models have largely lost ground for high-resolution work, even though the paradigm remains conceptually clean and continues to influence multimodal architectures.

Stable Diffusion took the opposite bet. Instead of forcing images into the shape of language, it kept the diffusion formulation — gradually denoise random noise into a coherent image — and brought text in as a conditioning signal. The architecture has three distinct components, and tracing data through them once is the most clarifying exercise you can do for this topic. A text prompt enters a CLIP text encoder, which converts it into a sequence of embeddings that capture its semantic content. Those embeddings are injected via cross-attention into a U-Net that runs the denoising process not in pixel space but in the compressed latent space of a pre-trained VAE. After enough denoising steps, the VAE's decoder turns the final latent back into an image. Each component is doing one job: CLIP understands the prompt, the U-Net does the generation, the VAE handles compression. The whole system is a composition of pieces from earlier in the curriculum, which is why understanding it sideways unlocks intuition about how foundation models get assembled.

The reason latent diffusion mattered so much is computational. Running diffusion directly in pixel space at 512×512 resolution is brutally expensive; running it in a 64×64 latent space with a VAE doing the upscaling is tractable on a consumer GPU. This is what made Stable Diffusion's open release in 2022 a watershed event — not just the architecture, but the fact that the architecture made local inference and fine-tuning practical. DALL-E was a product. Stable Diffusion became an ecosystem.

The philosophical difference between the two systems is worth sitting with. DALL-E treats generation as a sequence prediction problem and lets the transformer's flexibility carry the weight. Stable Diffusion treats generation as iterative denoising and lets text conditioning shape an otherwise unconditional process. Neither is wrong. They make different tradeoffs about where to put the modelling burden, what kind of compute they assume at inference, and how controllable the generation process needs to be. Diffusion gives you natural handles like classifier-free guidance scale, negative prompts, and intermediate-step editing. Autoregression gives you a unified objective and easier integration with other modalities. The current generation of frontier image models (and the multimodal models that include image generation) have largely converged on diffusion or hybrid approaches, but the autoregressive paradigm is far from dead — it has resurfaced in image-token-aware multimodal LLMs.

This brings you to prompt engineering, which is not a parlour trick. The empirical regularities — that style modifiers like "oil painting" or "octane render" reliably shift output distribution, that negative prompts suppress unwanted features more effectively than verbal negation in the positive prompt, that token order matters more than prose grammar suggests — are downstream consequences of how conditioning enters these models. CLIP was trained on internet image-caption pairs, so prompts that resemble the way humans actually caption images on the internet work better than prompts written like instructions. Negative prompts work because classifier-free guidance literally subtracts an "undesired" embedding from the "desired" one during sampling. Once you understand the architecture, the empirical craft of prompting stops looking like superstition and starts looking like a learnable system with predictable failure modes.

Personalisation is where this gets practically interesting. Fine-tuning a Stable Diffusion model on a handful of images of a specific person or style — via DreamBooth, LoRA, or textual inversion — is what makes these models useful beyond demos. Each method modifies a different surface area of the model: textual inversion learns a new token embedding, DreamBooth fine-tunes the U-Net itself, LoRA inserts low-rank adapters that are cheap to train and cheap to swap. Choosing among them is a practical skill that depends on how much data you have, how much compute you can afford, and how many personalised concepts you eventually want to compose.

What you should walk away with is the ability to read a text-to-image system's architecture diagram and explain, in order, what each component does and why it is there. If you can do that for both DALL-E and Stable Diffusion, you have the mental model. Everything else — new samplers, new conditioning schemes, new fine-tuning methods — is a variation on these themes.

## Level 2 candidates

**DALL-E's autoregressive image generation** — How images are tokenised into discrete codes and generated sequentially by a transformer in the same way as text. Worth deeper study because it is the cleanest example of treating modality as a tokenisation problem, and the paradigm is resurfacing in modern multimodal LLMs that generate text and images from a unified vocabulary.

**Stable Diffusion's full architecture** — The end-to-end pipeline from text prompt through CLIP text encoder, into a latent-space U-Net with cross-attention conditioning, to VAE decoding. Worth deeper study because tracing a single inference through this composition is the highest-leverage exercise for understanding how modern generative systems are assembled from pre-trained components.

**Prompt engineering for image generation** — The empirical craft of writing prompts that produce intended results, including style modifiers, negative prompts, and weighting syntax. Worth deeper study because the regularities are not arbitrary — they are direct consequences of CLIP's training distribution and how classifier-free guidance manipulates the sampling trajectory, so understanding the mechanism turns trial-and-error into informed iteration.

**Fine-tuning: DreamBooth, LoRA, and textual inversion** — Methods for personalising a pre-trained diffusion model to specific subjects or styles, each modifying a different part of the model. Worth deeper study because the choice between them is a real engineering decision with implications for training cost, storage, composability, and overfitting, and understanding what each method actually changes about the weights is the entry point into parameter-efficient fine-tuning generally.

**Ethical considerations and misuse vectors** — The specific harms enabled by text-to-image systems (non-consensual imagery, deepfakes, copyright collisions, training data provenance) and the partial nature of technical mitigations like NSFW filters and dataset curation. Worth deeper study because the failure modes are technical as well as social — understanding why filters can be bypassed and why training data shapes downstream bias requires the same architectural understanding as building the systems in the first place.
