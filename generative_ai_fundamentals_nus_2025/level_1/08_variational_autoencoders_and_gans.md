# L1-08 · Variational Autoencoders and GANs

If you have only ever worked with diffusion models, it is tempting to treat VAEs and GANs as historical curiosities — the generative architectures that came before the good one. This is a mistake, and the mistake is not nostalgia. It is that diffusion models inherit most of their mathematical machinery from VAEs and most of their conditioning intuitions from GANs, and Stable Diffusion in particular has a VAE sitting inside it doing essential work. If you cannot articulate what a VAE is approximating or why a GAN's training loop is unstable, the design decisions in modern image generation will look arbitrary instead of principled.

The mental model to start with is that a generative model is trying to learn a probability distribution over images — not the images themselves, but the underlying distribution from which real images are drawn. Once you have that distribution, sampling from it gives you new images that look real. VAEs and GANs are two radically different mathematical strategies for solving this same problem. VAEs try to write down the distribution explicitly and optimise a tractable approximation of it. GANs refuse to write it down and instead set up a game in which one network learns to generate samples that another network cannot distinguish from real ones. Almost every property that distinguishes the two families — training stability, sample quality, latent space structure, mode coverage — falls out of this single difference in approach.

The VAE descends from the autoencoder: a network that compresses an input into a low-dimensional latent code and then reconstructs it. A plain autoencoder learns a useful compression but its latent space is a pile of disconnected points; you cannot sample from it because there is no distribution defined over it. The variational autoencoder fixes this by forcing the encoder to output a distribution over latent codes (typically a Gaussian) rather than a single point, and by penalising the encoder when that distribution drifts too far from a standard normal prior. The result is a latent space that you can sample from coherently, with a decoder that maps those samples back to images. The mathematical trick that makes the whole thing trainable end-to-end by gradient descent is the reparameterisation trick — a sleight of hand that moves the randomness out of the computation graph so gradients can flow through it. What the VAE is actually approximating, under the hood, is a lower bound on the data likelihood (the ELBO), and understanding this is the key that unlocks diffusion models, which can be derived as a hierarchical VAE with a particular fixed encoder.

The GAN takes the opposite approach. There is no encoder, no explicit latent distribution to fit, no likelihood being optimised. Instead, a generator network maps random noise to images, and a discriminator network is trained to tell generated images apart from real ones. The two networks are trained against each other: the generator wants to fool the discriminator, the discriminator wants to not be fooled. At equilibrium, the generator produces samples indistinguishable from the data distribution. The conceptual elegance is striking. The practical reality is that this minimax game is notoriously unstable. The two networks can oscillate, one can overpower the other, gradients can vanish, and the generator can collapse to producing a tiny set of outputs that happen to fool the discriminator — the canonical failure mode known as mode collapse. Most of the GAN literature from 2014 onward is patches and reformulations addressing these training pathologies.

The tradeoff between the two families is sharp and worth internalising. VAEs are stable to train and give you a meaningful, structured latent space you can interpolate through and reason about — but their samples tend to be blurry, because the reconstruction loss averages over plausible outputs rather than committing to one. GANs produce sharp, high-fidelity samples — but their training is fragile, their latent space is less interpretable, and they can silently fail to cover the full diversity of the data. If you need a latent representation, you reach for a VAE. If you need photorealism and you can afford the engineering pain, you reach for a GAN. Diffusion models eventually achieved both, which is why they took over.

Both families become genuinely useful when you can condition them — generate an image of a specific class, or matching a text prompt, rather than sampling unconditionally. Conditioning is conceptually simple (feed the condition into both networks alongside the noise or latent code) but it introduces its own tradeoff between fidelity to the condition and diversity of outputs. This tension reappears throughout generative modelling, including in classifier-free guidance for diffusion, and the GAN and VAE conditioning literature is where the intuitions were first developed.

The reason to spend real time on these architectures is not to deploy them — in 2024, you probably will not. It is that they are the cleanest setting in which to learn what generative modelling actually is. A VAE forces you to think about latent spaces, priors, and variational bounds. A GAN forces you to think about distribution matching without likelihoods, and about why training dynamics matter as much as objectives. Carry those intuitions into diffusion, and the field stops looking like a sequence of unrelated tricks and starts looking like a coherent set of answers to a small number of well-posed questions. Skip them, and you will be reading every diffusion paper from outside the conversation rather than inside it.

**Autoencoders and latent representations** — Covers the basic encoder-decoder bottleneck and the idea that forcing reconstruction through a low-dimensional code produces useful representations. Worth a deeper treatment because the latent-space concept is the ancestor of every modern generative architecture, including the latent space that Stable Diffusion operates in.

**VAE reparameterisation trick** — Covers the specific mathematical move that lets you backpropagate through a sampling operation by rewriting it as a deterministic function of noise. Worth going deeper because it is the lever that makes the VAE trainable at all, and the same trick reappears in policy gradient methods and normalising flows.

**The ELBO and what VAEs are actually optimising** — Covers the variational lower bound on the data likelihood and why optimising it is a principled approximation to maximum likelihood. Worth a Level 2 because diffusion models are most cleanly understood as hierarchical VAEs with a fixed forward process, and that derivation is opaque without the ELBO.

**GAN training dynamics and mode collapse** — Covers why the minimax objective is unstable in practice, what mode collapse looks like, and the families of fixes (Wasserstein GANs, gradient penalties, spectral normalisation). Worth deeper study because the failure modes are subtle, well-characterised, and illustrate a general principle about adversarial objectives that recurs in RLHF reward hacking.

**Conditional generation** — Covers how class labels or text embeddings are injected into VAEs and GANs to control their outputs, and the fidelity-versus-diversity tradeoff this creates. Worth going deeper because the conditioning machinery developed here is what classifier-free guidance and cross-attention conditioning in diffusion models build on directly.
