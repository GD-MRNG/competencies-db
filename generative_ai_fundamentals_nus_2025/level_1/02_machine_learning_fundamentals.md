# L1-02 · Machine Learning Fundamentals

Most people who arrive at machine learning through modern tools — through ChatGPT, through Copilot, through whatever embedding API they are using this week — have absorbed the vocabulary without the substrate. They know what a model is in the way that a driver knows what an engine is: as a thing that gets them somewhere. This is fine until something breaks, or until a decision has to be made that the documentation does not cover. At that point the gap shows. The gap is not technical sophistication; it is conceptual. It is the absence of a small set of ideas that, once internalised, make the entire field legible.

Those ideas are surprisingly few. A model is a parameterised function — a mathematical object with knobs on it, where the knobs are called weights and the function maps inputs to outputs. Training is the process of turning those knobs to make the function's outputs match what you want. The thing you are trying to minimise is called a loss, and the geometry of all possible knob-settings under that loss is called the loss surface. Optimisation is the act of walking downhill on that surface. That is essentially the entire game. Everything else in this programme — the transformer, the diffusion model, the reward model used in RLHF — is a more elaborate version of this same picture, with more knobs and a more interesting loss.

What makes this picture non-trivial is that the function you are training is not the function you actually care about. You care about how the model will behave on data it has never seen. The training data is a finite sample of some larger world, and the loss you can compute on that training data is only a proxy for the loss you would suffer in the world. This gap — between the loss you can measure and the loss you actually care about — is the source of the field's deepest pathology, which is overfitting. A model that has memorised the training data perfectly can still be useless, because memorisation is not the same as learning the structure that produced the data. This is why train/validation/test splits exist. They are not a procedural formality; they are the only mechanism you have for catching a model that is fooling you.

The bias-variance tradeoff is the formal name for the geometry of this problem. A model that is too simple will fail to capture the structure in the data and will be wrong in systematic ways — high bias. A model that is too flexible will capture not only the structure but also the noise, and will be wrong in different ways on every new sample — high variance. Every choice you make about a model's capacity, every regulariser you add, every dropout layer, every weight decay term, is an attempt to position yourself usefully on the curve between these two failure modes. Practitioners who do not have this picture experience hyperparameter tuning as a kind of superstition. Practitioners who do have it experience it as navigation.

The taxonomy that organises which problems machine learning can solve is the supervised/unsupervised distinction, and it is more consequential than it sounds. Supervised learning requires labels — pairs of inputs and the correct outputs — and labels are expensive, often prohibitively so. Unsupervised learning works on unlabelled data, which is abundant. The reason this matters for everything downstream is that the entire pre-training paradigm of modern foundation models is unsupervised (or, more precisely, self-supervised) — predicting the next token, reconstructing a masked patch, denoising an image. The pre-training revolution is, mechanically, the discovery that you can extract enormous structure from unlabelled data if you are clever about what you ask the model to predict.

The classical algorithms — linear regression, k-nearest neighbours, k-means clustering — are worth studying not because you will deploy them (you might) but because each one is simple enough to understand completely. Linear regression with a mean-squared-error loss is the simplest case of the optimisation-over-a-loss-surface idea, and its gradient is something you can compute by hand. Once you have done that, the gradient of a transformer is no longer mysterious; it is the same operation applied to a much larger graph. K-nearest neighbours is worth understanding precisely because it fails — it is the algorithm that does no learning at all, just storing every training point and looking up the nearest one at query time, and watching it break down at scale is what motivates the existence of parametric models in the first place. K-means is the cleanest possible example of the iterative optimisation pattern that recurs everywhere.

The skill this topic builds is the ability to look at any new model — however large, however fashionable — and locate it on the same conceptual map. What is the parameterised function? What is the loss? What data is it trained on, and is that data labelled? How is overfitting being controlled? What is the train/test setup, and could it be lying to you? These questions never stop being the right questions. They are the questions you ask of a logistic regression and the questions you ask of GPT-4. The vocabulary scales; the ideas do not change. If you cannot answer them confidently for a simple model, you will not be able to answer them for a complicated one — you will just have more places to hide your confusion.

## Level 2 candidates

**Supervised vs unsupervised learning** — Covers the distinction between learning from labelled input-output pairs and learning structure from unlabelled data. Worth deeper treatment because the dominance of self-supervised pre-training in modern LLMs is a direct consequence of this distinction, and the conceptual move from "labels are the signal" to "the data is its own signal" is the single most important shift to internalise before approaching transformers.

**Linear regression and loss functions** — Covers the simplest parameterised model, the MSE loss, and the gradient that minimises it. Worth deeper treatment because doing the gradient derivation by hand once is the cleanest way to demystify everything that comes later; every more complex model is a generalisation of this exact pattern.

**K-means clustering** — Covers the canonical unsupervised algorithm: iteratively assigning points to clusters and updating centroids. Worth deeper treatment because its specific failure modes (sensitivity to initialisation, the curse of dimensionality, the need to choose k) build intuition for the harder clustering and embedding problems that appear later in the curriculum.

**K-nearest neighbours classification** — Covers the non-parametric baseline of "store everything, look up the nearest neighbour at query time." Worth deeper treatment because its failure at scale is the historical motivation for parametric learning, and understanding why KNN breaks is the cleanest way to understand why neural networks were invented to replace it.

**Bias-variance tradeoff** — Covers the formal decomposition of generalisation error into systematic error and sample-dependent error. Worth deeper treatment because it is the organising principle behind every regularisation technique you will encounter, and without it, choices like dropout rate, weight decay, and model size feel arbitrary rather than navigable.

**Overfitting and the train/validation/test split** — Covers the failure mode where a model memorises rather than generalises, and the methodological apparatus built to detect it. Worth deeper treatment because the subtle ways this protocol can be violated (data leakage, validation-set overfitting through repeated tuning) are responsible for a large fraction of ML results that do not reproduce in production.
