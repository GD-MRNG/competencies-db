# L1-05 · Transformer Architecture and Attention Mechanisms

For a few years in the late 2010s, the consensus answer to "how do you model a sequence" was some flavour of recurrent network. You processed tokens one at a time, carried a hidden state forward, and hoped the gradient survived the trip. The transformer's contribution was not a clever new layer or a better optimiser. It was the observation that you could throw away recurrence entirely — process the whole sequence at once, in parallel, and let every token look directly at every other token. That sounds like a step backwards (you have lost the temporal structure that seemed to be the whole point), and it would be, except for the mechanism that replaces it. Attention is what the transformer is. Everything else in the architecture exists to support it.

The mental model worth carrying around is this: attention is a learned weighted average. Given a sequence of token representations, each token decides how much it cares about every other token, then takes a weighted sum of their information. The weights are not fixed — they are computed on the fly, from the tokens themselves, which means a token's neighbourhood is contextual rather than positional. The word "bank" attends to "river" or to "deposit" depending on what else is in the sentence. This is the entire conceptual move. Once you see attention as a soft, content-addressable lookup, the rest of the architecture stops feeling arbitrary.

The mechanics of how the weights get computed is the query-key-value formulation, and it is worth getting right. Each token produces three vectors by linear projection: a query (what am I looking for), a key (what do I offer), and a value (what do I actually contribute if attended to). To compute attention from token A to token B, you take A's query and B's key, dot them together, and that scalar is the unnormalised relevance score. Do this for every pair, softmax the scores so they sum to one, and use them as weights over the values. The output for each token is a weighted mixture of every other token's value. This is a soft dictionary lookup — keys that match the query contribute more, keys that don't contribute less — and it is fully differentiable, so the projections that produce queries, keys, and values are learned by gradient descent like everything else.

Two refinements turn this primitive into a working architecture. The first is multi-head attention. A single attention operation collapses all relationships into one weighted average, but tokens relate to each other in many ways at once — syntactically, semantically, referentially. Multi-head attention runs several attention operations in parallel with different learned projections, then concatenates the results. Each head is free to specialise; interpretability work has shown heads that track syntactic dependencies, heads that resolve coreference, heads that attend to the previous token. You do not engineer this specialisation, it falls out of training. The second refinement is positional encoding. Because attention is symmetric across sequence position — every token sees every other token equally — the raw mechanism has no notion of order at all. "Dog bites man" and "man bites dog" would be indistinguishable. Positional encodings are vectors added to (or rotated into) the token embeddings to inject sequence position. The choice between sinusoidal, learned, and rotary encodings affects how well the model extrapolates to sequences longer than it was trained on, which becomes a real problem at scale.

A transformer block is then attention plus a feedforward network, with residual connections and layer normalisation gluing them together. The feedforward network is a two-layer MLP applied independently to each position — and despite looking like the boring part of the architecture, it is where most of the parameters live. Recent interpretability work suggests the feedforward sublayers function as a kind of key-value memory, storing factual associations that attention then retrieves and combines. Stack these blocks, and you have a transformer.

The architectural fork comes from how you wire the blocks together for a given task. Encoder-only stacks (BERT) let every token attend to every other token in both directions, producing rich contextualised representations well suited to classification and extraction. Decoder-only stacks (GPT) mask attention so each token can only see tokens to its left, which makes the model autoregressive and lets it generate text one token at a time. Encoder-decoder stacks (the original transformer, T5) use an encoder to process an input sequence and a decoder that attends to both its own previous outputs and the encoder's output, which suits translation and other sequence-to-sequence tasks. The choice of fork determines what your model can do as much as the size of the model does.

Training these things at scale is its own discipline. Learning rate warmup, gradient clipping, careful initialisation, and curriculum decisions are not incidental — they are the difference between a model that converges and one that diverges in the first thousand steps. The attention mechanism is numerically delicate (those softmaxes over thousands of tokens can saturate), and the residual stream accumulates contributions from many layers in ways that need to be kept stable. None of this is interesting in isolation, but ignoring it is why most attempts to train large transformers from scratch fail.

The reason this topic is the highest-leverage investment in modern ML is that almost everything downstream is a transformer with modifications. BERT and GPT differ in their masking and training objective, but the architecture is the same. Vision transformers cut images into patches and feed them in as tokens. Diffusion models use transformers (or transformer-like cross-attention) to condition denoising on text. CLIP uses a transformer to encode text and another to encode images. If you understand attention as a learned weighted average over content-addressed values, and you understand the architectural fork between encoder-only, decoder-only, and encoder-decoder, you have the skeleton on which the rest of generative AI hangs. If you do not, every subsequent paper will feel like a sequence of unmotivated choices.

## Level 2 candidates

**Self-attention as query-key-value** — The mechanics of how a token's query is dotted against every other token's key, normalised by softmax, and used to weight the values. Worth a deep dive because the QKV formulation is the single most important piece of mathematical machinery in the curriculum, and getting it wrong (or holding it as a vague intuition) propagates confusion into every downstream topic.

**Multi-head attention** — Running several attention operations in parallel with different projections, then concatenating. Worth deeper exploration because the empirical question of what different heads actually learn — induction heads, syntactic heads, positional heads — is the entry point to mechanistic interpretability and to understanding how transformers represent structure.

**Positional encoding** — The mechanism for injecting sequence order into an inherently order-agnostic operation. Worth depth because the design choices (sinusoidal, learned, rotary, ALiBi) have real consequences for long-context behaviour and length extrapolation, and this is where many practical transformer failures actually live.

**Encoder-decoder vs encoder-only vs decoder-only** — The architectural fork that produces BERT, GPT, and T5 from the same underlying components. Worth depth because the choice between bidirectional and causal attention is a design decision you will keep making, and the tradeoffs (representation quality vs generation, fine-tuning cost vs prompting flexibility) are not obvious from the architectures alone.

**Feedforward sublayers as key-value memory** — The two-layer MLP that follows attention in each block, where the bulk of model parameters live. Worth a deep dive because the interpretability framing — that FFN layers store factual associations as key-value pairs — changes how you think about what a transformer knows and where that knowledge sits, with direct implications for editing and fine-tuning.

**Training dynamics at scale** — Warmup schedules, gradient clipping, initialisation, and the engineering decisions that determine whether a large transformer converges. Worth depth because it explains why most reproductions fail and why the published recipes look the way they do, and because debugging a stalled training run requires this vocabulary.
