# L1-09 · Diffusion Models

The strange thing about diffusion models is that they generate by destroying. The training procedure begins by taking a clean image and progressively corrupting it with Gaussian noise, step by step, until nothing remains but static. Then a neural network is taught to undo that corruption — to look at a noisy image and predict what the noise was, so it can be subtracted away. Generation is what happens when you start from pure noise and run that denoising process forward: the model hallucinates structure where there was none, one small step at a time. If this sounds backwards, that is because it is. The whole field spent years trying to generate images by learning the data distribution directly, with GANs and VAEs, before someone noticed that learning to remove noise is a more tractable problem than learning to create from nothing.

To see why this matters, it helps to remember what came before. GANs generate in a single forward pass, which is fast, but training them is a notorious knife-edge — the generator and discriminator are locked in an adversarial game that frequently collapses, with the generator learning to produce a few outputs that fool the discriminator and refusing to explore further. VAEs are stable to train but tend to produce blurry samples, because the reconstruction objective averages over uncertainty. Diffusion models sidestep both problems. There is no adversary; the training objective is a simple regression — predict the noise that was added — and the loss is well-behaved. The cost is at inference time: instead of one forward pass, you run the denoiser many times, walking the sample from noise back toward data. Stability in exchange for sampling cost is the central tradeoff of the family.

The mathematical setup has two halves. The forward process is fixed and requires no learning: it is just a Markov chain that adds a small, calibrated amount of Gaussian noise at each step, eventually arriving at something indistinguishable from pure noise. The reverse process is what the model learns. At each step, given a noisy image and the timestep it came from, the network predicts the noise component, which is enough to reconstruct an estimate of the slightly less noisy image one step earlier. Training is straightforward: sample a clean image, sample a random timestep, apply the corresponding amount of forward noise, and ask the network to predict the noise you added. The loss is mean squared error. There is no adversarial game and no variational bound to wrestle with — just a regression problem repeated across timesteps.

The neural network at the heart of all this is, almost universally, a U-Net. The U-Net's defining feature is its skip connections, which carry high-resolution spatial information from the encoder side directly across to the decoder side, bypassing the bottleneck. This matters because denoising must preserve fine spatial structure: the model needs to know where edges and textures live, not just what is roughly in the image. A standard encoder-decoder without skip connections would lose that information in the bottleneck and produce mush. The U-Net was originally invented for medical image segmentation, and its adoption for diffusion is a good example of architecture choices being driven by what the loss function actually demands.

Two extensions take diffusion from "interesting research" to "what powers Midjourney and Stable Diffusion." The first is classifier-free guidance, which is the trick that makes prompt-following actually work. During training, the model is occasionally shown its conditioning signal (a text prompt, say) and occasionally has it dropped out, so it learns both conditional and unconditional generation in the same weights. At inference, you can extrapolate between the two — pushing the sample further in the direction of the prompt than the conditional model alone would suggest. This single technique is what gives image generators the dial that controls how literally they follow your instructions. The second extension is latent diffusion: instead of running the noisy Markov chain in pixel space, you first compress images into a much smaller latent space using a VAE, run diffusion there, and then decode back to pixels at the end. The compression makes high-resolution generation computationally feasible. Stable Diffusion is, in essence, a latent diffusion model with a CLIP text encoder feeding cross-attention layers in the U-Net.

The remaining practical lever is sampling. The original DDPM formulation requires hundreds or even thousands of denoising steps to produce a clean image, because the math assumes small steps. DDIM and its descendants reformulate the reverse process so that you can take much larger steps deterministically, often producing comparable quality in twenty or thirty iterations instead of a thousand. This is why image generation has gone from minutes per image to seconds. The tradeoff is subtle — fewer steps can reduce sample diversity and occasionally produce artifacts — but for production systems it is almost always worth taking.

What you should walk away with is a concrete pipeline rather than a vague impression. A prompt is encoded into a vector by a text encoder. A random noise tensor in latent space is initialised. A U-Net, conditioned on the text vector and the current timestep, predicts noise; that noise is subtracted in a controlled way; the result is fed back in; this repeats for some number of steps. A VAE decoder turns the final latent into pixels. Every modern text-to-image system you have used is some variation on this loop. Once you can trace data through it once, the literature stops feeling like a pile of unrelated tricks and starts looking like sensible engineering on top of one good idea: that learning to undo noise is easier than learning to create from nothing.

## Level 2 candidates

**Forward and reverse diffusion processes** — The mathematical formulation of the fixed forward Markov chain and the learned reverse process, including why predicting noise (as opposed to predicting the denoised image directly) is the parameterisation that actually works. Worth going deeper because the math here is what licenses the simple regression loss, and without it the training procedure feels like a coincidence rather than a derivation.

**U-Net architecture for denoising** — The specific design of the encoder-decoder with skip connections, and why this network rather than a pure transformer or a plain CNN became the default backbone. Worth deeper attention because the architectural choice is tightly coupled to what denoising demands of a network, and the same logic explains why some recent diffusion transformers (DiT) had to make specific compensating moves.

**Classifier-free guidance** — The training-time dropout of conditioning combined with inference-time extrapolation between conditional and unconditional predictions. Worth a deep dive because it is the single mechanism behind every "guidance scale" or "CFG" slider in image generation tools, and understanding it explains both why higher guidance values produce more literal, often oversaturated outputs and why too-high values destroy the image.

**Latent diffusion (LDM)** — Running the diffusion process in the compressed latent space of a pretrained VAE rather than in raw pixels. Worth going deeper because this is the architectural move that made high-resolution open-weights generation feasible; understanding the VAE-then-diffusion pipeline is the prerequisite for reading the Stable Diffusion paper or modifying it.

**Sampling methods (DDPM vs DDIM and beyond)** — The reformulations of the reverse process that allow deterministic, large-step sampling and the newer solvers (DPM-Solver, Euler) that push step counts even lower. Worth deeper study because sampler choice is one of the few user-facing knobs that materially changes generation quality and speed, and the field is still actively producing better solvers.

**Conditioning mechanisms and cross-attention** — How a text embedding actually enters the U-Net (cross-attention layers between image features and text tokens) and how other modalities (depth maps, poses, reference images via ControlNet) plug into the same scaffold. Worth a separate treatment because conditioning is where diffusion meets the rest of the multimodal stack, and most practical control over generation lives in this interface.
