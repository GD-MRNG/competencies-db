# Level 2 Prompt

Generate a Level 2 depth post for a competency system. A Level 2 post goes one layer beneath the Level 1 mental model and explains how the chosen sub-concept actually works — its mechanics, its real tradeoffs, and where it breaks. It is not a tutorial. It is a conceptual deep dive that gives the reader the durable mental model they will need before they could build anything meaningful around this sub-concept.

The Level 3 handoff is generated by a separate prompt and appended afterward. Your output ends at Key Takeaways.

## Inputs

### Course context (Level 0)

```
{level_0_summary}
```

### Parent Level 1 post

```
{level_1_post}
```

### The Level 2 sub-concept

```
{level_2_candidate}
```

This is a single entry taken from the parent post's `## Level 2 candidates` section. It names the sub-concept, summarises what it covers, and explains why it warrants going deeper. The post you write is about exactly this sub-concept — not the parent topic, not its sibling sub-concepts.

## Output

A markdown document, ending at Key Takeaways.

### Format

- A single H1 heading with the sub-concept title. Preserve hierarchical numbering if the parent uses it (if the parent L1 is "1.1 Networking Fundamentals", an L2 underneath is something like "1.1.3 DNS: The Resolution Chain").
- An opening paragraph with no heading — the bridge from Level 1.
- 3–7 mechanics subsections under H2 descriptive headings of your own choosing (e.g. "How a TLS Handshake Establishes Trust", not "The Core Mechanics"). Each subsection is flowing prose.
- A `## Tradeoffs and Failure Modes` section near the end, containing 2–4 nested H3 subsections, each named after a specific tension (e.g. "The TTL Tension", "Parallelism vs. Resource Cost", "Right-Sizing Into a Latency Cliff").
- A `## The Mental Model` section with one to two closing paragraphs that distil the durable conceptual shift.
- A `## Key Takeaways` section with 5–8 bullet points.
- 2,000–2,500 words across the whole post.

### Voice

- Direct, precise, practitioner-oriented. Second person ("you").
- No throat-clearing, no transitional summaries that repeat the previous paragraph, no congratulating the reader at the end. If a sentence is not doing work, cut it.
- Honest about complexity. Do not flatten tradeoffs. If the right answer depends on context, say what it depends on.
- Use exact terminology because precision is useful, not to signal expertise. Define terms only when they are doing real work in the explanation.
- Stay within the territory of the chosen sub-concept. Do not drift into sibling sub-concepts from the parent's handoff list.
- Code blocks, command snippets, and short config examples are allowed where they illustrate a mechanic concretely (e.g. a Kubernetes resource spec, a DAG-structured pipeline definition). Keep them short and use them only when prose alone would obscure.
- No bolded key terms inside prose paragraphs.

### Opening pattern

Open with the gap between common understanding and reality. The pattern is: name the misconception in concrete terms, then name the specific failure mode it produces. Examples from existing Level 2 posts:

> Most engineers think of DNS as a single lookup: you ask for api.example.com and you get back 93.184.216.34. A black box with a domain going in and an IP coming out. This mental model works fine right up until you're staring at an incident where half your users can reach your new service and the other half are still hitting the old IP...

> Most engineers understand that right-sizing means "don't pay for resources you're not using." That framing is correct but shallow, and it leads to a specific failure: someone looks at a utilization graph, sees 15% average CPU, halves the instance size, and causes a latency regression or an outage.

> Most engineers can describe what a CI pipeline does: it runs when you push code, it builds and tests your changes, and it tells you if something is broken. What most engineers have not internalized is that a pipeline is a feedback system with economic properties...

Match this opening shape: surface understanding, then the gap, then the consequence.

### Content arc

Across your subsections, cover these in order:

1. **The bridge.** Open without a heading. One paragraph that names the gap between Level 1 understanding and the underlying reality, and the consequence of operating without that deeper view. Do not recap Level 1; assume the reader has read it.
2. **The mechanics.** Multiple H2 subsections walking through how the sub-concept actually works — the model, the process, the moving parts, the lifecycle. Use concrete sequences where the process has meaningful steps. Use analogies where they earn their place. Go as deep as the sub-concept requires; completeness over brevity.
3. **Tradeoffs and Failure Modes.** A dedicated H2 section with 2–4 nested H3 subsections, each named after a specific tension. This is what separates a depth post from a glossary entry. Be concrete about what fails, what it costs, and what behaviour confuses people who only have Level 1 understanding.
4. **The Mental Model.** An H2 section with one to two paragraphs that distil the durable conceptual shift the post produces. Not a summary. The one thing the reader can now reason from that they could not reason from before.
5. **Key Takeaways.** An H2 section with 5–8 bullet points. Each bullet is a complete, standalone idea — a sentence the reader could copy into their notes and have it mean something on its own, not a fragment, not a vague reminder. This is the only place in the post where bullets are used.

### Adaptation

Do not assume the sub-concept is technical. If the source material is non-technical, adapt the same arc — bridge, mechanics, tradeoffs and failure modes, mental model, key takeaways — to the actual subject. The "Most engineers think..." opening generalises to whatever audience the course is for.

## Rules

- Do not invent content not present in or directly inferable from the inputs. The Level 1 post sets the conceptual ceiling; do not contradict it.
- Do not repeat the Level 1 post. Build on it.
- Stay within the chosen sub-concept. Sibling sub-concepts belong in their own Level 2 calls.
- Output only the document, ending at Key Takeaways. The Level 3 handoff is generated by a separate prompt.
- No preamble, no commentary.
