# L1-01 · The Company and You Don't Have the Same Interests

The hardest thing to see clearly about your job is also the most basic thing about it: the company you work for does not want what you want. Not in some adversarial, villainous sense — just structurally, mechanically, in the way the arrangement is set up. You want to be paid as much as possible for work you find tolerable. The company wants to pay you as little as possible for as much output as it can get. That gap is not a flaw in the relationship. That gap is the relationship. Everything else — the lanyard, the offsite, the values poster in the lobby — sits on top of it.

This is the load-bearing idea for everything else you'll learn about how workplaces actually function, and almost all corporate communication is designed to keep you from seeing it cleanly. You will be told, in dozens of small ways every week, that you and the company are on the same team, pulling in the same direction, sharing the same mission. Some of the people saying this believe it. Some are obligated to say it. None of them are wrong about the tone — most workplaces really are pleasant, most colleagues really are decent, most managers really do care. But tone and structure are different things, and confusing the two is what gets people hurt.

The structural reality is straightforward once you stop looking away from it. A profitable company, by definition, takes in more value from its workers' labour than it pays out in wages. If it didn't, there would be no profit and the company wouldn't exist. This is not a conspiracy or a moral failing — it's the mechanism. What this means in practice is that every conversation about your compensation, your hours, your workload, and your effort is happening across a structural gap. When you ask for more, the company has a built-in reason to say no. When the company asks for more from you, it has a built-in reason to ask. The pressure is not symmetrical, and it never will be, because the arrangement was not designed to be symmetrical.

This is why so much workplace language sounds slightly off when you listen to it carefully. Phrases like "we're all in this together," "we're a family here," "we win as a team" do real work — they're trying to paper over a gap that would otherwise be visible. If your interests and the company's interests genuinely aligned, no one would need to keep saying so. The repetition is the tell. The same goes for "alignment" itself: when a company says it wants everyone aligned, it does not mean it wants to find the overlap between your goals and its goals. It means it wants you moving in the direction that serves its goals. Whose goals are centred is the whole question, and the language is carefully built to keep you from asking it.

None of this means your job is bad, or that your colleagues are enemies, or that you should walk around suspicious of every kind word. It means something more useful and more specific: when you hear workplace communication, you should know which side of the gap it's coming from. A manager telling you they appreciate your work might genuinely appreciate it — and also be transmitting an incentive structure that wants you to keep producing at this level without expecting more in return. Both things can be true. The skill is holding both at once instead of collapsing into either cynicism ("they're lying to me") or naivety ("we're all on the same team"). Neither of those is accurate. The structural gap is.

Once you can see this clearly, a category of confusing experiences starts to make sense. Why does working harder not produce proportional reward? Because the system is not designed to return value to you in proportion to what you put in — it's designed to retain as much of that value as possible. Why does loyalty so often go unrewarded? Because the relationship is not actually a loyalty relationship; it's an exchange that one side has more leverage in. Why does "be grateful you have a job" land so heavily, even when you're doing excellent work? Because your dependence on the job is itself a tool — the more you need the work, the less the company has to offer to keep you. None of this is personal. All of it is structural. And all of it becomes legible the moment you stop trying to read it through the lens of shared interest.

The practical skill this builds is a kind of double vision. You learn to participate normally in your workplace — be friendly, do good work, build real relationships — while also reading the structural layer underneath. You stop being surprised when the company acts in its own interest, because of course it does; that's what it's built to do. You stop feeling personally betrayed by policies that are simply the system functioning as designed. And you start to make decisions — about how much of yourself to give, when to push, when to leave — from a clearer place. Not from anger, not from grievance, but from an accurate read of what kind of relationship you're actually in.

That clarity is the whole point. You can be a good colleague, do meaningful work, even love your job — and still know exactly where you stand. The two are not in conflict. What's in conflict is the story the company tells about itself and the structure it actually runs on. Once you can tell those apart, the rest of this course is just specific applications of the same skill.

## Level 2 candidates

**Why "we're all in this together" does so much work** — A close read of the most common alignment phrases and what specifically they're trying to get you to stop noticing. Worth deeper treatment because the language is so ubiquitous and the mechanism by which it suppresses normal questions about pay, hours, and risk is subtle enough that most people never name it.

**Your dependence as a management tool** — How the simple fact that most people need their job to survive is not background condition but active leverage, used to keep wages down and pushback muted. This deserves its own treatment because it reframes "be grateful" messaging from sentiment into mechanism, and changes how you hear a whole category of workplace talk.

**The gap between your good performance and your reward** — Why effort and output don't translate into proportional compensation, and what actually determines how much of the value you create comes back to you. Worth going deeper on because almost everyone has felt this gap personally and most people interpret it as a failure of their employer rather than as the system working as intended.

**What "alignment" really means** — The specific corporate use of "alignment" and how it quietly assumes whose direction is the right one. A short but high-leverage deep dive, because the word appears in nearly every strategic communication and learning to hear it correctly changes what you take from those communications.

**The structural gap vs. the personal relationship** — How to hold the structural reality in one hand and a genuine, decent relationship with colleagues and managers in the other, without collapsing into either cynicism or naivety. This is worth its own treatment because most people who grasp the structural point overcorrect into a posture that's both unpleasant and inaccurate, and the balance is a skill in itself.
