# L1-07 · Databases and State Persistence

The model call at the centre of your system is stateless. It takes tokens in and gives tokens out, and the moment the response finishes streaming, the provider has forgotten you exist. Your application cannot afford that amnesia. Users expect their conversation from yesterday to still be there. Your billing system needs to know how many tokens that user consumed last month. Your RAG pipeline needs to find the right three paragraphs out of a million embedded documents in under fifty milliseconds. None of that lives in the model. All of it lives in the persistence layer you build around the model, and the shape of that layer determines whether your system feels fast and reliable or slow and broken.

The mistake most AI engineers make is treating storage as a single decision — "we'll use Postgres" or "we'll use Pinecone" — when in reality a production AI system almost always uses three or four different storage technologies at once, each handling the kind of data it's actually designed for. The cost of getting this wrong is not theoretical. Store embeddings in Postgres without the right extension and your similarity search will scan the entire table on every query. Store conversation history in a vector database and you'll pay a premium for queries that a fifty-dollar relational instance would answer in milliseconds. Treat a queue like a database, or a database like a queue, and you will rediscover, painfully, why those abstractions exist as separate things.

Start with the relational database, because almost every system needs one. Postgres (or its managed cousins like Aurora) is where structured, transactional data lives: users, accounts, conversations, messages, jobs, billing records, anything where the relationships between rows matter and where you need guarantees that two operations either both succeed or both fail. Relational databases give you schemas, foreign keys, indexes, and ACID transactions. These are not glamorous features, but they are the features that prevent your "users" table from drifting out of sync with your "subscriptions" table and silently overbilling customers. If you are starting a new AI product and you don't know what storage you need, the answer is Postgres. You can grow into the others.

Vector databases are the genuinely new piece in the AI stack, and they exist because relational databases were not built for the question "find the hundred rows whose 1536-dimensional embedding is closest to this query vector by cosine similarity." That operation, called approximate nearest neighbour search, is the engine of every RAG system, every semantic search feature, every "find similar documents" button. Specialised stores (Pinecone, Weaviate, Chroma, Qdrant, and the pgvector extension that bolts the capability onto Postgres itself) implement indexing structures like HNSW that make this search fast at scale. The tradeoff is that vector databases are narrower than relational databases — they're optimised for one query pattern, and they generally cost more per gigabyte. Use them for embeddings. Do not use them as a general-purpose store.

Queues are the third leg, and they are not storage in the same sense — they are storage for work. When a user uploads a document and asks your agent to analyse it, you do not want your web server to hold that HTTP connection open for the ninety seconds the analysis takes. You want the web server to write a job onto a queue (SQS, RabbitMQ, Redis-backed queues like Celery or BullMQ) and return immediately with a job ID. A worker process, running on different infrastructure entirely, picks up the job, does the work, and writes the result back to your relational database. The frontend polls or subscribes for completion. Without a queue, every slow operation becomes a server-blocking operation, and your system falls over the first time three users hit it simultaneously. With a queue, you can scale workers independently of web traffic and survive bursts gracefully.

The other stores you will eventually need fit into the gaps these three leave. A cache, almost always Redis, sits in front of expensive queries and LLM calls to serve repeated requests in microseconds instead of seconds; if a hundred users ask the same question in an hour, you should be paying the model once. Object storage (S3 and its equivalents) handles the things that don't belong in a database at all — uploaded PDFs, generated images, log archives, model artefacts. Document stores like DynamoDB or MongoDB show up when you have semi-structured data with flexible schemas and access patterns dominated by lookups on a single key. Each of these has a clear job. The skill is recognising which job you actually have.

The reason this matters for AI systems specifically, more than for traditional web apps, is that AI workloads stress storage in unusual ways. Embeddings are large and numerous. Conversation histories grow without bound and need to be truncated, summarised, or paged. Token usage needs to be attributed per-user, per-feature, per-model for cost control. Traces from agent runs are voluminous and need to be retained long enough for debugging but not so long that storage costs eclipse model costs. Every one of these problems has a wrong answer that looks reasonable on day one and becomes catastrophic by day ninety.

The practical takeaway is this: before you write a line of application code, sketch what data your system will produce, how often it will be read, how often it will be written, and what query patterns it needs to support. Then map each kind of data to the storage technology that actually fits it. A typical production AI system ends up with Postgres for application state, pgvector or a dedicated vector DB for embeddings, Redis for caching and rate limiting, S3 for large objects, and a queue for asynchronous work. That is not over-engineering — it is the minimum viable persistence layer for a system that doesn't fall apart under real users. The model is one API call. Everything else is data, and data is where production systems live or die.

## Level 2 candidates

**Relational Databases and Schemas** — Designing tables for users, conversations, messages, and jobs, with proper foreign keys, indexes, and migration practices. Worth a deep dive because schema decisions made in week one constrain you for years, and AI-specific patterns (storing message arrays, tracking token usage, versioning prompts) have non-obvious tradeoffs.

**Vector Databases and Embeddings at Scale** — How approximate nearest neighbour indexes (HNSW, IVF) actually work, the pgvector vs. dedicated-vector-DB decision, and how cost and recall scale with corpus size. Worth deeper treatment because this is the most AI-specific storage decision and the one most likely to be miscosted at planning time.

**Caching Strategies and Redis** — Cache-aside vs. write-through patterns, TTL design, cache invalidation, and the specific case of caching LLM responses (semantic caching, prompt hashing). Worth its own post because LLM cost optimisation often lives or dies on cache hit rate, and naive caching produces stale or wrong answers.

**Job Queues and Asynchronous Work** — Queue semantics (FIFO vs. priority, at-least-once vs. exactly-once, dead-letter queues), worker pool sizing, and how to design idempotent job handlers. Deserves depth because this is also the foundation of L1-14 (distributed execution) and the patterns generalise across SQS, Celery, BullMQ, and Temporal.

**Data Retention, Privacy, and Compliance** — Deciding what to store, for how long, with what encryption, and how to honour deletion requests under GDPR and similar regimes. Worth its own treatment because AI systems generate enormous volumes of trace and conversation data, and the default of "keep everything forever" is both expensive and legally dangerous.

**NoSQL and Document Stores** — When DynamoDB or MongoDB is genuinely the right choice over Postgres, and the access-pattern thinking required to design them well. Worth a deeper look because the decision is frequently made on vibes ("it's more flexible") rather than on access patterns, and the migration cost if you choose wrong is severe.
