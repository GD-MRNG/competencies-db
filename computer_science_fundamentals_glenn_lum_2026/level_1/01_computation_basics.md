# Computation Basics

Most working programmers have never asked what computation actually is. You write code, the machine runs it, the tests pass — the question feels academic, the kind of thing that belongs in a philosophy seminar rather than a sprint. But the question is not academic. It was answered, definitively, in 1936, before any computer existed, and that answer is the reason your laptop and a supercomputer and the chip in your microwave are, in a precise mathematical sense, the same kind of machine. Everything you have ever built sits inside the boundary that answer drew. Knowing where the boundary is — and how it was drawn — changes how you read the rest of the field.

The question Alan Turing was trying to answer was not about machines at all. It was about mathematics. David Hilbert had asked, in essence, whether every mathematical truth could be discovered by a mechanical procedure — whether mathematics could, in principle, be automated. To answer this, Turing needed a precise definition of "mechanical procedure," and the definitions on offer were vague. So he invented one. He imagined an idealised device with an infinite tape, a head that could read and write symbols on it, and a small set of rules for what to do based on what symbol it saw and what state it was in. That was it. No silicon, no electricity, no language. Just symbols, rules, and a tape. He then proved that this absurdly simple device could compute anything that any well-defined mechanical procedure could compute. And he proved, devastatingly, that there were well-defined questions it could never answer.

The first half of that result is the more important one for you. The Turing machine is not a real machine; it is a definition. When Turing showed that his model could compute anything mechanically computable, he was not describing a piece of hardware — he was drawing a circle around a concept. The concept is computability, and the circle is the outer wall of the entire field. Every programming language, every CPU architecture, every cloud platform sits inside that circle. They differ in speed, in ergonomics, in safety, in how much they cost to run. They do not differ in what they can compute. This claim, that any reasonable model of computation is equivalent to a Turing machine, is the Church-Turing thesis, and it is why a question like "is this language Turing-complete?" actually means something — it is asking whether the language touches the wall of the circle, or stops short of it.

The second half of Turing's result is where things get strange. He proved that some problems cannot be solved by any mechanical procedure at all. The most famous is the halting problem: there is no algorithm that, given an arbitrary program and input, will correctly decide whether that program eventually finishes or runs forever. Notice the structure of this claim. It is not that we have not yet found such an algorithm. It is not that we lack the computing power. It is that no such algorithm can exist, ever, on any machine, by any means. This is a hard mathematical result, like the irrationality of the square root of two. The boundary of computation has an inside and an outside, and there are real, well-defined problems that live on the wrong side.

Sitting one layer beneath this is the older idea that made it possible: the algorithm itself. An algorithm is a finite, precise procedure for solving a problem — finite because it must terminate, precise because each step must be unambiguous. The word comes from al-Khwarizmi, a ninth-century Persian mathematician, and the concept is older still; Euclid's procedure for finding the greatest common divisor is an algorithm written down more than two thousand years before the first computer. This matters because it separates the idea of a solution from the idea of a machine. An algorithm is a solution to a problem. A program is an algorithm encoded for a particular machine. Most working developers spend their careers writing programs without ever clearly distinguishing the two, which is why so much code works without anyone being able to say exactly what problem it is solving.

The other half of the bridge from mathematics to machine is encoding. A Turing machine manipulates symbols on a tape, and your computer manipulates voltages in transistors, and these are connected by a single insight: anything you want to compute over — text, images, sound, video, a 3D model of a protein — can be reduced to numbers, and numbers can be represented in binary. Claude Shannon's 1948 information theory paper is where this got its rigorous treatment, but the practical consequence is what you already half-know: at the bottom of every abstraction in software is a sequence of bits, and the choice of how to encode something into bits is itself a design decision with real consequences. Floating-point numbers have rounding errors. Unicode has historical scars from the era when everyone assumed English. Every encoding is a tradeoff between expressiveness and size, and the tradeoffs propagate upward through every layer above.

What you take from this layer is not a skill you will use on Monday morning. It is a frame. When someone tells you that a new architecture or a new language has fundamentally changed what computers can do, you will know to be skeptical, because the wall of the circle has not moved since 1936. When you encounter a problem that resists every elegant solution you try, you will know that some problems are genuinely unsolvable, and the honest move is sometimes to redefine the problem rather than keep searching. And when you read about Turing-completeness, decidability, or the halting problem in the wild — in language design, in type systems, in security proofs — you will recognise them as recurring shadows of the same 1936 result, casting across the entire field.

## Level 2 candidates

**What is an algorithm** — The precise notion of a finite, unambiguous procedure for solving a well-defined problem, traced from Euclid through al-Khwarizmi to the modern definition. Worth deeper treatment because the distinction between an algorithm and a program — between a solution and its encoding for a machine — is the conceptual move that separates principled engineering from code that merely happens to work.

**The Turing Machine** — The formal model itself: tape, head, states, transitions, and what it means for such a machine to compute a function. Worth depth because seeing the construction in detail is the difference between knowing the term and understanding why every real computer is, structurally, an instance of it.

**The Church-Turing Thesis** — The claim that all reasonable models of computation are equivalent in power, including lambda calculus, recursive functions, and Turing machines. Worth going deeper into because the thesis is not a theorem — it is a conjecture about the nature of computation itself, and the reasons it is believed are more interesting than the statement.

**Decidability and the halting problem** — The proof that some problems have no algorithmic solution at all, and the technique (diagonalisation) that establishes it. Worth a deep dive because the proof is one of the most elegant in mathematics and the technique recurs throughout complexity theory and logic.

**Binary and information encoding** — How all data reduces to bits, and the design choices in specific encodings (two's complement, IEEE 754, UTF-8). Worth depth because the practical consequences of encoding choices — precision loss, locale bugs, size-versus-speed tradeoffs — appear constantly in real systems and are usually misdiagnosed as something else.
