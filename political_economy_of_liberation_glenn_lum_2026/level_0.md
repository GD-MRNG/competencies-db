# Political Economy of Liberation — Level 0: Course Map

> **Intent:** To understand not just what the system does to you, but how people have historically named it, organised against it, and attempted to replace or reform it — so that your understanding of your own life connects to a tradition of collective response rather than ending in private clarity.
> **Your angle:** You already feel the system. You've started naming it. This map is for moving from recognition to orientation — knowing where the ideas came from, how they connect, and where the live debates are so you can find your own position within a real tradition rather than reinventing it alone.

---

## How to use this map

Level 1 is orientation — read every entry once to get the full landscape. Level 2 is where you descend when something feels urgent, personally resonant, or like a gap in your understanding. Don't complete it sequentially. Use the sequencing note at the bottom to find your entry point, then let your own questions pull you deeper. The map is navigation infrastructure, not a syllabus.

## Topic Inventory

### GROUP 1 — The Diagnosis: What the System Does to People

#### L1-01 · Alienation

The foundational wound. Marx identified in the 1844 manuscripts that capitalism doesn't just exploit workers economically — it severs them from the product of their labour, the act of making, other people, and their own human nature. This is not a metaphor for feeling bad at work. It is a structural condition produced by wage labour itself, which means it cannot be solved individually. Understanding alienation is the entry point because it transforms the experience of modern life from personal failure into legible systemic output.

**Level 2 candidates:**
- **The four estrangements** — mapping all four separations reveals that they are one process viewed from different angles, and that fixing one without the others leaves the root untouched.
- **Species-nature** — Marx's claim that humans are uniquely conscious, creative, and cooperative beings; understanding this is what gives alienation its moral weight and explains why wage labour feels like more than just inconvenience.
- **The self-concealment of alienation** — the mechanism by which structural suffering presents as personal inadequacy; this is the most practically urgent concept for daily life.
- **Alienation beyond the workplace** — tracing how the logic extends into relationships, culture, and consumption reveals that there is no private refuge from it, only cracks and counter-practices.
- **Reification** — Lukács' extension of Marx: when human relationships take on the character of things, and things appear to have agency; this explains why the market feels like a force of nature rather than a human construction.

---

#### L1-02 · Class and the Structure of Exploitation

Alienation describes the experience. Class analysis describes the structure that produces it. A class is not an income bracket or a cultural identity — it is a relationship to the means of production. Those who own the means of production extract surplus value from those who do not. Understanding this precisely cuts through the ideology of meritocracy and mobility and shows that the structure reproduces itself regardless of who occupies which position within it.

**Level 2 candidates:**
- **Surplus value** — Marx's account of where profit actually comes from; understanding it shows that exploitation is not an accident or a corruption of capitalism but its operating mechanism.
- **The means of production** — what it actually means to own or not own them in a modern economy, beyond the factory floor image.
- **Class fractions and the middle** — understanding the professional-managerial class, the petit bourgeoisie, and the labour aristocracy explains why class solidarity is structurally complicated and why the middle often sides with capital under pressure.
- **Reproduction of class** — Bourdieu's work on how class reproduces itself through culture, education, and taste rather than just inheritance; this is where class analysis meets lived everyday experience.
- **The reserve army of labour** — Marx's concept of structural unemployment as a disciplinary mechanism that keeps wages down and workers compliant; understanding it reframes unemployment as a feature, not a bug.

---

#### L1-03 · Ideology and Hegemony

The system cannot run on force alone. Gramsci, writing from prison in the 1930s, asked why workers consent to a system that exploits them — and answered that the ruling class controls not just the economy and the state but the common sense of the society. What feels natural, inevitable, and just is itself a product of power. This is the most immediately practical topic in the whole map because identifying ideology in real time — in news, in conversation, in your own assumptions — is a skill that changes how you move through the world.

**Level 2 candidates:**
- **Hegemony** — the mechanism by which dominant ideas come to feel like universal common sense rather than the interests of a particular class; understanding it explains why working people often defend the system that harms them.
- **The war of position** — Gramsci's concept of the long cultural and institutional struggle for ideological ground that must precede or accompany political struggle; this is why working class culture, media, and education are politically contested terrain.
- **Naturalisation** — the rhetorical move that converts historically specific arrangements into apparent facts of nature; identifying it in everyday language is immediately useful.
- **Capitalist realism** — Mark Fisher's 2009 diagnosis of the specific modern ideological condition: not "capitalism is good" but "there is no alternative"; enforced not by belief but by imaginative closure.
- **Meritocracy** — tracing how the belief in earned outcomes functions to redirect structural critique into self-blame; it is the ideology most responsible for converting alienation into depression.

### GROUP 2 — The State: Tool, Terrain, or Enemy

#### L1-04 · Theories of the State

The most consequential strategic question in the whole tradition is what the state actually is — because your answer determines what you think is possible. Is it a neutral instrument that workers can capture and use? Is it structurally embedded in capital's interests? Is it a terrain of struggle where gains are possible but never permanent? Or is it itself a form of domination that must be abolished rather than seized? Every major split in working class politics — reformist vs revolutionary, Marxist vs anarchist — runs through this question.

**Level 2 candidates:**
- **The liberal state** — the idea that the state is a neutral arbiter between interests; understanding why Marxists reject this reveals the foundation of the critique of parliamentary reformism.
- **The state as instrument of class rule** — the classical Marxist position that state institutions (law, police, courts, military) are structurally organised around the protection of property relations, not neutral governance.
- **Lenin's State and Revolution** — the argument that the bourgeois state cannot be taken over and used by workers but must be smashed and replaced; this is the theoretical basis of vanguard Leninism and its most contested claim.
- **The relative autonomy of the state** — Poulantzas' revision arguing the state has its own logic and can be a genuine site of struggle, not just a pure instrument of capital; this grounds the case for reformist strategy on the left.
- **The anarchist abolition position** — the argument that seizing state power merely reproduces domination under new management; understanding it alongside the Marxist position reveals the genuine strategic dilemma rather than a simple answer.

---

#### L1-05 · Reform and Its Limits

The reformist tradition has delivered the most tangible material improvements in working class life — the weekend, public healthcare, child labour laws, the minimum wage, union rights. This cannot be dismissed. But Rosa Luxemburg's 1900 critique remains unresolved: reforms won within the system tend to stabilise capitalism, extend its life, and leave the ownership of the means of production intact. Understanding both the achievements and the structural ceiling of reform is essential for any honest strategic thinking.

**Level 2 candidates:**
- **Social democracy** — the tradition of using electoral politics and the state to regulate capital and redistribute wealth; understanding its post-war peak and subsequent collapse under neoliberalism shows both its power and its fragility.
- **Luxemburg's Reform or Revolution** — the foundational left critique of gradualism; her argument that reform treats symptoms while leaving the disease is still the sharpest statement of the revolutionary objection.
- **The Keynesian settlement** — how the post-war compromise between capital and labour was constructed, what it achieved, and why it was dismantled from the 1970s onwards; understanding this period explains what was lost and why.
- **Co-optation** — the mechanism by which radical demands are absorbed, defanged, and incorporated into the system; tracing it historically is a warning about the limits of winning within existing institutions.
- **The horizon problem** — the structural tendency of reformist parties to moderate once in power because the state and capital impose real constraints on what is possible within the system; understanding this is not cynicism but strategic realism.

### GROUP 3 — The Revolutionary Tradition: Forms and Debates

#### L1-06 · Revolutionary Theory and Its Splits

The revolutionary tradition is not a single position — it is a set of live and unresolved debates about who the revolutionary subject is, what form organisation should take, and what the goal actually looks like. The three major branches — Leninist vanguardism, council communism, and anarchism — have genuinely different analyses of power, organisation, and human nature. Understanding the splits is more useful than picking a side prematurely, because each tradition identified real problems that the others failed to solve.

**Level 2 candidates:**
- **Vanguardism** — Lenin's argument that the working class alone cannot develop revolutionary consciousness and requires a disciplined party of professional revolutionaries to lead; this is the most influential and most contested idea in the tradition.
- **Workers' councils and soviets** — the historical emergence of self-organised workplace and community councils as an alternative to both parliamentary politics and party leadership; the Russian soviets of 1905 and 1917, the German councils of 1918.
- **Anarchism's critique of power** — the argument that the seizure of state power by any group, even a revolutionary one, reproduces domination; Bakunin's prescient prediction of what Marxist state power would look like in practice.
- **The party form** — the debate about whether revolutionary organisation should be a disciplined vanguard party, a mass party, a network, or something else; this is not an abstract question — it determines what movements actually do.
- **Rosa Luxemburg's spontaneism** — her argument against Leninist discipline: that revolutionary consciousness emerges from the mass strike and direct experience of struggle, not from party tutelage; this is the bridge between revolutionary and libertarian socialism.

---

#### L1-07 · Prefigurative Politics

One of the most important concepts in the tradition and one of the least discussed in mainstream politics. Prefigurative politics is the idea — developed most strongly in the anarchist and libertarian socialist traditions — that you must build the new world in the shell of the old, right now, rather than waiting to win power and then construct the alternative. The organisations and relationships of the movement must themselves embody the values of the world you are trying to create. This directly connects alienation theory to organising practice: the remedy is not deferred to after the revolution — it is practised in the building of counter-institutions today.

**Level 2 candidates:**
- **The commune form** — from the Paris Commune of 1871 to the Zapatistas to contemporary housing communes; understanding these as practical experiments in post-capitalist organisation rather than utopian fantasies.
- **Mutual aid** — Kropotkin's argument that cooperation is as fundamental to human nature as competition; tracing its history as working class practice shows it is not idealistic but has sustained communities through crisis.
- **The cooperative economy** — worker-owned enterprises as a prefigurative economic form; understanding Mondragon in the Basque Country as the largest working example shows the model is viable at scale.
- **The relationship between means and ends** — the political argument that authoritarian means produce authoritarian ends regardless of intention; this is the deepest critique of Leninism and the foundation of the prefigurative position.
- **Dual power** — the strategy of building alternative institutions that meet real needs (housing, food, healthcare, education) alongside and in tension with existing state institutions, gradually shifting where people turn for what they need.

### GROUP 4 — Expansions: What the Classical Tradition Missed

#### L1-08 · Colonialism, Race, and the World System

The classical Marxist tradition was developed primarily by European thinkers analysing European capitalism, and it has significant blind spots. The wealth that funded European industrialisation was extracted from colonised peoples through slavery, land theft, and resource extraction. Race is not simply a product of class — it has its own logic, its own history, and its own forms of resistance. The Black radical tradition (W.E.B. Du Bois, C.L.R. James, Frantz Fanon, Angela Davis) and world-systems theory (Wallerstein) expand the frame from the national to the global and from the economic to the racial.

**Level 2 candidates:**
- **Primitive accumulation and colonialism** — extending Marx's analysis of how capitalism began to include the colonial extraction that funded European industrialisation; this reframes the global as the actual unit of analysis.
- **The Black radical tradition** — Du Bois, James, Fanon, and Davis developing a Marxism that takes race seriously as an independent axis of domination with its own history and its own forms of consciousness and resistance.
- **The wages of whiteness** — Du Bois' argument that white workers have historically been offered a psychological wage of racial status that divides them from Black workers and serves capital; this is the sharpest analysis of why working class solidarity fails along racial lines.
- **World-systems theory** — Wallerstein's argument that capitalism must be analysed as a global system with core and periphery nations, not as a set of national economies; this reframes what development and underdevelopment actually mean.
- **Decolonial theory** — the argument that political and economic independence from colonial power is insufficient without also decolonising knowledge, culture, and subjectivity.

---

#### L1-09 · Gender, Social Reproduction, and Feminism

Social reproduction theory is the most important expansion of Marxist analysis in the last fifty years. The question it asks: who produces the worker? The answer is: unwaged, mostly female labour — childrearing, cooking, cleaning, emotional care. This labour is invisible in classical economics and underweighted in classical Marxism, but without it the working class cannot reproduce itself. Silvia Federici's *Caliban and the Witch* shows that capitalism was founded on the violent suppression of women's communal power. Understanding this makes the nuclear family critique, the atomisation argument, and the alienation analysis complete.

**Level 2 candidates:**
- **Social reproduction theory** — the argument that unwaged domestic and care labour is not separate from capitalism but constitutive of it; wages for housework debates reveal the political stakes.
- **Federici's primitive accumulation of the body** — the argument that the witch trials were specifically about breaking women's control over their own bodies and communal healing networks as a precondition for capitalist accumulation.
- **The double shift** — the structural position of women who perform wage labour and then return home to unwaged reproductive labour; tracing its economic function shows it is not a cultural hangover but a structural requirement.
- **Socialist feminism** — the tradition that refuses to subordinate gender liberation to class struggle or class struggle to gender liberation, insisting that the two are inseparable; the debates here remain live and unresolved.
- **Care as political terrain** — the argument that the crisis of care (childcare, elder care, mental health) is not a policy failure but a structural consequence of commodification, and that reclaiming care is itself a revolutionary act.

---

#### L1-10 · Praxis: The Unity of Theory and Action

This is where the map turns from orientation to motion. Praxis is Marx's term for the unity of theory and practice — not theory first and then action, not action without understanding, but both together, each informing the other. Its practical implication is that understanding alienation without organising tends to produce either paralysis or private enlightenment, both of which leave the system untouched. The tradition insists that knowledge is for changing the world, and that changing the world generates knowledge that no amount of reading alone can produce.

**Level 2 candidates:**
- **Consciousness-raising** — the feminist practice of collective reflection on personal experience as a method for developing political understanding; it is the most successful modern form of praxis and directly bridges the personal and the structural.
- **The union as school** — the argument that participation in collective bargaining and industrial action is itself a form of political education that cannot be replicated in the classroom.
- **Reflexive impotence** — Mark Fisher's name for the condition of someone who understands the system perfectly and concludes that nothing can be done; identifying it is the first step out of it.
- **Organising vs mobilising** — the distinction between building durable relationships and power (organising) and turning out people who already agree (mobilising); understanding it is the difference between a movement and a march.
- **The long game** — Gramsci's war of position applied to contemporary organising: what it looks like to build counter-institutions, alternative media, and working class culture as political infrastructure rather than just waiting for the next election cycle.

## Sequencing note

If you want the emotional and experiential foundation first, begin with **L1-01** (Alienation) and **L1-03** (Ideology/Hegemony). These two together explain what the system does to your inner life and why it is so hard to see clearly.

If you want the structural and strategic picture first, begin with **L1-02** (Class) and **L1-04** (the State). These anchor everything else in the material relations of power.

**L1-07** (Prefigurative Politics) and **L1-10** (Praxis) should be read early and returned to often — they are the antidote to the paralysis that pure critique can produce. The remedies are not at the end of the map; they run alongside the whole thing.

**L1-08** and **L1-09** are not optional supplements — they are corrections to the blind spots of the classical tradition without which the analysis is incomplete and the politics will reproduce the exclusions it claims to oppose.

The highest-leverage single entry point for someone in your position is **L1-03** — ideology and hegemony — because it is the skill of seeing the water you swim in, and once you can see it, everything else becomes more legible.
