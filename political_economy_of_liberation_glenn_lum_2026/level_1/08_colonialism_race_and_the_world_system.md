# L1-08 · Colonialism, Race, and the World System

The classical Marxist canon has a geography problem. It was written in London, Berlin, and Paris, by thinkers looking at smokestacks in Manchester and textile mills in the Rhineland, and it tells the story of capitalism as something that grew up inside Europe and then spread outward. That story is wrong in a specific and consequential way. The cotton in the Manchester mill was picked by enslaved people in Mississippi. The capital that built the mill was accumulated in the Atlantic triangle. The iron came from mines worked under colonial conditions. If you take this seriously, the unit of analysis cannot be the national economy and the central drama cannot be the wage relation alone. Capitalism was global before it was industrial, and it was racial before it was anything else.

This is the correction that the Black radical tradition and world-systems theory force on the classical frame. They are not addenda or special interest topics — they are arguments that the original picture had the scale and the cast wrong from the start. Once you see it, you cannot unsee it: the story of European prosperity is unintelligible without the story of colonial extraction, and the story of the modern working class is unintelligible without the story of who got counted as a worker and who got counted as property.

Start with the wealth question. Marx's account of how capitalism began — what he called the original or primitive accumulation — emphasised the enclosure of the English commons, the violent separation of peasants from their land, the creation of a propertyless class that had nothing to sell but its labour. All of this happened. But it is half the story. The other half is the Atlantic slave trade, the silver mines of Potosí, the destruction of Indian textile manufacturing, the plantation economies of the Caribbean. The seed capital for European industrialisation did not come from European thrift. It came from extraction at a scale that required the racialisation of entire continents to make morally tolerable. Eric Williams made this argument bluntly in 1944: slavery did not arise because of racism; racism arose to justify slavery. The economic relation came first, and the ideology that made it bearable was constructed afterward and then took on a life of its own.

This is where race acquires its independent logic. Once racial categories exist as the organising fiction of colonial extraction, they do not stay confined to the colony. They reshape the metropole. They sort the working class. They become the basis on which capital divides workers from each other and offers some of them what W.E.B. Du Bois called a psychological wage — the compensation of racial status to white workers who were materially exploited but symbolically elevated above Black workers. This is the answer to one of the oldest puzzles in left politics: why does the working class so reliably fail to act as a class? Often because it has been offered, and accepted, a racial settlement instead. You cannot understand the politics of the American South, the rise of European fascism, or the collapse of the post-war social democratic compact without this.

The Black radical tradition — Du Bois, C.L.R. James, Frantz Fanon, Angela Davis — built a body of thought that takes this history seriously. James's *The Black Jacobins* reread the Haitian Revolution as a working class revolution that happened before the European working class existed, led by enslaved people who took the rhetoric of the French Revolution more seriously than the French did. Fanon, writing from Algeria, analysed colonialism as a psychic as well as economic condition — one that produced specific forms of self-hatred and specific forms of rage, neither of which classical Marxism had vocabulary for. Davis connected the prison system, the gendered division of labour, and the legacy of slavery into a single analysis of how race, class, and gender intersect rather than stack. None of this is a softer Marxism. It is a more complete one.

World-systems theory, developed by Immanuel Wallerstein from the 1970s, is the structural counterpart to this tradition. Wallerstein's claim is that capitalism has never been a set of national economies that happened to trade with each other; it has always been a single global system divided into a core (where high-wage, high-tech production and financial control are concentrated), a periphery (where raw materials and cheap labour are extracted), and a semi-periphery that mediates between them. The position of a country in this hierarchy is not a developmental stage that everyone will eventually pass through. It is a structural location, and the wealth of the core depends on the poverty of the periphery. This reframes what development means. Underdevelopment is not a starting condition that aid and good governance can fix; it is an active product of the same global system that produces development elsewhere. The Congo is not poor despite globalisation. It is poor because of it.

The practical implication for how you think and organise is significant. If you take this frame seriously, certain reflexes have to change. National solutions to inequality that depend on continued extraction from the global south are not actually solutions — they are redistributions of imperial loot. A working class politics that does not address race is not neutral on race; it actively reproduces the white workers' settlement that has historically defeated working class politics. Solidarity is not a moral posture; it is a strategic recognition that the people picking your coffee, sewing your clothes, and assembling your phone are the same class as you, and that any movement which forgets this will be outflanked by capital, which has never forgotten it.

The deepest takeaway is methodological. When you encounter any analysis of the modern world — a policy proposal, a news story, a left strategy document — ask where its boundaries are drawn. Does it stop at the national border? Does it count only waged workers? Does it treat race as a cultural overlay on a colourblind economy? If so, it is working with the European frame that this topic exists to correct. The world is bigger and older and more violent than that frame admits, and the politics that emerges from the smaller frame keeps losing for reasons it cannot see.

## Level 2 candidates

**Primitive accumulation and colonialism** — Extending Marx's account of capitalism's origins to include the Atlantic slave trade, colonial extraction, and the destruction of indigenous economies that funded European industrialisation. Worth a deeper dive because it changes the unit of analysis from the nation to the world system and reframes what capitalism actually is.

**The Black radical tradition** — A focused treatment of Du Bois, James, Fanon, and Davis as a coherent intellectual tradition rather than a list of names. Worth the depth because each thinker addresses a specific failure of the classical frame — Du Bois on the white worker, James on revolutionary subjectivity, Fanon on colonial psychology, Davis on the carceral economy — and the tradition is more than the sum of its parts.

**The wages of whiteness** — Du Bois's argument that white workers accept a psychological compensation of racial status in exchange for material exploitation, and how this divides the working class. Worth its own treatment because it is the single sharpest explanation for why class solidarity repeatedly fails along racial lines, and the mechanism is still operating in contemporary politics.

**World-systems theory** — Wallerstein's framework of core, periphery, and semi-periphery as a single global capitalist system rather than a collection of national economies. Worth depth because it reframes development and underdevelopment as structurally linked rather than sequential, with direct implications for how you read globalisation, migration, and inequality.

**Decolonial theory** — The argument that political and economic independence from colonial power is incomplete without decolonising knowledge, culture, and subjectivity. Worth depth because it extends the analysis beyond material extraction into the epistemic and cultural dimensions of imperial power, and grounds a critique of how the Western frame itself shapes what counts as legitimate knowledge.
