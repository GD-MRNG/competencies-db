# L1-07 · Prefigurative Politics

The deepest trap in radical politics is the assumption that liberation is a destination. You analyse the system, you organise against it, you win power, and then — finally, on the other side of the rupture — you build the world you actually want to live in. The trouble with this sequence is not just that the rupture rarely comes. It is that the means you use to get there shape what arrives. A movement built on hierarchy, discipline, and instrumental relationships does not suddenly produce horizontal, free, and humane institutions the morning after victory. It produces what it practised. Prefigurative politics is the recognition that the future is not built after the struggle; it is built by how you struggle.

The phrase itself is plain. To prefigure is to enact in advance, to embody now what you claim to be working toward. If you say you want a world without bosses, the organisation you build cannot have bosses dressed up in revolutionary language. If you say you want a world where care is shared and labour is dignified, the meeting cannot run on the unpaid emotional work of three women while the men talk strategy. The form of the movement is itself a political claim. This is not a slogan about authenticity or personal integrity — it is a structural argument about how new social relations actually come into being. They are practised into existence or they are not built at all.

This is what makes prefigurative politics the bridge between the diagnostic side of the tradition and the question of what to do. Alienation, as the early Marx described it, is the severing of people from their work, from each other, and from their own creative nature by the structure of wage labour. The classical revolutionary answer was that this could only be healed by abolishing capitalism at the level of the state and the economy. The prefigurative answer is not a rejection of that — it is an insistence that the healing also has to happen in the building. A workers' cooperative, a tenants' union that makes decisions collectively, a mutual aid network that responds to a flood — these are not consolation prizes for people who have given up on revolution. They are the only places where the experience of unalienated cooperation actually exists, and without that experience the abstract demand for a different world has no felt content.

The tradition comes most strongly from anarchism and libertarian socialism, and its central quarrel is with the Leninist position that revolutionary ends justify whatever organisational means are required to achieve them. Bakunin's prediction — that a vanguard party seizing state power would simply install a new ruling class — was the first sharp version of this argument, and the twentieth century gave it more evidence than anyone wanted. The prefigurative critique generalises the point: authoritarian means produce authoritarian ends regardless of intention, because the people, habits, and structures you build through the struggle are the people, habits, and structures that exist on the other side. There is no clean handover from the disciplined cadre to the free commune. Whatever you have practised is what you have.

In concrete terms this is what the strategy of dual power describes — the patient construction of alternative institutions that meet real needs alongside and in tension with the existing state, gradually shifting where people turn for housing, food, healthcare, conflict resolution, education. The Paris Commune of 1871 is the archetype: not a seizure of national power but a city running itself on radically different principles, briefly, before being crushed. The Zapatistas in Chiapas have sustained autonomous municipalities for decades. Mondragon in the Basque Country shows worker ownership functioning at industrial scale. These are not utopian experiments; they are working demonstrations that the cooperative forms which classical economics treats as impossible are in fact ordinary human capacities that have been actively suppressed. Kropotkin's argument that mutual aid is as fundamental to human life as competition is not sentimental — it is descriptive of what happens whenever the structures that prevent it are weakened.

The honest objection to prefigurative politics is that it can collapse into lifestyle, into small islands of right relations that change nothing about the structure surrounding them. A co-op bakery does not abolish capitalism. A consensus-run collective does not threaten the state. Read uncharitably, the position can become a permission slip for political withdrawal — a way of feeling that one is doing radical work without ever confronting power. This is a real risk, and it is why prefigurative practice without strategic ambition tends to degrade into subculture. The serious version of the position is not "build the alternative instead of fighting" but "the alternative is what you are fighting for, so it had better exist somewhere before you win, or you will not know how to build it after." Prefiguration is the laboratory; it is not the whole of the work.

What this changes, practically, is the texture of organising. It means the meeting matters as much as the campaign. It means that how decisions are made, who speaks, who cleans up, who is paid and who is not, who is listened to when they are upset — these are not secondary process questions but the actual political content of what you are doing. It means the remedy for alienation is not waiting somewhere on the other side of an event called the revolution. It is available, partially and imperfectly, in any space where people are practising a different way of being together. The skill this topic builds is the ability to read the form of an organisation as a political statement — to ask of any movement, however righteous its slogans, whether the world it is actually building inside itself is one you would want to live in.

## Level 2 candidates

**The commune form** — A historical line from the Paris Commune of 1871 through the Zapatistas to contemporary housing communes, read as practical experiments in post-capitalist organisation. Worth a deep dive because the commune is the most concrete answer to "what does the alternative actually look like at scale" and its repeated emergence across very different contexts suggests something structural rather than utopian.

**Mutual aid** — Kropotkin's argument that cooperation is as foundational to human and animal life as competition, traced through working-class history as a sustained practice rather than an idealistic gesture. Worth deeper treatment because mutual aid is the most accessible entry point into prefigurative practice and its history defends it against the charge of naivety.

**The cooperative economy** — Worker-owned enterprises as a prefigurative economic form, anchored by the Mondragon federation in the Basque Country as the largest working example. Worth depth because it forces the question of whether prefigurative forms can survive at industrial scale and what they have to compromise to do so.

**The relationship between means and ends** — The argument that authoritarian means produce authoritarian ends regardless of intention, which is the philosophical core of the prefigurative critique of Leninism. Worth a deep dive because this is the hinge of the entire anarchist–Marxist split and the place where the twentieth century's evidence weighs heaviest.

**Dual power** — The strategy of building alternative institutions that meet real needs alongside the existing state, gradually shifting where people turn for what they need. Worth deeper treatment because it is the most developed answer to the charge that prefigurative politics is merely lifestyle, and it specifies what the relationship between the alternative and the existing system actually looks like in practice.
