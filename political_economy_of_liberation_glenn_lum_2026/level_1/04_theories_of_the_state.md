# L1-04 · Theories of the State

Before you can have a serious argument about strategy — about whether to vote, to organise, to build, to fight, to abstain — you have to answer a prior question that most political conversation skips: what is the state? Not what does it do, not whether you like it, but what kind of thing is it. Every strategic disagreement on the left, from the gentlest social democrat to the most committed insurrectionary, can be traced back to a different answer to this question. People who think they are arguing about tactics are usually arguing about ontology without knowing it.

This is why the question matters more than it appears to. If the state is a neutral tool — a set of institutions that will serve whoever wins the election — then your job is to win elections and pass good laws. If the state is structurally bound to capital — built from the ground up to protect property — then winning elections is a category error, because the institutions you inherit will constrain what you can do regardless of your intentions. If the state is itself a form of domination, then capturing it is not a victory but a substitution: the same boot on the same neck, with a different face above it. The same person, holding three different theories of the state, would pursue three completely different lives.

The liberal theory is the default, and most people hold it without realising they hold a theory at all. On this view the state is a referee — an arbiter that stands above competing interests, enforces rules fairly, and corrects market failures when necessary. Politics is the contest over who gets to use this neutral instrument and for what ends. This is the assumption baked into civics class, into mainstream journalism, into the entire grammar of representative democracy. It is also the assumption that the entire Marxist tradition was built to demolish, because if you accept it you cannot explain why, century after century, every state in the capitalist world ends up reliably protecting property over people.

The classical Marxist answer is that the state is an instrument of class rule. The police protect property, not persons. The courts enforce contracts, not justice. The military secures the conditions of accumulation at home and abroad. This is not a conspiracy or a corruption — it is the design. On this reading, the state's apparent neutrality is itself an ideological achievement that conceals what the state actually does. Lenin pushed this further in State and Revolution: the bourgeois state is not a tool that can be picked up and used by workers, because its institutional form is shaped by the function it serves. You cannot run a workers' republic through a police force trained to break strikes. The conclusion is that the existing state must be smashed and replaced with a different kind of structure — the soviet, the council, the commune.

Poulantzas, writing in the 1970s, tried to rescue something from both sides. He argued that the state has relative autonomy from capital — it is not a pure instrument, it has its own logic, its own bureaucratic interests, and it can be a genuine site of struggle. The state does protect capital, but it does so through contradictions that can be exploited. This is the theoretical ground beneath every serious reformist project: the welfare state, public healthcare, labour rights, all the things that were won within capitalism by treating the state as terrain rather than enemy. It is also the theory that explains why those gains are always vulnerable — relative autonomy is relative, and when capital is sufficiently threatened, the state's autonomy contracts.

The anarchist position rejects all three of the above by relocating the problem. The issue is not which class controls the state but the state form itself. Centralised, hierarchical, coercive power produces domination regardless of whose name is on the door. Bakunin, in the 1870s, predicted with eerie precision what would happen if Marxists ever took state power: a new class of administrators and party functionaries would emerge, exercising power over the workers in the workers' name, and the dictatorship of the proletariat would become a dictatorship over the proletariat. The twentieth century did not refute him. From this position, the strategic task is not to seize the state but to build forms of life and organisation that make the state unnecessary — federations, communes, councils, mutual aid networks that meet human needs without command from above.

Notice what these four positions actually disagree about. They do not disagree about whether capitalism is bad, or whether workers are exploited, or whether the existing distribution of power is unjust. They disagree about what the state is, and therefore about what can be done with it, to it, or instead of it. This is why the reformist–revolutionary split and the Marxist–anarchist split are not personality differences or temperamental preferences. They are working out the practical consequences of different theories of an institution that touches every part of your life.

The practical skill this topic builds is the ability to listen to any political argument — about elections, protests, policy, revolution, abolition, reform — and hear the theory of the state underneath it. Once you can hear it, you can ask the real question: not "is this strategy correct" but "what would have to be true about the state for this strategy to make sense, and do I believe that." Most political disagreement becomes legible at that level. You do not have to resolve the question to use it. You just have to stop pretending it has already been answered.

## Level 2 candidates

**The liberal state** — The view that the state is a neutral arbiter between competing interests, enforcing rules fairly above the fray. Worth a deeper dive because it is the default theory most people hold without naming it, and the entire critical tradition is built as a response to it; you cannot evaluate the alternatives without first understanding what they reject.

**The state as instrument of class rule** — The classical Marxist position that state institutions (law, police, courts, military) are structurally organised around the protection of property relations. Worth deeper treatment because the argument depends on a careful demonstration that the state's apparent neutrality is itself produced by class power, and that demonstration is what gives the position its force.

**Lenin's State and Revolution** — The argument that the bourgeois state cannot be captured and used by workers but must be smashed and replaced with a new institutional form. Worth a Level 2 because it is the single most influential and most contested text on this question, and the entire history of twentieth-century revolutionary politics is a commentary on whether Lenin was right.

**The relative autonomy of the state** — Poulantzas' revision arguing the state has its own logic and can be a genuine terrain of struggle, not just a pure instrument of capital. Worth deeper treatment because it grounds the case for serious reformist strategy from a Marxist starting point, and understanding it prevents you from collapsing all left politics into a binary of revolution or naivety.

**The anarchist abolition position** — The argument that seizing state power reproduces domination under new management, and that the strategic task is to build forms of life that make the state unnecessary. Worth a Level 2 because Bakunin's prediction of what Marxist state power would become has more historical evidence behind it than most political predictions ever do, and engaging it honestly is the hardest test for anyone in the Marxist tradition.
