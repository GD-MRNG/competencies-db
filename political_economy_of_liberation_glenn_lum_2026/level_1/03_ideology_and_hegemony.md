# L1-03 · Ideology and Hegemony

The most powerful form of control is the one you mistake for reality. If you had to keep a population of working people compliant inside a system that extracts most of what they produce, you would quickly discover that police and prisons are too expensive, too slow, and too obvious to do the job at scale. You would need something cheaper and harder to see: you would need people to govern themselves, to find the arrangement broadly fair, to imagine no serious alternative, and to interpret their own suffering as personal failure rather than structural output. That self-policing condition is what Gramsci called hegemony, and recognising it in the texture of your own thoughts is the most practically useful skill in the whole political tradition.

Antonio Gramsci was an Italian communist who spent the last eleven years of his life in one of Mussolini's prisons, writing in fragments because a fascist prosecutor had famously demanded the court "stop this brain from working for twenty years." The question that occupied him was not why revolutions happen but why they don't — specifically, why the working class in advanced capitalist countries had failed to act on its own interests despite manifestly being exploited. His answer reframed the entire problem. The ruling class does not maintain power principally through the state's monopoly on violence. It maintains power by controlling what counts as common sense — the unstated background assumptions through which people interpret everything else. Schools, newspapers, churches, entertainment, professional norms, the rhythms of family life: all of these transmit and reinforce a worldview in which the current arrangement appears not as one possibility among many but as simply the way things are.

The crucial move here is to stop thinking about ideology as a set of explicit beliefs you could write down and debate. Ideology in this sense is rarely argued for, because it does not present itself as an argument. It presents itself as the floor everyone is standing on. "Of course you have to work for a living." "Of course housing costs what it costs." "Of course the economy needs to grow." "Of course some people are just better at this than others." Each of these is a historically specific, contestable claim dressed up as a feature of nature. The ideological work is not in the foreground belief; it is in the word "of course." When you find yourself nodding before a claim has even been made, you are inside the hegemonic field.

This matters because it dissolves a problem that otherwise looks like personal weakness. You probably have moments where you can see clearly that the system is rigged, and other moments where that same clarity slips and you find yourself wondering if you simply haven't worked hard enough, networked enough, optimised enough. You are not being inconsistent; you are oscillating between standing outside the common sense and standing inside it. The inside view is constantly reinforced by every newsfeed, every workplace incentive, every offhand remark from people you respect. The outside view has to be actively maintained against that pressure. Understanding hegemony is what lets you stop reading those oscillations as a failure of personal conviction and start reading them as the predictable result of swimming in a saturated ideological medium.

A few specific mechanisms are worth naming because they show up everywhere once you can see them. Naturalisation is the move that converts a historical arrangement into an apparent fact of nature — "people have always competed for resources," when what they have actually done is wildly variable across history. Individualisation is the move that takes a structural problem (wages haven't risen with productivity for forty years) and reformulates it as a personal one (have you considered a side hustle?). The horizon move, which Mark Fisher named capitalist realism, doesn't even bother defending the system on its merits; it simply makes the alternative unimaginable, so that even people who hate the arrangement cannot picture coherently what they would replace it with. The point of all three is to short-circuit the question before it reaches the level of conscious deliberation.

Hegemony is not total, which is why noticing it matters. Gramsci was clear that the common sense of any era is contradictory and unstable, full of older fragments that don't quite cohere with the dominant story, full of lived experiences that don't match what people are told. Workers know when they are being underpaid even if they cannot articulate why. Parents know that something is wrong with how their children are being raised even if they accept the official story about screens or schooling. These cracks are where what Gramsci called counter-hegemonic work happens: the patient construction of alternative common sense in unions, neighbourhood organisations, independent media, study groups, and ordinary conversations where someone says the thing everyone is thinking but no one has said. This is not propaganda in the cynical sense. It is the slow labour of building a shared frame in which the current arrangement looks like what it actually is — a specific, recent, contestable way of organising human life.

The practical skill this topic builds is a kind of double vision. You learn to listen to ordinary speech — your own included — for the unargued premises, the appeals to inevitability, the slide from "is" to "must be," the conversion of political questions into technical or personal ones. You start to notice when a news story presents a market outcome as if it were weather, when an economic indicator is treated as a verdict on human worth, when a piece of received wisdom turns out, on inspection, to be about forty years old. None of this requires you to become a bore who corrects everyone at dinner. It requires you to stop letting the common sense pass uninspected through your own head. Once you can see the water, you can decide whether you want to keep drinking it.

## Level 2 candidates

**Hegemony** — The specific mechanism by which the worldview of a dominant class comes to be experienced as the universal common sense of an entire society, including by the people it harms. Worth a deeper dive because the Level 1 post sketches the concept but does not work through Gramsci's distinction between coercion and consent, or the role of intellectuals, civil society, and the integral state in producing consent at scale.

**The war of position** — Gramsci's strategic concept that ideological and cultural ground must be contested over the long term, in the institutions of civil society, before or alongside any direct political contest for power. This deserves its own treatment because it is the bridge between ideology as analysis and organising as practice, and it reframes apparently non-political terrain (schools, media, neighbourhoods, culture) as the actual site of struggle.

**Naturalisation** — The rhetorical and cognitive move that converts historically specific arrangements into apparent facts of nature, so that "the way things are" becomes indistinguishable from "the way things must be." A Level 2 treatment would catalogue the specific linguistic markers and argumentative shapes that signal naturalisation, turning it into a practical detection skill.

**Capitalist realism** — Mark Fisher's 2009 diagnosis of a specific contemporary form of ideological closure: not the belief that capitalism is good but the inability to coherently imagine anything else. Worth its own post because it names something distinctive about the present moment that Gramsci's framework alone doesn't quite reach, and because it connects directly to the depression, exhaustion, and political paralysis that many readers will recognise in themselves.

**Meritocracy** — The belief that outcomes in life broadly track effort and ability, and the corresponding conversion of structural inequality into personal verdict. Deserves its own treatment because it is arguably the single most effective ideological mechanism in contemporary capitalism, the one that does the most work converting alienation into private shame, and the one most resistant to direct argument because it flatters its believers.
