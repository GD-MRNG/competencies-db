# L1-06 · Revolutionary Theory and Its Splits

If you have spent any time around the left, you have probably noticed that the people who agree most about what is wrong disagree most viciously about what to do. This is not a failure of discipline or a sign of sectarian pettiness — though it often presents as both. It is the surface expression of a genuine and unresolved set of problems that the revolutionary tradition has been arguing about for roughly a hundred and fifty years, and the disagreements track real fault lines in the analysis of power itself. The mistake is to assume one side has the answer and the others are confused. The more useful posture is to understand that each major branch identified a real problem that the others failed to solve, and that the splits persist because the underlying tensions are not artefacts of personality but features of the terrain.

The tradition fractures around three questions, and almost every internal argument can be traced back to one of them. Who is the revolutionary subject — who actually changes things, and through what kind of consciousness? What form should organisation take — and what does that form do to the people inside it? And what is the goal — what does "after" actually look like, and is the path to it continuous with the destination or does it require a violent rupture? Different answers to these questions produce different traditions, and the traditions are not interchangeable. They have different theories of human nature underneath them.

The Leninist answer starts from a hard observation: left to its own devices, the working class develops trade-union consciousness, not revolutionary consciousness. It fights for better wages, shorter hours, more respect — and stops there. Lenin's conclusion was that revolutionary politics requires a disciplined party of professional revolutionaries who can hold the strategic horizon that workers under daily pressure cannot. This is vanguardism, and it is the most influential idea in twentieth-century revolutionary politics because it actually worked once — the Bolsheviks took power. It is also the most contested idea in the tradition, because what followed in Russia gave the anarchist critique a century of vindication and made "the party" into a word that means something specific and frightening.

The council communist answer points to a different historical reality: in 1905, in 1917, in Germany in 1918, in Hungary in 1956, workers spontaneously formed councils — workplace and neighbourhood assemblies that ran production, distribution, and defence directly. The soviets were not built by parties; they emerged from struggle and were then, in the Bolshevik case, systematically subordinated to the party. Rosa Luxemburg's position is the bridge here: revolutionary consciousness emerges from the mass strike and the direct experience of collective action, not from a vanguard's tutelage. The council tradition takes self-organisation as both the means and the form of the future society, and it treats the party form with suspicion precisely because it has watched parties absorb and replace the councils they claimed to represent.

The anarchist answer goes further and cuts deeper. Bakunin, arguing with Marx in the First International in the 1870s, predicted exactly what would happen if Marxists took state power: a new ruling class of intellectuals and administrators would emerge, governing in the name of the workers, and the structure of domination would reproduce itself under different management. He was, by any honest reading of the twentieth century, correct. The anarchist position is not that the state is bad and should be replaced with a better state, but that the seizure of centralised power by any group, however well-intentioned, recreates the conditions of oppression. The conclusion is that organisation must be horizontal, federated, and built from below, and that the means must already be the ends.

Each of these positions identifies a real problem. The Leninists are correct that spontaneous worker consciousness has limits, that strategic coordination matters, and that diffuse movements can be crushed by disciplined opponents. The council communists are correct that self-organisation is the actual school of revolutionary capacity and that a politics done to people on their behalf produces passivity. The anarchists are correct that the structures you build during the struggle become the structures of the world after, and that concentrated power has its own logic regardless of who holds it. Each position also has a characteristic failure mode: Leninism produces party dictatorships that betray the revolution they made; council communism produces moments of brilliant self-organisation that get crushed because no one coordinated their defence; anarchism produces principled prefigurative communities that remain marginal because they refuse the instruments of scale.

The practical point is that you do not need to resolve this debate to participate in it, and you should be suspicious of anyone who tells you the question is settled. The traditions are not bullet points on a menu — they are different theories of what humans are and what power does to them, and they imply genuinely different practices. What you can do is hold the three questions — subject, form, goal — as a diagnostic. When you read a piece of strategic argument, when you encounter an organisation, when you find yourself drawn to a particular line, ask: which subject is this assuming, which form does it take for granted, which goal does it presume continuous with its means? That single habit will save you from years of confusion. The splits in the revolutionary tradition are not noise to be filtered out. They are the signal.

## Level 2 candidates

**Vanguardism** — Lenin's argument that the working class alone cannot develop revolutionary consciousness and requires a disciplined party of professional revolutionaries to lead it. Worth going deeper because it is the most consequential organisational idea in twentieth-century left politics, and understanding both its internal logic and its catastrophic historical record is the precondition for any serious strategic thinking about parties.

**Workers' councils and soviets** — the historical emergence of self-organised workplace and community councils as an alternative to both parliamentary politics and party leadership, from the Russian soviets of 1905 and 1917 to the German councils of 1918. Worth going deeper because the council form is the most concrete answer the tradition has produced to the question of what post-capitalist self-governance actually looks like in practice, and its repeated suppression — often by the left itself — is one of the most important and least examined patterns in modern political history.

**Anarchism's critique of power** — the argument that the seizure of state power by any group, even a revolutionary one, reproduces domination, traced through Bakunin's prescient predictions about Marxist state power. Worth going deeper because it is the sharpest internal critique of the revolutionary tradition and the strongest case for taking the question of means seriously, and because reading Bakunin against Marx is one of the best ways to understand why the twentieth century unfolded as it did.

**The party form** — the specific debate about whether revolutionary organisation should be a disciplined vanguard, a mass party, a network, a federation, or something else entirely. Worth going deeper because organisational form is not a technical question downstream of theory — it determines what movements can actually do, who has power within them, and what they become if they succeed.

**Rosa Luxemburg's spontaneism** — her argument against Leninist discipline, locating revolutionary consciousness in the mass strike and direct experience of struggle rather than in party tutelage. Worth going deeper because Luxemburg is the most important bridge figure between authoritarian and libertarian socialism, and her critique of Lenin — written before the worst of what she predicted came true — is one of the most prescient documents in the tradition.
