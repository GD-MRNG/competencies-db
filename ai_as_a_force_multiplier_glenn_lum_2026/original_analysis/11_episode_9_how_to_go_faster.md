# How to Go Faster

Going faster is the goal almost everyone gets wrong. The instinct is to add — more hours, more tools, more caffeine, another productivity app, a new system synced across three devices. But speed in knowledge work is not produced by adding throughput to a strained system. It is produced by removing the things that quietly drain the system's capacity to do anything well. Your brain is the bottleneck. Everything else is upstream or downstream of that single fact.

Start with attention, because attention is the resource everything else converts into output. Your email inbox is somebody else's to-do list. Slack is somebody else's to-do list. The notification on your phone is a tech company's to-do list. If you let any of these touch your working memory the moment they arrive, you have stopped being the director of your day and become a processor for other people's priorities. The fix is not willpower — willpower is a finite daily budget that is mostly spent by evening, which is exactly when you reach for your phone. The fix is to make the distracting thing physically harder to reach than the productive thing. Batch email to one or two fixed windows. Put the phone in a drawer, on airplane mode, behind a deliberate friction (a device in the garage, a locked app, a separate room). You are not trying to be disciplined. You are trying to design a day in which discipline is not required.

Once attention is protected, direct it. A daily to-do list of five items — one major task that genuinely moves things forward, four smaller ones — beats any maximalist productivity tool, because a list you can actually finish is a list you will actually start. Above the daily list sits a weekly list, above that a monthly one, above that an annual one. The point of this hierarchy is not bureaucracy. It is the continuous breakdown of large goals into the smallest unit your brain will accept without flinching. "Write a book" triggers paralysis. "Write 500 words today" does not. This is the same pattern as breaking 100 push-ups into sets of five: you keep subdividing until your brain stops resisting.

Hours are not equal, and treating them as if they were is one of the most expensive mistakes in productivity. The first eight hours after you wake up are when your brain is freshest, before adenosine builds up and fog sets in. Those hours have a different price tag than the dregs of the afternoon, and you should spend them accordingly. Deep work — coding, writing, anything creative or cognitively heavy — goes into the expensive hours, in 90-minute blocks with 15–30 minute breaks, because creative work has a high initiation cost and short bursts never let you reach the zone. Email, admin, and rote tasks go into the cheap hours in 25-minute Pomodoro chunks. If you are spending a 10am hour scrolling or replying to messages, you are buying junk food with cash you should be saving for groceries.

The second order of going faster is biological. Your brain runs on hardware, and the hardware has settings. Sleep is the largest lever — there is no supplement, stimulant, or technique that produces the cognitive effect of a genuinely good night's sleep, and almost everything else you do to "perform" is a worse substitute for it. Blood sugar is the second lever: heavy carbs and sugar at lunch produce the 3pm slump that you then try to fight with caffeine, which then disrupts the sleep that would have prevented the slump. Caffeine itself is borrowed energy, not free energy — it blocks adenosine receptors temporarily and the bill comes due as a crash, often inside your sleep window even if you cannot feel it. Exercise, counterintuitively, produces time rather than consuming it: a half-hour walk or a few sets of squats during the afternoon dip can buy back two hours of useful work.

Then there are the cues that tell your brain which mode it is in. The same room cannot be both your office and your sanctuary; the same clothes cannot mean both work and rest. If you work from home, separate the space, even if the space is a corner. Have work clothes and not-work clothes. Have a work playlist. Have a shutdown sequence in the evening — an alarm thirty minutes before bed, lights moved from overhead to floor level (mimicking firelight rather than midday sun), no devices in the bedroom, a cool dark room. These cues are not decorative. They are how you stop carrying work-stress into your evenings and morning-fog into your work.

Finally, decisions. Every decision you make withdraws from the same budget that powers strategic thinking, so the trick is to pre-decide the boring ones. Routinise breakfast, clothing, the route to work, the time you check email — anything where novelty offers no real upside. Then build heuristics for the decisions that matter: never commit on a first call, never write an email longer than five sentences, never accept a task that isn't a "hell yeah" unless it's genuinely necessary. These rules are not rigidity. They are how you stop spending high-value cognition on questions you have already answered, so you have something left for the questions you haven't.

The thread tying all of this together is simple: going faster is not about doing more per hour. It is about protecting the hours that matter, removing the friction from starting, and refusing to let other people, other apps, and your own bad defaults consume the cognitive bandwidth you need for work that actually counts. Your brain is a precious resource. Stop dumping things into it.

# Discussion

## Why This Conversation Is Happening

Most people think speed in knowledge work comes from adding more: more hours, more tools, more hacks, more urgency. But most of the time, work does not slow down because you lack throughput. It slows down because your attention is fragmented, your best cognitive hours are misallocated, and your environment is constantly pulling you into other people’s priorities. You are trying to drive faster with the brakes lightly on.

That matters because in knowledge work the real engine is not time alone. It is high-quality attention applied at the right moment to the right task. If you do not understand that, you will keep trying to optimize around the edges while your actual bottleneck — your brain’s usable cognitive bandwidth — gets consumed by distraction, context switching, bad timing, and avoidable decisions.

This topic exists because “go faster” sounds like a motivation problem when it is mostly a systems problem. The concept is trying to replace the vague idea of working harder with a more exact one: protect scarce cognitive resources, spend them where they matter, and design your day so that focus is the default rather than the exception.

## What You Need To Know First

**1. Attention residue**  
When you switch from one task to another, some of your mind stays attached to the first one for a while. That leftover drag is attention residue. You do not need the research details here; the practical point is enough: constant switching makes you slower than the clock suggests because each switch carries hidden recovery cost.

**2. Deep work vs. shallow work**  
Deep work is cognitively demanding work that requires concentration and creates high-value output: writing, designing, coding, solving hard problems. Shallow work is lighter coordination or maintenance work: email, scheduling, quick admin, routine updates. The article depends on treating these as different resource consumers that should not compete for the same hours.

**3. Friction design**  
Friction design means making unwanted actions harder and desired actions easier. Putting your phone in another room is friction against distraction; keeping today’s task list visible is friction reduction for starting work. The article leans heavily on this idea because it rejects willpower as the main control mechanism.

**4. Decision fatigue**  
Every decision consumes a bit of mental energy. Too many small decisions leave less capacity for important ones. This matters because the article argues that speed improves when you pre-decide low-value choices and reserve cognition for high-value work.

## The Key Ideas, Connected

**1. Going faster starts with subtracting drains, not adding effort.**  
The article’s first move is to reverse the usual productivity instinct. Most people try to speed up by layering on tools or effort. But if the system is already leaking attention and cognitive energy, extra effort just gets poured into a weak container. So the real question becomes: what is draining the container? That leads directly to attention.

**2. Attention is the primary resource because everything valuable is downstream of it.**  
In knowledge work, output quality depends on what you can hold in mind long enough to do meaningful thinking. If your attention is constantly interrupted by inboxes, messages, and notifications, your day stops being organized around your priorities and starts being organized around incoming demands. That is why the article frames email, Slack, and phone notifications as other people’s to-do lists entering your working memory. Once attention is identified as the core asset, the next issue is how to protect it.

**3. Protection works better through friction and environment than through willpower.**  
The article is explicit that self-control is too fragile to be the main system. If the distracting thing is always within reach, you are depending on repeated acts of resistance all day long. A better design is to make distraction physically or digitally inconvenient and focus relatively easy. This is the practical meaning of batching email, putting the phone away, and adding deliberate barriers. Once protected attention exists, though, it still needs direction or it will be wasted on vague intentions.

**4. Clear task hierarchy turns protected attention into usable motion.**  
The daily list, nested under weekly, monthly, and annual lists, is the structure that connects immediate action to longer goals. The article’s point is not that lists are magical; it is that they reduce the intimidation of large goals by shrinking them into units your brain will actually start. A big ambition becomes actionable only when it becomes a small task. This sets up the next refinement: not all hours are equally capable of handling those tasks.

**5. Time should be allocated by cognitive quality, not just by availability.**  
A central idea in the article is that the first hours of the day are more valuable for demanding work than later hours. If you spend your freshest attention on shallow tasks, you are using premium cognitive time on low-return activity. So task scheduling should follow energy quality: deep work goes into your high-value hours, shallow work into the cheaper ones. This matters because it turns productivity from a simple scheduling problem into an energy-matching problem. Once energy matters, biology becomes impossible to ignore.

**6. Your biological state is part of the productivity system, not separate from it.**  
Sleep, blood sugar, caffeine, and movement all affect the quality of the attention you are trying to protect and allocate. The article’s deeper claim is that many “focus problems” are really hardware problems in disguise. Poor sleep, destabilizing meals, and stimulant misuse degrade the very cognitive performance you are trying to optimize with apps and systems. Exercise, by contrast, can restore usable energy. Once you accept that cognition runs on biology, it becomes natural to support it with cues and routines.

**7. Environmental and sensory cues help the brain switch modes more reliably.**  
Dedicated spaces, work clothes, playlists, lighting changes, and shutdown rituals are not presented as aesthetic preferences but as mode-switching signals. They help the brain distinguish work time from recovery time. This matters because blurred contexts create blurred mental states: you carry work fog into rest and rest inertia into work. Designing cues reduces transition cost. That leads to the final layer: reducing avoidable cognitive load from repeated small choices.

**8. Pre-deciding low-value decisions preserves capacity for high-value ones.**  
The article closes by arguing that routine choices should be systematized so that strategic thinking is not spent on trivia. Standard breakfasts, repeated clothing choices, fixed email windows, and heuristics like “never decide on a first call” reduce the number of judgments you must make from scratch. This completes the chain: remove drains, protect attention, direct it with task hierarchy, match task type to energy quality, support that energy biologically, reinforce it with cues, and conserve it further by reducing decision overhead.

## Handles and Anchors

**1. Your brain is the bottleneck.**  
This is the article’s master idea. Speed is not mainly limited by apps or hours. It is limited by the quality and availability of your cognitive bandwidth.

**2. Protect the expensive hours.**  
Some hours produce disproportionately valuable output. Treat them like scarce capital, not like generic calendar space.

**3. Make distraction harder than focus.**  
This is the practical rule that replaces vague advice about discipline. Good systems increase the friction of bad defaults and lower the friction of good ones.

## What This Changes When You Build

**A knowledge worker who understands this will design their environment to block interruption because attention loss costs more than the few seconds each distraction appears to take.**  
So they will batch communication, remove devices from reach, and create friction around reactive tools instead of relying on repeated self-control.

**A knowledge worker who understands this will separate deep work from shallow work in their calendar because cognitively expensive tasks require better hours and longer uninterrupted blocks than admin does.**  
They will stop treating all work as interchangeable and schedule writing, coding, and problem-solving differently from email and coordination.

**A knowledge worker who understands this will decompose goals into very small daily tasks because the main barrier to starting is often psychological overwhelm rather than lack of total time.**  
They will use task hierarchies to connect long-term aims to concrete next steps their brain can accept immediately.

**A knowledge worker who understands this will manage sleep, meals, caffeine, and movement as performance variables because cognitive quality is partly a biology problem, not just a planning problem.**  
That means they will look for energy crashes and focus problems in their physical inputs rather than only in their software or habits.

**A knowledge worker who understands this will build personal rules for repeated low-value decisions because preserving mental energy for ambiguous, strategic work creates more leverage than improvising every small choice.**  
They will standardize routine decisions and use heuristics to reduce the background tax of constant decision-making.
