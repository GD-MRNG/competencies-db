# L1-05 · Writing and Communication

The economics of writing have collapsed, and most people are still pricing their work at the old rate. Drafting used to be expensive — the bottleneck was the act of producing prose, so you wrote three drafts, maybe five if the stakes were high, and called it done. That bottleneck is gone. What remains is the work that was always the actual work: knowing what you want to say, and judging whether what got said is any good. AI has not made writing easier. It has made the cheap parts free and exposed how much of writing was never about typing in the first place.

The mental model to hold is that writing is now a three-stage process where the middle stage is essentially free. Upstream is clarity of thinking — the part where you decide what you actually mean, what the reader needs, and what shape the argument takes. Midstream is drafting, editing, and iteration — the mechanical production of prose, which AI does competently and tirelessly. Downstream is judgment — does this sound like you, is it accurate, does it land, is the tone right for this audience. The force multiplication comes from compressing the middle to near-zero so you can spend more cycles on the ends. The failure mode comes from letting AI compress the ends too — letting it decide what you mean, or letting it decide whether the output is good.

Treat AI as a writing coach, draft generator, and editor — not as a writer. This distinction sounds semantic but it isn't. When you ask AI to write something for you, you're outsourcing the upstream work; you'll get prose that is grammatically clean and structurally competent and almost always slightly wrong, because AI is guessing what you would have wanted to say. When you ask AI to draft from your thinking, edit your prose, or coach you on a specific weakness, you're keeping the judgment loop intact. The output looks similar in either case. The trajectory of your work over six months looks completely different.

The brutal part of this multiplier is iteration count. You used to iterate five times on an important email. Now you can iterate fifty, and the marginal cost of each iteration is seconds. You can ask for three versions in different tones. You can ask for the version that's 30% shorter, then the version that opens with the conclusion, then the version that lands harder on the second paragraph. Most of these will be worse than what you had. A few will be better in ways you wouldn't have found alone. The skill is no longer producing drafts; it's recognising which draft is better and why, fast enough that iteration doesn't become procrastination.

The same dynamic compounds across formats. The mental cost of switching between email, internal documentation, a Slack message, a long-form post, and a customer-facing brief used to be real — each format has its own conventions, its own register, its own shape. AI handles format translation cleanly, which means you can move the same idea across five surfaces in the time it used to take to write one. This matters more than it sounds. The reason most people communicate inconsistently across channels is that the cost of doing it well in every channel is too high. That cost is now negotiable.

The trap, and it is a real trap, is voice. AI default voice is competent, generic, slightly over-explained, allergic to strong claims, and instantly recognisable. If you let AI own your voice, your writing converges to the median of the internet and your distinctiveness — the thing readers actually came for — disappears. The defence is structural: you need a clear voice before AI can preserve it, you need to recognise AI default voice when you see it, and you need to treat the final pass as non-negotiable human work. The best AI-assisted writing reads as if AI was never involved, because the writer used AI to produce more drafts, not to produce the final draft.

What this means practically is that the skill stack for writing has shifted. Producing competent prose is no longer a differentiator. Knowing what you think, recognising when something doesn't sound like you, and judging when prose is actually clear versus when it's just smooth — these are the skills that scale. If you can articulate your thinking precisely enough that AI can draft from it, and you can edit AI output ruthlessly enough that your voice survives, you can produce more, across more formats, at higher consistency, than was previously possible at any cost. If you can't, AI will make you faster at producing forgettable writing that sounds like everyone else.

The discipline this topic builds is editorial judgment at speed. Not writing, not prompting — judgment. The ability to look at a paragraph and know, quickly, whether it's right; whether it sounds like you; whether the argument holds; whether the tone fits the reader. That skill was always the core of good writing. AI just removed every other excuse for not having it.

Worth deeper treatment because the failure mode here is invisible to the writer (AI default voice feels fine in isolation) and only becomes obvious when readers stop responding the way they used to.

Worth depth because the difference between five iterations and fifty is not just speed; it changes which kinds of edits are worth making and how you decide when to stop.

**Format-specific prompting for communication** — Covers why the same prompt structure that works for emails fails for documentation, social posts, or long-form, and how to develop reusable templates per format. Worth depth because format conventions are subtle, easy to violate, and a major source of AI output that's technically correct but contextually wrong.

**The editing frontier** — Covers where AI editing genuinely improves work (clarity, redundancy, structural flow, tonal consistency) versus where it actively makes work worse (conceptual accuracy, domain-specific judgment, reader psychology, intentional choices that look like mistakes). Worth depth because most users either over-trust AI editing or refuse it entirely, and the productive middle requires knowing exactly where the line falls.

**Ghostwriting vs. human-written as strategic choice** — Covers the tradeoff between speed (AI-heavy) and credibility/distinctiveness (human-heavy), and when each genuinely serves your goals versus when you're rationalising convenience. Worth depth because the right answer varies by audience, format, and stakes, and the wrong answer compounds over time as readers learn to recognise AI-heavy output.
