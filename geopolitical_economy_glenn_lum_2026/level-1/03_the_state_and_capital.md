# L1-03 · The State and Capital

The fastest way to misread the global economy is to assume the state and capital are separate things that occasionally interact — the state regulating, taxing, sometimes subsidising; capital operating in a private sphere that the state either protects or constrains. Once you accept that framing, every interesting question becomes invisible. Why do "free market" economies run permanent industrial policy when it suits them? Why has globalisation, which was supposed to dissolve borders, produced a world where state power over trade, currency, and technology has never been more decisive? Why does the most ideologically anti-statist economy on earth — the United States — depend on the most expansive state apparatus to maintain its position? You cannot answer any of these from inside the liberal frame, because the frame itself is what produces the puzzles.

The geopolitical economy tradition starts somewhere else. The state is neither a neutral referee standing above the economic game nor a simple puppet of whoever owns the most factories. It is a contested terrain — an institutional structure that different class forces fight over, capture partially, and shape — and at the same time a relatively autonomous actor with its own interests, particularly its interest in surviving as a state in a world of other states. Hold both of those at once. The state is shaped by class power without being reducible to it. That is the analytical move the tradition asks you to make, and it is the one that unlocks almost everything downstream.

The liberal view fails because it cannot explain pattern. If states were neutral regulators balancing competing interests, you would expect their policies to track median voter preferences or some impartial public good. They don't. Across centuries and across regimes, state action systematically tracks the interests of dominant capital fractions — industrial capital in one era, finance capital in another, with shifts that correspond to which fraction has consolidated power. The crude Marxist view fails for the opposite reason: it cannot explain when states act against the apparent short-term interests of dominant capital. Roosevelt's New Deal, post-war social democracy, even contemporary anti-trust action — these are real, and a theory that calls them all elaborate ruses is not doing analytical work. The tradition's answer is that the state has its own institutional logic. It needs legitimacy, it needs revenue, it needs to manage class conflict so the system reproduces itself, and in the international arena it needs to maintain its position against rival states. Sometimes those imperatives require disciplining capital, particularly the most short-sighted parts of it, in the long-term interest of the system as a whole.

This is where Desai's distinctive contribution lands. Mainstream globalisation theory, in both its liberal and its left variants, predicted that capital would slip the leash of the nation-state. Production would globalise, finance would deterritorialise, and states would become administrative units managing flows they could no longer control. The prediction was wrong, and not by a little. What you actually see is that capitalism has always developed within and through national frameworks, and that the supposed era of borderless globalisation was a specific historical moment — the post-WWII period of US hegemony — that depended on a particular state, the United States, providing the institutional architecture (dollar, military, IMF, WTO) within which transnational capital operated. That was never globalisation transcending the state. It was one state's power projected globally and dressed up as a market outcome.

Once you see that, the current moment becomes legible rather than chaotic. The rise of China is not an anomaly to be explained — it is exactly what the geopolitical economy framework predicts. A state that directs capital, controls its financial system, plans industrial development, and treats currency as a strategic instrument is doing what every successful industrialiser has done, from Hamilton's America to Bismarck's Germany to post-war Japan and Korea. The countries that "globalised" without retaining state capacity over their economies are precisely the ones that did not develop. The pattern is so consistent that the burden of proof should sit on anyone claiming markets-without-states is a real path to industrialisation. There are no examples.

The state's role in money is the sharpest instance of all this, and it is worth pausing on because it underwrites everything Hudson does on dollar hegemony. Money is not a neutral medium that emerged from barter to grease exchange. Money is a creature of the state — issued, denominated, taxed in, and enforced by sovereign power. Once you accept this, monetary policy stops looking like technical management and starts looking like what it is: one of the central instruments by which states shape distribution domestically and project power internationally. Currency hierarchy in the world system is state hierarchy. Dedollarisation is not an economic question that happens to have political consequences; it is a political contest using monetary instruments.

What you should take from this is a habit of reading. When you see a state act, do not ask first whether the action was efficient or whether it served the public interest in some abstract sense. Ask which class fraction's interests it tracks, what institutional imperative of the state it serves, and how it positions the state in the inter-state system. Most of the time, two or three of those will line up, and the action that looked irrational from inside liberal economics will resolve into something coherent. The state is not above capital and it is not beneath it. It is the terrain on which the contest happens, and increasingly — as the unipolar moment ends — the principal actor reshaping the global order from the top down.

## Level 2 candidates

**Relative autonomy of the state** — The theoretical mechanism that explains why states sometimes discipline dominant capital without ceasing to be capitalist states. Worth deeper treatment because this is exactly where the tradition is most often misread, and getting it right is what separates structural analysis from conspiracy thinking.

**The state and currency** — How states create, denominate, and enforce money, and why this destroys the textbook account of currency as a market-emergent medium of exchange. Deserves its own post because it is the conceptual hinge between this topic and the entire dollar hegemony argument that follows.

**Nationalism and the national question** — Why the nation-state has strengthened rather than dissolved under globalisation, and what that means for any theory that treats borders as residual. Worth depth because the empirical refutation of globalisation theory is one of the tradition's strongest cards and the historical record rewards close inspection.

**The developmental state** — The historical pattern that successful industrialisation always involves active state direction of capital, with Japan, South Korea, and China as the clearest cases. A deep dive here is high-leverage because it converts a theoretical claim into an empirical argument that is hard to dismiss.

**State competition and multipolarity** — Why inter-state rivalry is structural to capitalism rather than a pre-modern residue, and how this underpins the analysis of the current transition away from US hegemony. Worth its own treatment because it directly bridges the foundational theory to the contemporary geopolitical analysis in Group 3.
