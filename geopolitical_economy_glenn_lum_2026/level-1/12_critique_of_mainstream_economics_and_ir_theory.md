# L1-12 · Critique of Mainstream Economics and IR Theory

There is a particular kind of frustration you encounter when you try to explain why sanctions, debt crises, or trade wars happen using the tools you were taught in undergraduate economics or international relations. The vocabulary keeps slipping. Markets are supposed to clear, states are supposed to maximise utility, comparative advantage is supposed to make everyone richer — and yet the world produces sanctions regimes that immiserate civilians, debt arrangements that transfer wealth from poor countries to rich ones for decades, and wars that no rational utility-maximiser would start. The mainstream frameworks do not just fail to predict these outcomes; they lack the conceptual equipment to even describe them properly. This is not an accident, and understanding why is the entry point to using geopolitical economy credibly rather than merely as a counter-assertion.

The two orthodoxies you are arguing against are neoclassical economics and liberal IR theory, and they share a structural feature: both treat their object of study as a self-contained system whose dynamics can be understood without reference to power, class, or history. Neoclassical economics models the economy as a system of voluntary exchanges between rational agents, where prices coordinate supply and demand and markets tend toward equilibrium. Liberal IR theory models the international system as a set of rational states pursuing interests in an anarchic environment, with cooperation emerging from interdependence and institutions. In both cases, the analytical move is the same — abstract away from the question of who owns what, who extracts from whom, and how the system you are describing came to exist.

The consequence is that the mechanisms that actually drive the global order become invisible. Neoclassical economics has no theory of why some countries industrialise and others don't, beyond appeals to "institutions" or "human capital" that beg the historical question. It has no theory of money as a political instrument — money is just a neutral medium, central banks are just technical managers. It has no theory of rent extraction distinct from productive earnings, because the classical distinction between earned and unearned income was deliberately erased after the 1890s. Liberal IR theory, for its part, treats states as if they were unitary billiard balls, with no account of which class interests are encoded in their foreign policy, or why "rational" American behaviour so reliably tracks the structural needs of US capital and the dollar system. None of the dominant IR schools — realism, liberalism, constructivism — has a theory of capitalism, which means none can explain the economic drivers of conflict and cooperation that the discipline is ostensibly there to study.

This is where the tradition makes its sharpest claim. These frameworks are not merely incomplete; they are ideologically functional. The blind spots are not random gaps that better data would fill — they are structurally aligned with the interests served by the current order. Comparative advantage, the foundational case for free trade, is a doctrine that powerful states have always selectively applied, using tariffs and industrial policy to develop themselves while imposing free trade on weaker rivals — Britain in the nineteenth century, the United States in the twentieth, while the theory continues to be taught as if its historical record of selective application were a peripheral footnote. The Washington Consensus packaged this same asymmetry as technical advice: privatise, liberalise, cut public spending, open the capital account — a policy bundle whose outcomes for the Global South were predictable and whose function as a wealth-transfer mechanism was never seriously hidden, only theoretically denied.

The professionalisation of economics after the Second World War deepened this. By recasting itself as a technical discipline modelled on physics, with mathematical formalism and the assumption of self-equilibrating markets, economics removed distributional questions — who gets what, and at whose expense — from the domain of legitimate professional debate. Questions about class, rent, power, and historical formation were redefined as ideology or sociology, outside the discipline's competence. What remained was a vocabulary in which extraction looks like exchange, dependency looks like comparative advantage, and monetary coercion looks like prudent central bank policy. You do not need a conspiracy theory to explain why this framework dominates; you need only an institutional account of who funds the universities, the think tanks, and the media outlets that produce and distribute economic and geopolitical analysis. The frameworks that survive that ecosystem are the ones that do not threaten its funders.

What this means practically is that when you deploy the geopolitical economy framework, you are not just adding more variables to the mainstream model — you are working from a different ontology. The mainstream sees a world of voluntary exchanges and rational state actors, with frictions and externalities to be managed. You see a world of class-structured states, surplus extraction through monetary and debt arrangements, and inter-imperial rivalry as a structural feature rather than a deviation. The two pictures are not reconcilable by adding more nuance to either side. This is why arguments across the divide so often feel like people talking past each other: they are operating in incompatible frames, and one of them is institutionally dominant for reasons that have nothing to do with its explanatory power.

The skill this topic builds is the ability to read mainstream analysis without being colonised by it. You will still encounter neoclassical and liberal IR arguments constantly — in the *Financial Times*, in IMF reports, in foreign policy journals, in the assumptions of almost every interlocutor you meet. Knowing what they get wrong is the easy part. Knowing why they get it wrong — that the wrongness is not error but function — is what lets you engage these frameworks without either dismissing them as propaganda or being slowly recolonised by them. Treat them as artefacts of the order they describe, not as neutral tools for describing it.

## Level 2 candidates

**Comparative advantage and free trade ideology** — The Ricardian model and its actual historical application by Britain, the US, and other industrialisers who used protection to develop and free trade to compete. Worth a deep dive because the gap between the theory's claims and the historical record is the cleanest single demonstration of mainstream economics functioning as ideology rather than analysis.

**The Washington Consensus** — The specific privatisation-liberalisation-austerity package imposed on the Global South from the 1980s and the consistent pattern of its outcomes. Worth depth because it is the case study where the ideological function of development economics becomes empirically inescapable, and because its rhetoric persists even after its track record is in.

**Realism, liberalism, and constructivism in IR** — The three dominant IR schools, what they share, and the specific ways each fails to theorise capitalism. Worth depth because most readers' instinctive vocabulary for international politics comes from one of these schools without their realising it, and naming the shared blind spot is what makes the geopolitical economy alternative legible.

**The depoliticisation of economics** — The post-WWII professionalisation of economics as a mathematical, technical discipline and the consequent removal of distributional questions from professional debate. Worth depth because it is the intellectual history that explains why contemporary economic discourse cannot see what classical economists from Smith to Marx took as central.

**Media and think tank ecosystem** — The institutional infrastructure — funding sources, career incentives, publication venues — that shapes which frameworks get amplified and which get marginalised. Worth depth because it provides the structural rather than conspiratorial explanation for orthodox dominance, and because it is the answer to "why don't more economists say this if it's true."

**The neoclassical erasure of rent** — The specific late-nineteenth-century theoretical move by which the classical distinction between earned income and economic rent was collapsed into a single category of "factor income." Worth depth because Hudson treats this as the pivotal intellectual operation that made rentier capitalism invisible, and reconstructing it gives you a concrete example of ideology working through technical-looking theory.
