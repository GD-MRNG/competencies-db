# L1-01 · Historical Materialism

Most accounts of why the world looks the way it does start at the top — with ideas, ideologies, the decisions of statesmen, the clash of civilisations, the spread of values. The Cold War was a contest between freedom and totalitarianism. The British Empire was the export of liberal institutions. The current confrontation with China is a struggle over the rules-based international order. If you read the news this way, you will find a coherent story, but you will not be able to predict anything, and you will repeatedly be surprised by events that, in retrospect, were structurally overdetermined. Historical materialism is the move that flips this picture upside down — and once you have made the flip, it is very hard to read the world the other way again.

The premise is straightforward, even if its implications are not. Before a society can have politics, philosophy, religion, or international relations, it has to feed itself, clothe itself, and reproduce itself materially. How it organises that work — who owns the land, who works it, who controls the tools and the surplus — is not just one feature of the society among many. It is the structural condition that shapes which political institutions are possible, which ideologies feel like common sense, and which international arrangements are stable. Marx's claim is not that ideas don't matter, or that everything reduces to economics. The claim is about direction of determination: the way people materially produce their lives is the ground from which political and ideological forms grow, not the other way around.

This sounds abstract until you apply it. Why did liberal democracy emerge in industrialising Britain rather than in feudal Russia? Not because the British had better ideas about freedom, but because a particular configuration of capitalist production — wage labour, urban concentration, industrial bourgeoisie with interests against the landed aristocracy — generated the political forms that protected its conditions of existence. Why does the United States, which describes itself as a free-market democracy, run a permanent war economy and a vast prison system? Because those institutions are not aberrations from its political-economic structure but expressions of it. Why does the IMF, staffed by trained economists who genuinely believe in development, consistently produce policies that immiserate the countries it lends to? Because the institution sits at a particular position in a particular global structure of capital, and the technical economics it produces is shaped, downstream, by that position. You do not need to assume bad faith. You need to understand structure.

The framework rests on a few interlocking moves. The mode of production is the way a society organises labour, ownership, and the extraction of surplus — feudalism, capitalism, the various transitional forms. The base-and-superstructure metaphor names the relationship between those productive arrangements and the legal, political, and ideological forms that grow out of them and stabilise them. Class names the groups defined by their position in the productive system — those who own the means of production and those who must sell their labour to live — and class struggle names the ongoing contest between them, which is the engine that drives historical change. Ideology names the systematic way that the worldview generated by a particular mode of production tends to make that mode of production look natural, eternal, and beyond political contestation.

What makes historical materialism a method rather than just a doctrine is that it gives you a procedure. When you encounter a political event, an institutional decision, or a piece of widely-accepted economic common sense, you ask: what material interests does this serve, what class configuration produced it, and what does it obscure? You do not assume that the stated reasons are the real reasons, and you do not assume the actors are lying. People generally believe the ideologies that align with their material position. The free-trade economist genuinely believes in free trade. The central banker genuinely believes in the independence of monetary policy. The question is not whether they are sincere but whose interests are served by the framework they sincerely hold.

The geopolitical economy tradition takes this method and pushes it onto terrain that orthodox Marxism has historically underweighted: the nation-state and the international system. The classical Marxist tendency was to treat the nation-state as a transitional form that capitalism would dissolve as it globalised. Desai's argument — and it is the thing that makes geopolitical economy distinct from both mainstream IR and from globalisation theory — is that this prediction was always wrong, because capitalism has always been organised through national frameworks, and inter-state competition is constitutive of how the global capitalist order actually works. Historical materialism, applied properly, does not predict the withering of the state. It predicts the persistence of inter-state rivalry as the structural form through which capitalist competition expresses itself at the global scale.

This is why the framework matters as the foundation for everything downstream. The analysis of imperialism is unintelligible without it: imperialism is not a moral failing or a policy choice but a structural tendency of mature capitalism, derivable from how surplus, capital, and class interact in capitalist nation-states. Dollar hegemony is unintelligible without it: the dollar's role is not a market outcome but the institutional expression of a particular configuration of class power and inter-state hierarchy. The current multipolar transition is unintelligible without it: it is not a story about Chinese ambition or American decline as personality traits, but about the material conditions under which the post-WWII US-led order was viable and the material conditions that are now eroding its viability.

The skill historical materialism builds is the capacity to read political and economic phenomena as expressions of underlying material structures rather than as the surface story their participants tell about them. You will find, once the habit sets in, that most mainstream commentary becomes legible as ideology in something close to the technical sense — not lies, but accounts that systematically describe the world from the standpoint of the interests they serve. That is the working mental model. Everything else in this tradition is built on top of it.

## Level 2 candidates

**The base and superstructure** — The relationship between the material conditions of production and the legal, political, and ideological forms that grow from them. Worth deeper treatment because the metaphor is routinely caricatured (as crude economic determinism) when its actual operation is more subtle, and getting the relationship right is what separates usable historical materialism from vulgar Marxism.

**The mode of production** — The conceptual unit that lets you periodise history into qualitatively distinct epochs (feudalism, capitalism, the transitional forms in between) rather than treating each era as a unique configuration. Worth a deep dive because it is the framework that makes comparative historical analysis possible — and because debates about the character of the contemporary mode of production (is it still industrial capitalism? rentier capitalism? something else?) are live and consequential.

**Class and class struggle** — The mechanism by which historical materialism explains change rather than just describing structure. Worth deeper treatment because the standard objections to Marxism (the working class hasn't behaved as predicted, identity has displaced class, etc.) all turn on what the concept of class actually entails, and getting it right matters for analysing why the state takes the forms it does.

**Ideology and false consciousness** — The account of how systems of belief that serve dominant interests come to feel like neutral common sense to populations whose material interests they harm. Worth deeper treatment because this is the concept that links historical materialism to media analysis, the sociology of knowledge, and the critique of mainstream economics — and because the version that survives serious scrutiny is more sophisticated than the cartoon "false consciousness" caricature.

**Combined and uneven development** — Trotsky's extension of Marx, which explains why capitalism does not produce convergence between nations but permanently reproduces hierarchy, with peripheral nations integrating advanced and archaic forms simultaneously. Worth a deep dive because it is the theoretical bridge from classical historical materialism to dependency theory, world-systems theory, and the geopolitical economy account of why the global system is structurally hierarchical rather than tending toward equilibrium.
