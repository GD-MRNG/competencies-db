# L1-09 · China and the Challenge to Western Hegemony

The standard Western framing of China's rise treats it as an anomaly demanding explanation: how did an authoritarian state with the wrong institutions, the wrong property regime, and the wrong relationship between politics and markets produce four decades of growth that the Washington Consensus said was impossible? The geopolitical economy framework reverses the question. China's trajectory is not the puzzle. The puzzle is why anyone expected a different outcome, given that China has been doing more or less what every successful industrialiser has done — and what the Washington Consensus told the Global South never to do.

Start with the historical pattern. Britain industrialised behind tariff walls, navigation acts, and a state-backed monopoly trading company. The United States in the nineteenth century ran some of the highest industrial tariffs in the world and stole British technology with state encouragement. Germany under Bismarck, Japan in the Meiji period, South Korea under Park Chung-hee — every case of catch-up industrialisation involved active state direction of capital, protection of infant industries, suppression of consumption in favour of investment, and tight control over the financial system. Free trade and capital account liberalisation are what successful industrialisers impose on others once they have arrived, not the road they took to get there. China's distinctiveness is not its model. Its distinctiveness is that it is doing this at the scale of one-fifth of humanity and refusing to defer to the financial architecture that has policed every previous developmental project.

The institutional details matter because they are the load-bearing parts. China's banks are predominantly state-owned and lend on the basis of policy priorities rather than purely on shareholder returns. Strategic sectors are organised through state-owned enterprises that can absorb losses, take long horizons, and coordinate across firms in ways private capital cannot. The capital account remains controlled, which means Chinese savings are deployed inside China rather than recycled into US Treasury markets, and which means speculative capital cannot mount the kind of currency attacks that disciplined the Asian Tigers in 1997. Industrial policy — explicit, planned, with measurable targets — directs investment into sectors the state deems strategic. None of this is exotic. It is the standard developmental state toolkit. What is exotic, from the perspective of mainstream economics, is that it works, because mainstream economics has spent a century insisting it cannot.

The challenge to Western hegemony begins to come into focus once you see this. China is not just growing. It is building, deliberately, the institutional architecture of a parallel economic order. The Belt and Road Initiative exports surplus Chinese capital and construction capacity into Eurasia, Africa, and Latin America in a form — infrastructure financing tied to Chinese contractors — that resembles the imperial capital export Lenin described, except that the recipient states are not colonies and the terms, by IMF standards, are remarkably soft. Yuan-denominated trade settlement, swap lines with central banks across the Global South, the Cross-Border Interbank Payment System as an alternative to SWIFT, and the gradual pricing of oil and other commodities outside the dollar all chip away at the recycling mechanism that sustains dollar hegemony. None of these initiatives, individually, dethrones the dollar. Cumulatively, they constitute the first serious institutional alternative since Bretton Woods.

The Western response has clarified the structural logic considerably. Export controls on advanced semiconductors, sanctions on Chinese technology firms, the attempted decoupling of supply chains, the framing of TikTok and Huawei as national security threats — these are not the actions of a confident hegemon enforcing rules of fair competition. They are the actions of a declining power attempting to arrest a competitor's development trajectory by denying it access to inputs the hegemon controls. The geopolitical economy reading is that this strategy is likely to fail on its own terms and accelerate what it aims to prevent. Cut off from advanced Western chips, China is investing at extraordinary scale in domestic semiconductor capacity. Excluded from dollar-denominated finance for sanctioned trades, Russia and China have built parallel rails. Each act of denial demonstrates to non-Western states that dollar dependence and supply-chain dependence on the US are political vulnerabilities, not neutral facts of life.

This is where the deepening Sino-Russian alignment becomes legible as structure rather than coincidence. Two states that the geopolitical economy framework would predict to align — a continental industrial power with surplus capital and a resource-rich power with surplus energy, both targeted by US sanctions, both excluded from the financial architecture they did not design — have done exactly that. The relationship is not ideological. It is the kind of coalition that the framework expects when the costs of the existing order outweigh the costs of building alternatives. Read this way, the BRICS expansion, the Saudi acceptance of yuan settlement for some oil sales, and the broad Global South refusal to enforce Western sanctions on Russia are not separate events. They are the same event: the emergence of an exit option from a system that for seventy years had none.

What you should take from this is that "the China challenge" is not really about China. It is about what happens when the developmental state model — long denied to the Global South under the cover of free trade ideology — is executed at sufficient scale that the hegemon's tools of denial stop working. Whether China itself becomes the centre of a new order, or whether its rise is the catalyst for a more genuinely multipolar one, is contested even within the tradition. What is not contested is that the post-1991 unipolar moment is over, and that the framework you need to read what comes next is not great-power competition theory or democracy-versus-autocracy moralism. It is the framework that treats state-directed industrialisation, monetary sovereignty, and capital control as the historically normal route to development, and dollar hegemony as the historically anomalous obstacle that is now being routed around.

## Level 2 candidates

**The Chinese developmental state model** — The specific institutional architecture (state-owned banks, SOEs, capital controls, Five-Year Plans, industrial policy targeting) that distinguishes China's political economy from both Western capitalism and Soviet-style planning. Worth deeper treatment because the precise mechanics — how state banks discipline SOEs, how local governments compete on industrial outcomes, how capital controls coexist with extensive trade integration — are where the model's actual functioning lives, and where most Western analysis goes wrong.

**Belt and Road Initiative as geopolitical economy** — How BRI functions simultaneously as surplus capital export, infrastructure diplomacy, and dollar-alternative construction, and how its terms compare empirically with IMF and World Bank lending. Worth a deep dive because the "debt trap diplomacy" narrative is the dominant Western frame and collapses on close examination — and the comparison reveals the structure of creditor power more clearly than abstract theory does.

**The yuan internationalisation project** — The concrete mechanisms by which China is building dollar-alternative financial infrastructure: CIPS, central bank swap lines, yuan-denominated commodity contracts, the digital yuan. Worth deeper treatment because the pace and ceiling of dedollarisation is the empirical question on which the multipolar argument lives or dies, and the answer is more nuanced than either dollar-triumphalist or dollar-collapse narratives admit.

**The technology war** — US export controls on semiconductors, AI hardware, and advanced manufacturing equipment, and the Chinese response. Worth going deeper because this is the front line where the structural logic of declining-hegemon denial meets developmental-state response in real time, and the outcomes over the next decade will test the framework's predictions directly.

**Sino-Russian alignment** — The deepening economic, energy, and strategic relationship between China and Russia, and what it reveals about coalition formation under hegemonic pressure. Worth a deep dive because the relationship is structurally over-determined by the framework but empirically constrained in ways (historical mistrust, asymmetric dependence, divergent long-term interests) that the headline-level "axis" framing obscures.

**China and the labour question** — The internal class dynamics of Chinese development: the role of migrant labour, the suppression of independent unions, the rural-urban divide, and the relationship between the Communist Party and Chinese capital. Worth treating separately because the geopolitical economy framework can otherwise read as uncritically celebratory of the Chinese state, and the internal contradictions of the model are part of any honest assessment of its trajectory.
