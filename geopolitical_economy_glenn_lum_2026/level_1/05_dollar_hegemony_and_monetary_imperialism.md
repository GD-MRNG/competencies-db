# L1-05 · Dollar Hegemony and Monetary Imperialism

The standard story about the US dollar is that it became the world's reserve currency because it was the most useful one — deep markets, stable institutions, the natural choice of traders and central banks acting in their own interest. This is wrong in a specific and consequential way. The dollar's global role is not what markets settled on; it is what was constructed, and what is now enforced. Once you see the construction, you stop reading US foreign policy as a series of decisions and start reading it as the maintenance of a particular monetary architecture — one that allows the United States to consume more than it produces, spend more than it taxes, and project military power on terms no other state has access to.

Michael Hudson named this arrangement super imperialism, and the name is precise. Classical imperialism, in Lenin's account, exported capital from the core to the periphery and extracted surplus through ownership of foreign productive assets. Super imperialism inverts the direction of capital flow. The United States runs persistent trade and fiscal deficits; the rest of the world ships it real goods and accepts dollars in return; those dollars then return to the United States as purchases of Treasury bonds. The surplus-producing countries — historically Germany and Japan, today China and the Gulf states — end up financing the very military and consumption apparatus that structures the system they are subordinate to. They are not being invested in. They are funding the centre.

To see how this works, you have to understand that the dollar is not just a currency; it is the unit in which the most important commodities (oil above all), the largest debts, and the deepest financial markets are denominated. A country that wants to import oil needs dollars. A country whose banks want to participate in international trade needs access to the dollar clearing system. A country that accumulates foreign exchange reserves has very few alternatives to holding them as US government debt — there is no other market large enough or liquid enough. This is not a preference. It is a structural compulsion that the architecture itself produces.

The architecture was built in two stages. The first was the 1944 Bretton Woods settlement, which made the dollar the only currency convertible to gold at a fixed rate and tied every other currency to the dollar. This worked as long as US gold reserves were credible relative to outstanding dollar claims, and by the late 1960s — after Vietnam War spending and Great Society programmes — they no longer were. The second stage is the part that confuses people who think monetary power requires hard backing. In 1971 Nixon unilaterally suspended dollar-gold convertibility, defaulting on the foreign claims against US gold. The expectation at the time was that this would end dollar hegemony. Instead, it strengthened it. Freed from the constraint of gold, the United States could now issue dollars without limit, and the rest of the world, having no organised alternative, kept holding them. The OPEC agreement to price oil exclusively in dollars locked the arrangement in. The dollar became a pure fiat reserve, backed by nothing except the demand created by the structures that required it.

This is where the system stops looking economic and starts looking coercive. Because the dollar is the medium of international settlement, control of dollar clearing is control of access to the world economy. The United States can — and increasingly does — exclude states from SWIFT, freeze their central bank reserves, and impose secondary sanctions that force third-country firms to choose between trading with a sanctioned country and trading in dollars. When the US froze around $300 billion of Russian central bank reserves in 2022, it demonstrated to every other state holding dollar reserves what those reserves actually are: not property, but conditional permission. The lesson was not lost on Beijing, Riyadh, or Brasília.

This is the contradiction the system has now arrived at. The methods used to enforce dollar hegemony — sanctions, asset seizures, exclusion from clearing — are precisely the methods that teach other states why dollar dependence is dangerous. Every sanctions regime accelerates the construction of alternatives. The BRICS payment systems, China's CIPS, bilateral currency agreements between trade partners, commodity-backed settlement experiments, the slow but real push to price oil in yuan — these are not utopian gestures. They are security responses. The states building them have concrete material reasons, drawn from observed cases, to want exits from a system whose costs they have either paid directly or watched others pay. The question of whether dedollarisation succeeds is not a question of whether the dollar is technically replaceable tomorrow (it is not) but of whether the structural compulsion that holds the system together is being eroded faster than it can be reinforced.

What this framework gives you is a way of reading contemporary events that mainstream economic commentary cannot. When the United States imposes sanctions, you are not watching policy; you are watching the enforcement of a monetary system that requires enforcement to persist. When deficit spending continues at scales that would collapse any other currency, you are not watching profligacy; you are watching the unique privilege the system grants the issuer of the world's reserve currency — what the French in the 1960s called the exorbitant privilege. When countries that have nothing in common politically (Saudi Arabia, Iran, Russia, Brazil) start aligning on payment infrastructure, you are not watching an anti-American conspiracy; you are watching states acting on the same structural lesson. Dollar hegemony is the connective tissue of the post-1945 order. Understanding it is what lets you see why the order is fraying, and why the fraying cannot be reversed by the tools the centre still has available.

## Level 2 candidates

**Bretton Woods and the gold-dollar standard** — The 1944 settlement that institutionalised dollar centrality and tied every other currency to it through fixed convertibility. Worth deeper treatment because the negotiation itself (Keynes's bancor proposal vs. White's dollar-centred plan) reveals that the dollar order was a chosen political construction, not a market discovery, and the alternatives that were rejected illuminate what a different architecture could have looked like.

**The Nixon shock (1971)** — The unilateral suspension of dollar-gold convertibility and its counterintuitive consequence: detaching the dollar from gold strengthened rather than weakened US monetary power. Worth deeper treatment because this is where most readers' intuitions about money break, and getting it right is the test of whether you actually understand fiat reserve dynamics.

**Treasury bond recycling** — The mechanism by which trade surplus nations are structurally compelled to hold US debt, converting their export earnings into financing for US deficits. Worth deeper treatment because this is the specific accounting relationship at the heart of Hudson's super imperialism thesis, and walking through the flows with concrete cases (Japan in the 1980s, China in the 2000s, the Gulf today) makes the abstraction operational.

**Sanctions as monetary weapon** — How control of dollar clearing infrastructure (SWIFT, correspondent banking, OFAC) functions as an instrument of coercion that can be applied unilaterally and extraterritorially. Worth deeper treatment because the empirical record of sanctions regimes — who they target, what they actually do to civilian populations, and how they have evolved since 2014 — is where the abstract claim about coercion becomes concrete.

**Dedollarisation and the multipolar monetary order** — What the BRICS payment systems, bilateral currency agreements, and commodity-backed alternatives reveal about the structural limits of dollar hegemony and the realistic shape of any successor order. Worth deeper treatment because this is where the analysis meets the present, and distinguishing what is happening, what is theatre, and what is structurally constrained is harder than either dollar-collapse or dollar-forever narratives suggest.

**The exorbitant privilege** — The specific economic benefits the United States derives from issuing the world's reserve currency, including the ability to run persistent deficits without the discipline imposed on other states. Worth deeper treatment because quantifying the privilege (seigniorage, lower borrowing costs, sanctions reach) and tracing how it has shaped US domestic political economy connects monetary imperialism to the financialisation and deindustrialisation analyses elsewhere in the tradition.
