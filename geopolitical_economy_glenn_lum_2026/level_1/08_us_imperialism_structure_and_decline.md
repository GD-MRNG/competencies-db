# L1-08 · US Imperialism: Structure and Decline

The temptation, when watching US foreign policy in the 2020s, is to reach for the language of irrationality. Sanctions that fail to bend their targets and instead push them into each other's arms. Military commitments that drain the treasury without securing the objectives they were launched for. A financial weaponisation strategy that has done more to motivate dedollarisation than any rival currency project could have managed on its own. Read as intentions, this looks like incompetence or hubris. Read structurally, it looks like something else: a declining hegemon defending privileges it can no longer afford to lose, using the only tools it still has, and accelerating the fragmentation it is trying to prevent.

That structural read is what the geopolitical economy tradition gives you. The argument is not that American policymakers are stupid, nor that they are uniquely cruel, nor that the empire is run by a coherent cabal. The argument is that the United States occupies a specific position in the world system — the issuer of the global reserve currency, the operator of the dominant military alliance system, the architect of the postwar institutional order — and that position generates a set of imperatives that any administration, Democratic or Republican, hawkish or restrained, will find itself executing. The continuity across administrations is the evidence. When the policy doesn't change with the personnel, the policy is not about the personnel.

What does the structural position actually consist of? Four interlocking instruments, each examined in more depth elsewhere in this course but worth seeing together here. First, military supremacy: roughly 750 overseas bases, a defence budget larger than the next ten nations combined, and a force posture organised around the capacity to project power into any region on the planet. Second, dollar dominance: the privilege of issuing the currency in which most international trade and reserves are denominated, which lets the US run permanent deficits that other countries would be punished for and weaponise the financial plumbing through which everyone else transacts. Third, the sanctions apparatus: the legal and technical machinery (OFAC, SWIFT access, secondary sanctions, correspondent banking control) that turns dollar dominance into a coercive instrument applicable to any state, firm, or individual the US designates. Fourth, institutional control: the IMF, World Bank, NATO, and the network of think tanks, ratings agencies, and media organisations that translate American preferences into apparently neutral global rules.

The trick of the postwar order — the thing that made it work for as long as it did — was that these instruments mostly stayed below the surface. Hegemony, when it is functioning, looks like consensus. Allies hold dollars because dollars are useful, not because they are coerced. The IMF imposes structural adjustment because the conditions are technically sound, not because the institution is an instrument of US capital. NATO expands because new members want to join, not because expansion serves American strategic interests. The ideological work of hegemony is to make these arrangements appear voluntary and natural, so that the underlying coercion need not be exercised visibly.

What you are watching now is the breakdown of that ideological cover. As US relative economic weight declines — China's GDP at purchasing power parity has exceeded America's since 2017, and the BRICS bloc now produces more than the G7 — the gap between American structural privileges and American productive capacity widens. Defending the privileges therefore requires more visible coercion: more sanctions, more secondary sanctions, more financial weaponisation, more explicit threats. But each act of visible coercion teaches the rest of the world that the system is coercive, that dollar holdings are politically conditional, and that exit options are worth building. The freezing of Russian central bank reserves in 2022 was the clearest example: a single act of financial warfare that did more to legitimise alternative payment systems among non-Western central banks than two decades of Chinese diplomacy had achieved. This is what Paul Kennedy called imperial overstretch in a different register — the cost of maintaining hegemony beginning to exceed the surplus it generates.

The contradiction sharpens because the domestic political economy cannot easily adjust. Permanent military expenditure is not an external policy choice; it is woven into the industrial base of entire congressional districts, and it functions as a politically untouchable form of Keynesian stimulus that no other category of public spending can replace. The financial sector's capture of policy means dedollarisation cannot be pre-empted by reforming the dollar's extractive functions, because those functions are precisely what generates the rents the sector lives on. The think tank and media ecosystem cannot pivot to a multipolar framing because its funding and prestige are tied to the assumption of American indispensability. Each component of the imperial apparatus has constituencies that depend on it, and those constituencies have veto power within the American political system. The empire cannot retrench gracefully because the institutions that would have to authorise the retrenchment are the ones that would lose from it.

This is why the tradition predicts not a managed transition but an increasingly erratic defence. Sanctions will be applied more widely and less effectively. Military commitments will multiply faster than the capacity to honour them. Financial weaponisation will accelerate the construction of alternatives. The rhetorical framing — "rules-based international order," "democracy versus autocracy," "great power competition" — will grow more strident as the underlying material position weakens. None of this is irrational from inside the structure. It is exactly what a system optimised for hegemonic extraction does when extraction becomes harder.

The skill this topic builds is the ability to read US foreign policy as the output of a structural position rather than the product of intentions. When the next sanctions package is announced, the next military deployment, the next institutional intervention, you are looking for the privilege being defended, the constituency being served, and the contradiction being deepened. The headlines will give you the justification. The framework gives you the function.

## Level 2 candidates

**The military-industrial complex as economic system** — How permanent war expenditure functions as a structural component of US domestic political economy rather than a separable foreign policy choice, including its role as untouchable Keynesian stimulus. Worth depth because it explains why even administrations elected on anti-war platforms cannot meaningfully reduce military spending — the constraint is internal, not external.

**NATO as imperial instrument** — The argument that NATO operates as the military enforcement arm of US-centred order rather than a defensive alliance, and the contradictions of its post-Cold War expansion. Worth depth because the gap between NATO's stated rationale and its actual function is one of the clearest cases of hegemonic ideology obscuring structural purpose, and because Ukraine has made the analysis empirically tractable.

**Sanctions as imperial tool and self-harm** — How secondary sanctions and dollar clearing control function as extraterritorial enforcement of US law, and why their overuse generates the dedollarisation it is meant to prevent. Worth depth because this is the cleanest live example of the contradiction at the heart of the topic — the instrument of hegemony actively dismantling its own foundations.

**Colour revolutions and regime change** — The pattern of US-backed political interventions framed as democracy promotion, from Cold War interventions through to contemporary cases. Worth depth because the mechanism reveals why liberal democracy discourse functions as imperial ideology rather than analytical category, and because tracing specific cases gives you a calibrated sense of what the pattern actually consists of versus what conspiracy theorising imagines.

**Imperial overstretch** — Paul Kennedy's framework, extended through the geopolitical economy tradition, that the cost of maintaining hegemony eventually exceeds the surplus it extracts. Worth depth because it provides the theoretical vocabulary for the structural dynamic underlying contemporary US fiscal and political dysfunction, and because the historical comparisons (Britain, Spain, the Dutch) sharpen the framework's predictive content.
