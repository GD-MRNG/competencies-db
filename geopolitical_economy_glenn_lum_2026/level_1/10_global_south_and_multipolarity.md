# L1-10 · The Global South and Multipolarity

The standard Western framing of the Global South in international politics is that these states are objects, not subjects — recipients of aid, sites of crisis, pawns to be pulled toward Washington or Beijing. When Brazil refuses to sanction Russia, when South Africa hosts naval exercises with China, when Saudi Arabia agrees to settle oil sales in yuan, the reflexive analysis is to ask which great power is pulling the strings. This framing is not just analytically wrong; it is constitutive of the problem it claims to describe. The Global South is not being maneuvered into a multipolar order. It is building one, for reasons rooted in concrete historical experience, and the inability to see this is one of the most consequential blind spots in contemporary Western analysis.

The starting point is that "the Global South" is not a geographic category but a political one — a name for the states that experienced the costs of the post-1945 US-centred order from the receiving end. They are the states that took IMF structural adjustment programmes and saw their public sectors gutted and their currencies devalued. They are the states whose dollar reserves became leverage against them the moment they stepped out of line. They are the states that watched their commodity prices set in markets they did not control, their development trajectories interrupted by externally backed coups, their assets frozen by sanctions whose legality rested on nothing but US willingness to enforce them. When you have lived through this, the proposition that the dollar-based order is neutral infrastructure is not a theoretical disagreement — it is contradicted by your own balance sheet.

What this means is that the multipolar project is driven by interest, not ideology. This is the analytical move Desai and Norton both insist on: the states moving toward BRICS, toward bilateral currency agreements, toward alternative payment systems, are not doing so because they have been ideologically captured by Beijing or Moscow. They are doing so because dollar hegemony has demonstrable, quantifiable costs to them, and because sanctions on Russia in 2022 made those costs newly visible. If the United States can freeze Russia's central bank reserves, it can freeze yours. The rational response is to hold fewer dollar reserves, settle more trade in other currencies, and build institutional alternatives before you need them. This is risk management, not romance.

You can see this logic operating across multiple registers simultaneously. BRICS expansion in 2023–24 brought in Saudi Arabia, Iran, the UAE, Egypt, Ethiopia, and Argentina (with subsequent applications and reversals) — a coalition whose members do not share an ideology, a political system, or even consistent regional alignments, but who do share a structural position with respect to the dollar system. The African Union's pursuit of monetary alternatives to the CFA franc reflects the same logic at a regional level: monetary sovereignty is a precondition for any developmental project, because you cannot pursue an industrial policy that contradicts your reserve currency's interests. The Latin American left governments — Lula, Petro, Boric, AMLO — each face the same destabilisation pressures that previous waves did, and their attempts to build regional financial architecture (a common South American currency, expanded use of the New Development Bank) are responses to a constraint they have already encountered.

The cleanest evidence that this is structural rather than ideological is the pattern of Global South abstention on Western sanctions regimes against Russia. The states that refused to comply were not the ones with the deepest ideological alignment with Moscow. They were the ones with the most acute experience of being on the wrong side of dollar weaponisation themselves, or with the most concrete trade dependencies that sanctions compliance would damage. When India buys discounted Russian oil and re-exports refined products, this is not ideology. It is the same calculus any state would make if it had the option, and the construction of the multipolar order is partly the project of giving more states that option.

The Western misreading of this dynamic is itself analytically interesting, because it is so consistent. Every Global South state that takes a position contrary to Western preferences is described as falling under Chinese or Russian influence, even when the policy in question predates the supposed influence and serves the state's own evident interests. This is not a series of analytical mistakes — it is the structural inability of an imperial framework to recognise its objects as agents. If you assume the natural state of the Global South is alignment with the West, then any deviation requires an external explanation. The geopolitical economy framework reverses this: the natural state of states is to pursue their own developmental and security interests, and what requires explanation is the period during which the dollar-based order successfully suppressed that pursuit.

What you should take from this is a habit of reading. When you encounter coverage of BRICS, of dedollarisation, of Global South positions on Ukraine or Gaza, the first question is not "whose side are they on" but "what does the structural position of this state predict, and does the policy reflect that?" Most of the time, the answer is that it does, and the framing of "Chinese influence" or "Russian disinformation" is doing the work of obscuring a more uncomfortable truth: that the order the West built has costs which its beneficiaries are no longer willing to absorb, and that this is happening because the costs are real, not because anyone is being deceived about them.

The deeper implication is that multipolarity is not an outcome that great powers are bringing about. It is the cumulative effect of many states, each acting on their own interests, finding that those interests increasingly point in the same direction. That convergence is the news.

## Level 2 candidates

**BRICS and its expansion** — The institutional capacity, internal contradictions, and concrete outputs (New Development Bank, contingency reserve arrangement, payment system work) of the bloc as it scales. Worth a deep dive because the gap between BRICS as symbol and BRICS as functional institution is where most of the substantive analysis lives, and where both triumphalist and dismissive readings tend to fail.

**Africa's monetary sovereignty project** — The CFA franc system, the African Union's single currency proposals, and the broader push to settle intra-African trade outside the dollar and euro. Worth depth because Africa is the clearest test case for the tradition's claim that monetary sovereignty is the precondition for developmental sovereignty, and because the colonial monetary architecture is unusually legible there.

**Latin American pink tide and its contradictions** — The successive waves of left governments, the structural constraints they face operating within dollar hegemony, and the destabilisation mechanisms (lawfare, sanctions, currency pressure, media campaigns) that have repeatedly interrupted developmental projects. Worth depth because Latin America is where the limits of national-developmental politics within a dollar order are most empirically documented.

**The Non-Aligned Movement as precedent** — The 1955–1970s attempt by newly decolonised states to build a third pole outside the Cold War, why it ultimately failed, and what those failure modes tell you about what the current multipolar project would need to avoid. Worth depth because the historical comparison disciplines the analysis — without it, contemporary multipolarity claims float free of the precedents that explain the structural difficulties.

**Commodity power and the resource curse** — Why resource-rich Global South states occupy a contradictory position: nominally powerful in markets for scarce commodities, but historically subject to price manipulation, sanctions, and political intervention that neutralise that power. Worth depth because the energy transition and the contest over critical minerals make this dynamic newly central, and because it explains why commodity exporters (Saudi Arabia, the Gulf, Indonesia, parts of Africa) are now politically pivotal in ways the older framing missed.

**The Western misreading as analytical artefact** — The structural reasons why mainstream Western analysis cannot see Global South states as agents, treating every deviation as foreign influence. Worth depth because understanding the production of this blind spot — in media, think tanks, and IR theory — is what lets you read coverage critically rather than just rejecting it, and connects directly to the L1-12 critique of mainstream frameworks.
