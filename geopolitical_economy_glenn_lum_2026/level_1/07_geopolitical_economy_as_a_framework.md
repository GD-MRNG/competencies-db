# L1-07 · Geopolitical Economy as a Framework

There is a particular intellectual move that mainstream economics and mainstream international relations both make, and once you see it, you cannot unsee it. Economics treats markets as if they exist in a space prior to and independent of states — states are external interventions, distortions, or at best regulators. International relations treats states as if they exist in a space prior to and independent of economic structure — capitalism is background scenery, not a driver of state behaviour. The two disciplines reinforce each other by drawing the boundary in the same place: economics on one side, geopolitics on the other, and a careful silence about how each constitutes the other. Geopolitical economy is the framework that refuses this division.

The name comes from Radhika Desai's *Geopolitical Economy* (2013), and the choice of name is deliberate. It is not "international political economy," which still treats the international as a separate domain layered on top of national economies. It is not "global political economy," which presumes the global has already superseded the national. It is geopolitical economy because the central claim is that capitalism has always been organised through nation-states and that inter-state rivalry has always been constitutive of how the world economy actually works. The state is not outside capitalism. Capitalism does not float above states. They produce each other.

This means the standard story you have been told about globalisation is upside down. In that story, the postwar decades were a transitional period in which national economies gradually dissolved into a single integrated global market, with the state withering into a regulator of flows it could no longer direct. The 2008 crisis, the rise of China, Brexit, Trump, the return of industrial policy, sanctions wars — all of these appear, in the mainstream account, as a "crisis of globalisation," a worrying retreat from the natural endpoint of economic integration. Geopolitical economy inverts this entirely. The era of US-led globalisation was not the natural condition of capitalism reaching maturity. It was a historical anomaly, made possible by the specific conditions of post-WWII US hegemony — overwhelming productive supremacy, the dollar's reserve status, and the Cold War alignment that locked allied capital into a single bloc. What you are watching now is not a crisis. It is the normal condition of capitalist inter-state competition reasserting itself as those specific conditions erode.

That reframing has direct consequences for how you read the news. When mainstream commentators describe China's industrial policy, the Belt and Road Initiative, BRICS expansion, or yuan-denominated trade as departures from the rules-based order, geopolitical economy reads them as the predictable behaviour of capitalist states pursuing developmental and strategic interests — exactly what every successful industrialiser, including Britain and the United States, did during their own ascents. When commentators describe US sanctions, NATO expansion, or industrial policy as defensive responses to disorder, geopolitical economy reads them as the equally predictable behaviour of a declining hegemon defending the structural privileges that its own historical moment produced. The frameworks that make one set of actions look natural and the other look anomalous are themselves political artefacts of the period they describe.

The components of the framework follow from this core move. Capitalism has nationality — capital accumulates within and through national institutions (legal systems, central banks, militaries, education systems, currencies), and the idea of stateless capital is mostly an ideological projection of one particular phase of US-led order. Hegemony is partial and conditional — the postwar US position rested on a stack of historically specific advantages, and there is no theoretical reason to expect any state to reproduce them. Multipolarity is not a political project but a structural tendency — capitalist development in non-Western states produces new centres of accumulation that eventually demand institutional expression, regardless of whether anyone in Washington approves. And "the West" itself is a Cold War political construct, not a civilisational unit, which is why its coherence is now visibly fracturing along the lines of actual material interest.

What geopolitical economy gives you, then, is an architecture rather than a position. It is the connective tissue between historical materialism (which tells you how to read society), classical imperialism theory (which tells you why capitalism expands beyond its borders), and the monetary and financial analysis of Hudson and others (which tells you how the current order extracts and disciplines). Without the architecture, those pieces sit next to each other; with it, they form a single explanatory system. You can take a contemporary event — a sanctions package, an IMF programme, a military deployment, a trade agreement, a currency swap — and locate it within a structure that tells you what it is doing and why, rather than relying on the actor's own description of its motives.

The skill this builds is the ability to read the international order without taking the order's self-description at face value. Mainstream frameworks are not simply wrong; they are functional. They make certain mechanisms of extraction and coercion difficult to see, and they make certain political possibilities difficult to think. Geopolitical economy puts those mechanisms back in view. Once you have the architecture, the apparent chaos of the present moment — the simultaneous decline of US power, the rise of multipolar coalitions, the weaponisation of the dollar, the return of industrial policy across the West, the realignment of the Global South — stops looking like chaos. It starts looking like a structural transition you can actually track.

## Level 2 candidates

**Capitalism and nationality** — The argument that capital has always accumulated within and through national institutions rather than dissolving them, against the dominant globalisation narrative of the 1990s and 2000s. Worth a deeper dive because it is the load-bearing claim of the entire framework, and the empirical case (national legal systems, central banks, militaries, currencies as the actual infrastructure of capital) repays close examination.

**Hegemony and its limits** — Why the post-WWII US position was always a contingent stack of historically specific advantages rather than a stable equilibrium, and what this implies about the trajectory of any hegemonic system. Going deeper here is what separates a serious analysis of US decline from the recurring "America is finished" rhetoric that has been wrong for decades.

**The multipolar transition** — What the current evidence actually shows about the emergence of new centres of power (China, Russia, Global South coalitions) and what the framework predicts about how the transition will unfold institutionally and monetarily. Worth depth because this is where the framework is being tested in real time and where overreach (predicting a cleaner break than the evidence supports) is a real risk.

**The West as a political construct** — Desai and Norton's argument that "the West" is a Cold War political category rather than a geographic or civilisational reality, and why its apparent coherence is now actively fracturing along material-interest lines. Worth a deep dive because the concept is doing a lot of unexamined work in mainstream commentary, and unpacking it sharpens your ability to read intra-Western divergence (Germany, France, Japan, the Gulf) accurately.

**Unequal exchange** — Arghiri Emmanuel's and Samir Amin's theory that trade between core and periphery systematically transfers value even without explicit coercion, through wage and productivity differentials embedded in price structures. Worth depth because it provides the technical mechanism behind global inequality that comparative advantage theory conceals, and it is contested enough within the tradition that the debate itself is instructive.
