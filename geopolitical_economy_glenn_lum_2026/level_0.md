# Geopolitical Economy — Level 0: Course Map

> **Intent:** To understand the global order not as the natural outcome of markets and comparative advantage, but as the product of political power, imperial history, and class interest. This tradition equips you to read the news, international institutions, and economic policy as what they are — instruments of power — rather than as neutral or technical phenomena.
>
> **Your angle:** You are approaching this as someone who can already see that mainstream economics and IR theory don't explain the world as it actually behaves. The goal is to build the theoretical architecture underneath that intuition — the historical materialist foundations, the monetary and financial critique, and the geopolitical framework — so your analysis has rigour and not just instinct.

---

## How to use this map

Each **Level 1 topic** is one concept post: what it is, where it sits in the tradition, and why it matters to the larger framework. Many of these topics are interconnected rather than sequential — the map reflects the structure of the argument, not a reading list.

Each **Level 2 sub-concept** is a candidate for a depth post: the mechanics, the historical evidence, the internal debates, where the concept breaks or gets contested. Descend only into sub-concepts where deeper understanding is actively blocking your reasoning.

## Topic Inventory

### Group 1 — Foundations: The Materialist Framework

*The theoretical bedrock the tradition stands on. These concepts explain why geopolitical economy looks at the world differently from both mainstream economics and liberal IR theory. Start here — everything downstream assumes these.*

---

#### L1-01 · Historical Materialism

**What it is and why it matters:** Marx's method for understanding history and society: the material conditions of production — how people organise labour, resources, and technology — shape political institutions, ideology, and international relations, not the other way around. This is the methodological foundation that separates this tradition from idealist accounts of history. Without it, the analysis of capitalism, empire, and the state floats free of its grounding. Desai's *Geopolitical Economy* opens with this framework explicitly to distinguish itself from both mainstream IR theory and Marxist orthodoxy that underweights the national and geopolitical dimensions.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · The base and superstructure** — Understanding this relationship explains why economic interests show up in legal, political, and ideological forms that obscure rather than announce themselves.
- **L2 · The mode of production** — The concept that lets you periodise history (feudalism, capitalism, imperialism) rather than treating each era as entirely novel — the framework that makes comparative historical analysis possible.
- **L2 · Class and class struggle** — Drilling here reveals why the state is not a neutral arbiter between interests but an instrument whose character is shaped by which class holds power at a given moment.
- **L2 · Ideology and false consciousness** — What becomes clear here is why populations often support arrangements that work against their material interests — and why economic narratives that serve power feel like common sense.
- **L2 · Combined and uneven development** — Trotsky's extension of Marx, critical for understanding why capitalism does not produce convergence across nations but permanently reproduces hierarchy — the theoretical basis for dependency theory.

---

#### L1-02 · The Nature of Capitalism

**What it is and why it matters:** Before analysing the global order, the tradition requires a clear account of what capitalism actually is — not a system of free exchange, but a system of surplus extraction organised through wage labour and private ownership of the means of production. Marx's *Capital* (1867) is the primary text; Hudson and Desai both return to it to distinguish productive capitalism from the financial and rentier capitalism that dominates the contemporary period. The distinction matters because much of what passes for capitalism today Marx would have recognised as its pathological form.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · Labour theory of value** — The contested claim that labour is the source of value in capitalism — drilling here reveals why prices and wages are not neutral market signals but expressions of social power relations.
- **L2 · Surplus value and exploitation** — The mechanism by which profit is generated — understanding this makes visible what is obscured when work is framed as fair exchange.
- **L2 · Capital accumulation and crisis** — Why capitalism is inherently prone to periodic crises rather than self-correcting — the internal logic that drives overproduction, financialisation, and imperial expansion.
- **L2 · Productive vs. rentier capitalism** — Hudson's central distinction: productive capital generates value; rentier capital extracts it through rent, interest, and financial fees. This split explains why financialisation hollows out economies rather than growing them.
- **L2 · Fictitious capital** — Marx's term for financial assets whose value exceeds any underlying productive activity — the concept that makes debt, derivatives, and asset bubbles theoretically legible rather than aberrations.

---

#### L1-03 · The State and Capital

**What it is and why it matters:** The tradition rejects both the liberal view (the state as neutral regulator) and the crude Marxist view (the state as simple instrument of capital). Instead, the state is a contested terrain shaped by class forces — but also a relatively autonomous actor with its own institutional interests, particularly in the international arena. This is where Desai's *Geopolitical Economy* makes its most distinctive contribution: nation-states are not being dissolved by globalisation but are actively constituting and contesting the global order, particularly the rising powers.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · Relative autonomy of the state** — Drilling here reveals why states sometimes act against the immediate interests of dominant capital — and why this doesn't disprove the class character of the state.
- **L2 · The state and currency** — Hudson and Desai both emphasise that states create and manage money — understanding this destroys the myth of currency as a neutral medium and reveals monetary policy as political power.
- **L2 · Nationalism and the national question** — Why the nation-state has persisted and strengthened despite decades of globalisation theory predicting its dissolution — and what that means for any theory of the global order.
- **L2 · The developmental state** — The historical evidence that successful industrialisation has always involved active state direction of capital rather than free markets — the Asian developmental states (Japan, South Korea, China) as the clearest cases.
- **L2 · State competition and multipolarity** — Why inter-state rivalry is not an aberration but a structural feature of capitalist development — the concept that underpins Desai and Norton's analysis of the current transition away from US hegemony.

### Group 2 — Core Theory: Imperialism and the World Order

*The tradition's account of how capitalism produces and reproduces a hierarchical international system. These are the concepts that directly explain colonialism, dollar hegemony, debt, and the structure of global institutions.*

---

#### L1-04 · Classical Imperialism Theory

**What it is and why it matters:** Between 1902 and 1917, a body of theory emerged — Hobson, Hilferding, Luxembourg, Lenin — that explained imperialism not as a moral failure or policy choice but as a structural necessity of mature capitalism: surplus capital needs outlets, domestic markets are insufficient, and expansion abroad is the result. Lenin's *Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism* (1917) is the canonical text. This framework is the ancestor of everything Desai, Hudson, and Norton build on — understanding it means you can read their arguments as extensions and corrections rather than starting from scratch.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · Finance capital and monopoly** — Hilferding's analysis of how banking capital merges with industrial capital to produce monopoly — the stage Lenin identified as the basis of imperialist expansion.
- **L2 · Capital export** — Why surplus capital is exported to the periphery rather than reinvested domestically — the mechanism that explains colonial investment patterns and contemporary debt relationships.
- **L2 · The labour aristocracy** — Lenin's contested argument that imperial superprofits fund a relatively privileged layer of workers in the core — the concept that explains why working-class politics in imperial nations often supports rather than opposes imperialism.
- **L2 · Rosa Luxemburg's accumulation theory** — The argument that capitalism requires a non-capitalist exterior to absorb surplus — a different account of imperialism's necessity that complements and challenges Lenin's.
- **L2 · Ultra-imperialism vs. inter-imperial rivalry** — Kautsky's prediction (contested by Lenin) that capitalists would cooperate globally rather than compete — the debate that directly anticipates today's arguments about US hegemony vs. multipolarity.

---

#### L1-05 · Dollar Hegemony and Monetary Imperialism

**What it is and why it matters:** Michael Hudson's most distinctive contribution: the US dollar's status as the global reserve currency is not a market outcome but a political privilege extracted through the post-WWII Bretton Woods order and sustained through military and financial power. Countries that run trade surpluses with the US must recycle those surpluses into US Treasury bonds — effectively financing US military spending and consumption. Hudson's *Super Imperialism* (1972, updated 2021) is the foundational text. This is the mechanism that makes the US-centred global order coercive rather than consensual, and it is the primary target of the dedollarisation strategies Desai and Norton track in the emerging multipolar order.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · Bretton Woods and the gold-dollar standard** — Understanding the 1944 settlement reveals that dollar hegemony was a political construction, not a market discovery — and that its eventual breakdown was structurally inevitable.
- **L2 · The Nixon shock (1971)** — When the US unilaterally ended dollar-gold convertibility, it transformed the dollar from a commodity-backed currency into a pure fiat reserve — drilling here shows how this actually *strengthened* rather than weakened US monetary power.
- **L2 · Treasury bond recycling** — The mechanism by which trade surplus nations (historically Japan, Germany, now China and the Gulf states) are compelled to hold US debt — the structural relationship Hudson calls "Super Imperialism."
- **L2 · Sanctions as monetary weapon** — How control of dollar clearing (SWIFT, correspondent banking) functions as a coercive instrument — and why dedollarisation is a security strategy as much as an economic one.
- **L2 · Dedollarisation and the multipolar monetary order** — What the BRICS payment systems, bilateral currency agreements, and commodity-backed alternatives reveal about the structural limits of dollar hegemony — and what a post-dollar order might actually look like.

---

#### L1-06 · Debt, Austerity, and the IMF Model

**What it is and why it matters:** Hudson's analysis of debt is central to the tradition: debt is not a neutral financial instrument but a mechanism of surplus extraction and political control. The IMF and World Bank were designed at Bretton Woods not to develop the Global South but to open it to US capital — structural adjustment programmes impose austerity, privatisation, and export orientation that systematically transfer wealth upward and outward. This is not conspiracy but structure: the institutions operate as designed. Understanding this framework makes IMF conditionality, sovereign debt crises, and contemporary debt trap narratives theoretically legible.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · Compound interest and the mathematics of debt** — Hudson's argument that debts that cannot be repaid will not be repaid — and that the historical solution (debt jubilee) has been systematically suppressed in the modern era.
- **L2 · Structural adjustment programmes** — What the standard IMF package actually does to a debtor economy — privatisation, currency devaluation, public sector cuts — and why the outcomes are predictable rather than accidental.
- **L2 · Odious debt** — The legal and political concept that debt incurred by illegitimate governments should not bind successor states — a weapon available to debtor nations that is systematically delegitimised by creditor institutions.
- **L2 · The debt trap narrative (and its inversion)** — Why accusations of Chinese "debt trap diplomacy" in Africa collapse under scrutiny — and what the comparison to IMF conditionality reveals about whose debt traps are structurally documented.
- **L2 · Debt jubilee as historical norm** — Hudson's recovery of the ancient Near Eastern practice of periodic debt cancellation as a system-stabilising mechanism — the concept that reframes debt relief as restoration of historical normality rather than radical departure.

---

#### L1-07 · Geopolitical Economy as a Framework

**What it is and why it matters:** Radhika Desai's *Geopolitical Economy* (2013) names and theorises the discipline itself — an approach that refuses the separation between economics and geopolitics that mainstream IR theory and economics both enforce. The argument: capitalism has always been nationally organised and inter-state rivalry has always been constitutive of the world order. The apparent era of US-led globalisation was an anomaly enabled by the specific conditions of post-WWII US hegemony — and its unravelling is not a crisis of globalisation but the return of the normal condition of capitalist inter-state competition. This framework is the architecture that organises everything else in the tradition.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · Capitalism and nationality** — Desai's argument that capitalism has always developed within and through national frameworks rather than dissolving them — the counter to globalisation theory's prediction of the state's decline.
- **L2 · Hegemony and its limits** — Why US hegemony was always partial, contested, and dependent on specific historical conditions rather than being a stable equilibrium — the argument that makes its current decline theoretically expected rather than surprising.
- **L2 · The multipolar transition** — What the evidence actually shows about the emergence of new centres of power (China, Russia, the Global South coalitions) and what geopolitical economy predicts about how the transition will unfold.
- **L2 · The West as a political construct** — Desai and Norton's argument that "the West" is a Cold War political category rather than a geographic or civilisational reality — and why its coherence is now actively fracturing.
- **L2 · Unequal exchange** — Arghiri Emmanuel's and later Samir Amin's theory that trade between core and periphery systematically transfers value from the latter to the former even in the absence of explicit coercion — the mechanism behind global inequality that comparative advantage theory conceals.

### Group 3 — Applied Analysis: The Contemporary World Order

*Where the theoretical framework meets the current moment. These topics apply the foundational and core concepts to the specific configuration of power, conflict, and transition that defines the present period.*

---

#### L1-08 · US Imperialism: Structure and Decline

**What it is and why it matters:** The tradition treats US imperialism not as a matter of bad intentions but of structural position: a declining hegemon using military power, dollar dominance, sanctions, and institutional control to maintain a system of extraction that is increasingly contested. Norton's reporting at Multipolarista and Desai and Hudson's collaborative work both emphasise that what looks like US foreign policy irrationality is better understood as a declining power defending its structural privileges. The contradiction is that the methods used to defend hegemony (sanctions, military expansion, financial coercion) accelerate the very fragmentation they are designed to prevent.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · The military-industrial complex as economic system** — Why US military spending is not separable from its domestic political economy — and how permanent war expenditure functions as a Keynesian stimulus that cannot be politically replaced.
- **L2 · NATO as imperial instrument** — The argument that NATO is not a defensive alliance but the military enforcement arm of US-centred order — and how its post-Cold War expansion contradicts its stated rationale.
- **L2 · Sanctions as imperial tool and self-harm** — How secondary sanctions function as extraterritorial enforcement of US law — and why the overuse of dollar weaponisation accelerates dedollarisation by demonstrating to non-Western states the cost of dollar dependence.
- **L2 · Colour revolutions and regime change** — The pattern of US-backed political interventions framed as democracy promotion — understanding the mechanism reveals why geopolitical economy treats liberal democracy discourse as often functioning as imperial ideology.
- **L2 · Imperial overstretch** — Paul Kennedy's concept (extended in this tradition) that the cost of maintaining hegemony eventually exceeds the surplus it extracts — the structural dynamic that explains US fiscal and political dysfunction as symptoms of imperial decline.

---

#### L1-09 · China and the Challenge to Western Hegemony

**What it is and why it matters:** China is the central empirical case in the contemporary multipolar argument. From the geopolitical economy perspective, China's rise is not an aberration to be explained but the expected outcome of a developmental state applying the same tools — state direction, industrial policy, controlled capital account — that every successful industrialiser from Britain to the United States to South Korea used. What is distinctive is the scale and the explicit challenge to dollar-denominated financial architecture. Norton's work at Multipolarista tracks this empirically; Desai provides the theoretical frame; Hudson analyses the financial dimension.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · The Chinese developmental state model** — Why China's economic trajectory refutes rather than confirms the Washington Consensus — and what the specific institutional features (state banks, SOEs, capital controls) explain about the outcome.
- **L2 · Belt and Road Initiative as geopolitical economy** — How BRI functions simultaneously as surplus capital export, infrastructure diplomacy, and dollar-alternative construction — the framework that makes it legible beyond Western media characterisations.
- **L2 · The yuan internationalisation project** — What China's efforts to settle trade in yuan, build alternative payment systems, and price commodities outside the dollar actually reveal about the pace and ceiling of dedollarisation.
- **L2 · The technology war** — Why US export controls on semiconductors, AI, and advanced manufacturing are better understood as attempts to arrest China's development trajectory than as security measures — and why they are likely to accelerate Chinese domestic development rather than contain it.
- **L2 · Sino-Russian alignment** — What the deepening economic and strategic relationship between China and Russia reveals about the structural logic of multipolar coalition-building — and why it was structurally predictable from the geopolitical economy framework.

---

#### L1-10 · The Global South and Multipolarity

**What it is and why it matters:** The tradition takes the Global South seriously not as a humanitarian concern but as an active political force reshaping the world order. BRICS expansion, African Union monetary proposals, Latin American left governments, and Global South abstention on Western sanctions all represent the same structural dynamic: states that have experienced the costs of dollar hegemony and IMF conditionality are building institutional alternatives. Desai and Norton both emphasise that this is not idealism but interest — these states have concrete material reasons to support a multipolar order, and the West's reaction (framing all deviation as Chinese or Russian influence) systematically misreads the dynamic.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · BRICS and its expansion** — What the addition of Saudi Arabia, Iran, Ethiopia, Egypt, UAE, and Argentina (and subsequent applications) actually signals about the organisational capacity of the multipolar coalition — and what BRICS can and cannot do institutionally.
- **L2 · Africa's monetary sovereignty project** — The African Union's push for a single currency and the broader resistance to CFA franc dependence — the clearest case of the tradition's argument that monetary sovereignty is a prerequisite for developmental sovereignty.
- **L2 · Latin American pink tide and its contradictions** — What successive waves of left governments in Latin America reveal about the structural constraints on developmental state projects operating within dollar hegemony — and why each wave has faced the same destabilisation mechanisms.
- **L2 · The Non-Aligned Movement as precedent** — The historical attempt by newly decolonised states to create a third pole outside Cold War blocs — understanding why it ultimately failed informs what structural conditions the current multipolar project would need to succeed.
- **L2 · Commodity power and the resource curse** — Why resource-rich Global South states occupy a structurally contradictory position — potentially powerful in a world of commodity scarcity, but historically subject to price manipulation and political intervention that neutralises that power.

---

#### L1-11 · Financialisation and the Rentier Economy

**What it is and why it matters:** Hudson's most sustained analytical contribution is the theory of financialisation: the process by which financial claims on productive activity — debt, rent, interest, fees — come to dominate and extract from the productive economy rather than supporting it. This is not a recent pathology but the mature form of rentier capitalism that Marx identified in *Capital* Volume III and that Hudson traces from ancient Babylonian debt structures through to contemporary Wall Street. The implication is that the economic stagnation, inequality, and political dysfunction of Western economies are not policy failures but structural outcomes of allowing the FIRE sector (Finance, Insurance, Real Estate) to capture the surplus.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · The FIRE sector** — Hudson's framework for understanding how finance, insurance, and real estate function as a unified extractive system rather than productive sectors — the concept that reframes asset price inflation as a redistribution mechanism.
- **L2 · Economic rent vs. earned income** — The classical distinction (Ricardo, Mill, George, Marx) between income earned through productive activity and income extracted through ownership of scarce assets — the conceptual tool that makes rentier extraction visible in national accounts that currently conceal it.
- **L2 · Student debt and healthcare as financialisation** — How essential services become mechanisms of financial extraction — and why the US model represents the most advanced case of rentier capture of previously public goods.
- **L2 · Quantitative easing and asset price inflation** — Why post-2008 monetary policy transferred wealth upward rather than stimulating productive investment — and what this reveals about whose interests central bank independence actually serves.
- **L2 · The classical economists vs. the neoclassical turn** — Hudson's argument that mainstream economics after the 1890s deliberately erased the classical distinction between earned income and rent — the intellectual history that explains why rentier extraction is invisible in contemporary economic discourse.

### Group 4 — Method and Critique

*How the tradition reads sources, evaluates evidence, and positions itself against mainstream alternatives. Essential for knowing how to use these frameworks without uncritically accepting them.*

---

#### L1-12 · Critique of Mainstream Economics and IR Theory

**What it is and why it matters:** Geopolitical economy defines itself in explicit opposition to two disciplinary orthodoxies: neoclassical economics (which treats markets as self-equilibrating, ignores power, and separates economics from politics) and liberal IR theory (which treats states as rational utility-maximisers in an anarchic system, ignoring the class character of states and the role of capital). Understanding what these frameworks get wrong — and *why* they get it wrong, not just that they do — is necessary for using geopolitical economy credibly rather than just assertively. The tradition argues these frameworks are not merely incomplete but ideologically functional: they obscure the mechanisms of extraction that sustain the current order.

**Level 2 candidates:**

- **L2 · Comparative advantage and free trade ideology** — Why the Ricardian case for free trade has always been selectively applied by powerful states for their own development while being imposed on weaker ones — the historical record that the theory systematically ignores.
- **L2 · The Washington Consensus** — The specific policy package (privatisation, liberalisation, fiscal austerity) imposed on the Global South from the 1980s onward — understanding its outcomes makes the ideological function of mainstream development economics visible.
- **L2 · Realism, liberalism, and constructivism in IR** — The mainstream IR frameworks and their shared blind spot: none has a theory of capitalism, so none can explain the economic drivers of international conflict and cooperation.
- **L2 · The depoliticisation of economics** — How the post-WWII professionalization of economics as a technical discipline (rather than a political one) functioned to remove distributional questions from legitimate debate — and whose interests that removal served.
- **L2 · Media and think tank ecosystem** — How the production and distribution of mainstream economic and geopolitical analysis is structurally shaped by funding sources — the institutional explanation for why certain frameworks dominate without needing a conspiracy theory.

## Sequencing note

The logical dependency runs from Group 1 through to Group 4, but not rigidly. **Historical materialism (L1-01)** and **the nature of capitalism (L1-02)** are genuine prerequisites — without them, the later arguments about imperialism, dollar hegemony, and financialisation lack theoretical grounding and can feel like assertions rather than derived positions.

**Classical imperialism theory (L1-04)** and **dollar hegemony (L1-05)** are the next highest-leverage entries: they provide the structural mechanisms that make contemporary US foreign policy legible, and they are where Hudson's work is most distinctive and most accessible.

For someone coming to this with strong political intuitions but patchy theoretical architecture, the recommended entry sequence is: **L1-02 → L1-04 → L1-05 → L1-07**. This gives you Marx's account of capitalism, Lenin's account of imperialism, Hudson's account of monetary power, and Desai's synthesis — the four pillars of the tradition in logical order.

**L1-11 (Financialisation)** can be read in parallel with L1-05 since Hudson's monetary and financialisation analyses are deeply connected. **Group 3 (Applied Analysis)** should be approached after Group 2 is established — the contemporary analysis is most powerful when the theoretical framework is already in place, otherwise it reads as journalism rather than structural analysis.

**L1-12 (Critique of Mainstream Frameworks)** is best read last, or revisited after the core tradition is established — its arguments land harder when you already have the alternative framework clearly in hand.
