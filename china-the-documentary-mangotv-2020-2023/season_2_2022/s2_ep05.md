## Episode Introduction

This episode is about the political heart of the mid-to-late Ming dynasty [明朝, Míngcháo], seen through two of its most famous officials: Zhang Juzheng [张居正, Zhāng Jūzhèng], the great reforming Grand Secretary, and Hai Rui [海瑞, Hǎi Ruì], the incorruptible censor-official whose moral severity became legendary. It is not simply a story about good ministers and bad emperors. It is about the changing relationship between ruler and official, between moral principle and administrative power, and between personal integrity and the practical demands of governing a vast empire.

As you watch, keep three questions in mind. What is a “good official” in the Chinese imperial tradition: the man who preserves his moral purity, or the man who compromises in order to make the state function? When the emperor stands above all institutions, who can actually save the dynasty — the remonstrator who speaks truth, or the strategist who concentrates power? And when later generations remember men like Hai Rui and Zhang Juzheng so differently, what does that tell us about what Chinese civilisation most admires, and most fears, in political life?

## Where We Are and How We Got Here

We are now in the sixteenth century, deep in the Ming dynasty [明朝, Míngcháo], especially under the Jiajing Emperor [嘉靖帝, Jiājìng Dì], the Longqing Emperor [隆庆帝, Lóngqìng Dì], and the early reign of the Wanli Emperor [万历帝, Wànlì Dì]. The episode follows the lives of Hai Rui and Zhang Juzheng from the 1550s to the 1580s, tracing how each responded to what both men saw as a profound crisis in the empire: administrative decay, fiscal weakness, court factionalism, and the growing gap between Confucian ideals and actual government.

The continuity from the previous episode matters, even though the subject has shifted sharply. We are coming from the Yuan dynasty [元朝, Yuáncháo], where conquest had broken the old elite order and theatre became one of the places where a conquered civilisation preserved its moral voice. Now we are in a native-ruled dynasty again, the Ming [明, Míng], founded after the expulsion of Mongol rule by Zhu Yuanzhang [朱元璋, Zhū Yuánzhāng], the Hongwu Emperor [洪武帝, Hóngwǔ Dì]. In one sense, this is a restoration: Chinese rule has returned, the examination system [科举, kējǔ] has been rebuilt, and Confucian statecraft once again claims the centre of political life. But the unresolved question carried forward from the Yuan is still there in a new form: where does moral authority really live when institutions fail to embody the values they proclaim?

Under the Ming, the answer was supposed to be clear. The dynasty presented itself as a Confucian moral state, more disciplined and more properly ordered than the Mongol regime it replaced. Yet the Ming founder had built that state around an extraordinarily concentrated monarchy. He abolished the office of chief minister or chancellor [丞相, chéngxiàng], ensuring that no single minister would formally stand between emperor and bureaucracy. That decision did not eliminate the need for coordination; it simply forced new institutions to emerge, above all the Grand Secretariat [内阁, Nèigé], whose senior grand secretaries gradually became de facto chief ministers without the formal security of the old post. That ambiguity is one of the key inheritances shaping this episode.

By the sixteenth century, the Ming empire was powerful and wealthy, but also strained. Emperors withdrew from direct governance, court politics hardened into faction, eunuchs [宦官, huànguān] and civil officials [文官, wénguān] competed for influence, and the bureaucracy often slowed into ritualism, delay, and mutual obstruction. What had carried forward from earlier centuries was the conviction that the empire should be ruled by morally cultivated scholar-officials [士大夫, shìdàfū]. What had changed was the difficulty of making that ideal work under an autocratic court. This is the world in which Hai Rui and Zhang Juzheng appear: one insisting that moral truth must be spoken regardless of consequence, the other concluding that only concentrated authority and hard institutional reform could rescue the state.

## The Ideas Underneath

At the centre of this episode is the Confucian idea that politics is a moral relationship before it is an administrative mechanism. The ruler was not meant to govern by force alone. He was supposed to embody virtue, receive remonstrance, and stand in a reciprocal relationship with his ministers. The minister, in turn, was not merely a servant. He was a subject [臣, chén], bound by loyalty [忠, zhōng], but also by a duty to correct the ruler when the ruler went astray. That is why the tradition honoured the ideal of “dying for remonstrance” [文死谏, wén sǐ jiàn]: a true official might risk prison or death to speak what the emperor needed to hear.

Hai Rui represents one extreme expression of that worldview. For him, politics begins with self-cultivation [修身, xiūshēn]. If the official is not morally clean, nothing else can be trusted. His uprightness was not simply personal temperament; it came from a world in which the classics [经, jīng], especially works such as the *Classic of Filial Piety* [《孝经》, Xiàojīng] and the *Great Learning* [《大学》, Dàxué], were understood to shape the moral bones of a human being. Hai Rui believed that if ruler and ministers returned to sincerity, frugality, discipline, and courage, the realm could be set right. He was the kind of figure later generations call a “pure official” [清官, qīngguān], not because he was naïve, but because he refused to separate morality from governance.

Zhang Juzheng begins in the same Confucian world, but his thinking moves in another direction. He too believed in loyalty to the throne and concern for the people [爱民, àimín]. But he came to distrust moral talk that did not produce results. In the atmosphere of the late Jiajing reign, with factional struggles and administrative paralysis everywhere, he concluded that the empire’s survival depended less on exhortation than on institutions. This is where Ming political thought reveals one of its deepest tensions. Confucianism supplied the language of virtue, but the actual running of the empire required records, taxes, reporting systems, military coordination, and enforceable discipline. Zhang moved toward a style of statecraft often described as practical governance [经世致用, jīngshì zhìyòng]: not abandoning morality, but embedding it in systems.

This is also why the episode places weight on the conflict between different strands of late imperial learning. The lingering influence of Wang Yangming [王阳明, Wáng Yángmíng] and the “learning of the mind” [心学, xīnxué] had encouraged attention to inner sincerity and moral intuition. Zhang Juzheng, facing bureaucratic drift, turned instead toward the more disciplinary and orthodox Neo-Confucian framework associated with Cheng-Zhu learning [程朱理学, Chéng-Zhū lǐxué]. That was not simply a philosophical preference. It was a political choice. A state that needed uniformity, hierarchy, and measurable performance was naturally drawn toward a more regulated vision of order.

Underlying both men is another old civilisational assumption: that the state exists for the welfare of the people, but that welfare is achieved through hierarchy, not equality. Land surveys, tax reform, mourning rites, court ritual, appointment procedures, and the duty to kneel or not kneel before authority all mattered because they were expressions of the moral order itself. In this world, procedure was never “just procedural.” It was a visible sign of whether Heaven’s pattern [天理, tiānlǐ] and human government still aligned.

## What Was At Stake

The central tension of this episode is the struggle to define what loyalty to the dynasty really required in a time of decline. Was the truest servant of the Ming the man who spoke uncompromising moral truth to power, or the man who bent rules, gathered authority, and imposed reform from above?

Hai Rui chose the first path. His famous memorial to the Jiajing Emperor, the *Memorial on the State of the Empire* [《治安疏》, *Zhì'ān Shū*], was an act of almost theatrical moral courage. He wrote as a minister who believed that to remain silent before imperial failure was itself a betrayal. That choice made him a legend, but it did not give him real power to reorder institutions. He became the conscience of the bureaucracy, not its master.

Zhang Juzheng chose the second path. He waited, learned, manoeuvred, entered the Grand Secretariat, and when the moment came under the young Wanli Emperor, used concentrated authority to force the machinery of state back into motion. The performance-evaluation system known as the Examination of Achievements law [考成法, Kǎochéngfǎ], the nationwide land survey [清丈土地, qīngzhàng tǔdì], and the Single Whip Reform [一条鞭法, Yītiáo Biānfǎ] all emerged from that choice. He restored revenue, tightened administration, and gave the dynasty a striking, if temporary, recovery.

But this success came with a second pivot, and it is the one that gives the episode its tragic force. To save the state, Zhang had to push beyond the accepted limits of ministerial power. He used the throne, the bureaucracy, and the censorial apparatus in ways that made him indispensable — and therefore dangerous. His decision to remain in office during mourning through “seizing affection” [夺情, duóqíng], though politically explicable, symbolised the problem. He was acting for the empire, but against deeply rooted Confucian expectations about filial duty [孝, xiào]. The very tools that let him reform the state also made many believe he had become a “power minister” [权臣, quánchén], a man whose strength overshadowed the emperor.

That is the hinge on which the episode turns. Hai Rui preserved moral legitimacy but could not govern. Zhang governed effectively but damaged the moral-political balance that should have made such effectiveness safe. After Zhang’s death, the speed with which he was denounced and stripped of honours shows how fragile reform becomes when it depends on one dominant personality rather than a broadly shared institutional settlement.

## Why This Still Matters

This period still matters because it fixed several enduring patterns in Chinese political culture. One is the extraordinary prestige of the upright official who dares to speak truth to power. Hai Rui remains a cultural type long after the Ming itself vanished. In modern Chinese speech and memory, to call someone a Hai Rui [海瑞, Hǎi Ruì] is to invoke severity, incorruptibility, and fearless remonstrance. The instinctive admiration many Chinese people still feel for the honest, plain-living official who offends superiors but protects principle comes from this long tradition of judging government morally as well as materially.

Another inheritance is the equally strong fascination with the capable reformer who centralises power in order to get things done. Zhang Juzheng’s reputation has risen and fallen across later centuries precisely because he embodies a recurring Chinese dilemma: can a sprawling state be repaired without an unusually forceful political centre? Modern Chinese debates about reform, bureaucratic discipline, anti-corruption campaigns, fiscal restructuring, and the relationship between efficiency and legality all echo Ming arguments in recognisable ways. The question is still alive: should one prefer clean hands or effective hands, if history rarely offers both in the same person?

This era also helps explain the lasting Chinese emphasis on examinations, officialdom, and governance as moral callings rather than merely technical careers. The scholar-official ideal did not disappear with the imperial system. Its cultural afterlife remains visible in the prestige attached to public service, the expectation that educated people should contribute to national order, and the tendency to judge leaders not only by prosperity but by rectitude, seriousness, and self-discipline. Even in secular modern settings, the old assumption lingers that learning should prepare one for responsibility, not just private success.

There is a more intimate legacy as well. The conflict over Zhang Juzheng’s refusal to leave office for full mourning shows how deeply family ethics and state service were intertwined. In Chinese life, filial duty and public duty were never cleanly separate spheres. That inheritance still shapes expectations around sacrifice for family, the moral meaning of obligation, and the sense that private conduct reveals public character. A person of Chinese heritage encounters this legacy not only in textbooks but in everyday instincts: respect for teachers, reverence for upright public figures, suspicion of factional manipulation, admiration for disciplined study, and the recurring belief that a society declines when officials become self-serving.

Finally, this episode matters because it shows something timeless about Chinese civilisation: the state has often been imagined not as a neutral machine, but as an ethical organism that depends on the character of those within it. That is why figures like Hai Rui and Zhang Juzheng endure. They are not remembered only for what they did. They are remembered because they dramatise a permanent question in Chinese history — whether order is saved by virtue, by system, or by some unstable combination of the two.

# Ming Dynasty Officials: Scholars, Governance, and Reform

## The Role of the Official Class

他们有一个共同的名字，叫做“臣” [officials who served the emperor]。在中国古代政治体系中，臣扮演着十分重要的角色。他们维系着国家机器的有序运转，是连接军与民的纽带；他们既是服务者，也是管理者，既是政策的制定者，也是执行者。很多流传至今的宫廷制度和规范，都是他们设计的。

“臣”是一种身份，也是一种职业。他们依附于君而存在，受君之命而行事，但他们又是有着各自思想与性格的人。能力有大有小，品性有好有坏；由于价值观不同，选择不同，所言所行也不同，于是他们在后世留下了褒贬不一的评价。

历史进入明代时，君和臣的关系发生了一次极大的变化。文官集团里出现了两个极致的代表，一个叫海瑞，一个叫张居正。

## The Wanli Emperor and the Changing Court

公元前的中国，经过经典文明的积淀，中华文明开启后，政治与社会制度不断演变。经历了一次严重的宫廷刺杀事件后，明帝国的第十一位皇帝朱翊钧 [the Wanli Emperor] 就很少上朝了。

他不信任任何人，除了贴身的太监王安 [inner court attendant] 以及曲尺科数的几位内阁大学士。他几乎不见其他人，无论是儿子，还是大臣。他宁愿跟自己的猫待在一起，只想着休息。

## Zhang Juzheng: Ambition and Return

公元1554年，时任翰林院编修的张居正 [Zhang Juzheng] 以养病为由，请长假回到荆州老家。他的病，是心病。自二十三岁以进士身份进入官场，七年里，他燃烧了自己的全部热情。

## Hai Rui: Moral Rectitude and Local Office

海瑞更加注重对自己的人格修炼。年轻时，他曾写下一篇《自警词》，时刻鞭策自己。在这篇文章里，他假借神明对自己进行灵魂拷问：入府县做官容易，还有花堂美女，会为此动心吗？在财帛世界中能否留得住那样的意志？会不会去贪腐事？会不会歧视到明？

海瑞的家乡在海南，这里偏僻、盘荒，远离中央王朝的统治中心，自古就是贬官的流放地。贬官大多是有气节的文人，他们为海南带来了文化传统，也用自己的风格构筑了一座精神孤岛。

这是青年海瑞对自己的人生设定：相远，是指外表警洁、实则为善之人，被孔子称为“偃之贼者” [a corrupting hypocrite, as discussed in classical moral terms]。海瑞决定用德行铺就自己的仕宦之路。

公元1553年12月，海瑞出任福建南平县教谕 [local education officer]，这是当地主管教育的学官。上任第一年，海瑞心中严明的准则就与明朝官场的风气产生了一次硬碰硬的对撞。

时逢巡按御史 [imperial inspector] 造访学宫。巡按御史代表天子巡视监察百官，所到之处地方官员无不屈身跪拜，唯独海瑞不懂。尽管只是一名小小的学官，他的骨头比谁都硬。巡按御史阅官无数，但也从来没有见过像海瑞这样的人。

他居然说，这里是教书育人的学堂，不是御史大人的衙署，怎么能下跪呢？不认官场潜规则，只认太祖的清规戒律，这就是海瑞。

他为自己取了一个别号，叫“钢风”。他决心像山风一样刚正。曾经，他的道德之镜只照自己；如今，他开始拿这面镜子映照整个国家。这让海瑞顿时名声大振，但也因为如此，他成了明朝官场的令雷折服 [a figure that made the court uneasy and fearful]。

## Reform, Conflict, and the Rise of Zhang Juzheng

三年对于像张居正这样清醒而自负的人，已经足够了。曾经的挫折感早已消迷，三年时间足够他想清楚自己究竟要什么，以及如何才能到达他心中的意志坚定如山。

公元1557年秋，张居正决意重返翰林院。几个月后，海瑞也有了一个更为正式的官职——浙江淳安知县 [magistrate of Chun'an, Zhejiang]。自此，他们几乎同时踏上了新的仕宦之路。

公元1564年，五十一岁的海瑞升任户部主事 [junior official in the Ministry of Revenue]。虽然官阶不高，但身在朝廷的中枢部门，海瑞认为自己看到了国家的生存危机：嘉靖皇帝二十多年都没有上过朝，这毫无疑问是君道不正；前首辅严嵩一党历经十数年方被铲除，但吏治依旧腐败，这源于臣职不明。如此严重的问题，怎么能不去改变？

海瑞感到无比愤慨。为臣者，对下理应尽全情；对上，即便逆鳞也要敢于触犯。海瑞认定，臣子有责任说出自己必须说的话。

明代的儒家弟子们，对立志的批评一向极为猛烈，即使因此获罪也在所不惜。二十多年前，阳明心学的弟子刘珝 [Liu Xun] 就因反对嘉靖皇帝臣迷修道而上书。上书前，他为自己准备了一口棺材，刘珝因此入狱四年，出狱不久便死了。这样的人在他之前不胜枚举，在他之后至少还有海瑞。所谓文死谏 [to die remonstrating for the ruler's correction]，这是士大夫的光荣。

十六世纪中叶，大明帝国是全球最大的政治实体，人口数量、领土面积、财富规模都是最大的帝国。治理需要人才，这一点海瑞和张居正应该是一致的：要做一个对国家有贡献的良臣。只是张居正觉得有非常之事，然后有非常之功；而海瑞认为，天下孰为重，得意为重。

为了能让皇帝惊醒，公元1565年六平小关，海瑞提笔写下一本《治安疏》 [Memorial on the State of the Empire]，抱着必死的决心，他决定台官上书，语出惊人。这是嘉靖皇帝看到的最尖刻、最辛辣的奏述，每一句话、每一个字都直刺他的心。

在这篇长达三千字的文书中，海瑞列述皇帝的微证之失和微军之婚。他说，天下之人不知陛下久矣，意思是全天下的人认为你不对已经很久了，从来没有一个人敢对自己说这样的话。

嘉靖皇帝的震怒不言而喻。他不等看完就大喊：抓住海瑞，不要让人家知道他跑了！当听说海瑞在上奏前已经被自己埋好棺材后，他重新打开奏本，不由发出一句长叹，说道：“这个人大概算是比干吧，只是我不是纣王。”

嘉靖皇帝破天荒地没有惩罚海瑞，只是把奏书留中不发。但在几个月后，他还是气愤不过，下旨把海瑞关进了诏狱 [imperial prison]。此时已升任首辅的徐阶欣赏海瑞的忠直，在他的暗中保护下，海瑞幸免于死。

身在狱中的海瑞是平静的，甚至是欢喜的。他不知道会面临怎样的结局，但他对自己坚信不疑。如果母亲在身边，应该也会为自己感到骄傲。

海瑞四岁父亲去世，从此与母亲相依为命。那是一位贤良而严厉的母亲，在海瑞很小时就用《孝经》《大学》《中庸》等儒家经典言传身教，为他构建了一片一尘不染的道德天空。海瑞没有辜负母亲的教诲。

半年多后，嘉靖皇帝驾崩，海瑞被释放。虽然皇帝至死都对海瑞的上书不以为然，但他和当朝群臣都知道，海瑞在道义上无可指责，帝国也需要这样的人。

嘉靖皇帝去世后，三十岁的朱载垕 [the Longqing Emperor] 即位，改年号为隆庆。公元1567年为隆庆元年。因为曾担任过朱载垕的王府讲官，张居正正式进入内阁。这是张居正重返翰林院的第十个年头。

十年里，明朝官场变化很大。他的老师徐阶用韬光养晦的方式，在残酷的内阁争斗中保全了自己，当上了首辅；而这次，张居正也逐渐变成和老师一样的人，甚至沉处于然 [becoming more restrained and strategic]，而圣于然 [more inwardly prepared]，谋事不再一时一刻。他不像年轻时那么急躁了。他清晰地看到，嘉靖皇帝在位四十五年，大明走向了迷乱，这个看似稳定成熟的国家，实则应患重重。

他看到了政府管理效率的低下，看到了各级官员的涣散，看到了军备的废弛，也看到了底层百姓如何在愈演愈烈的土地兼并中朝不保夕。他决心改变这一切，但他也明白，此时仍然需要等待。

## Longqing, Wanli, and the Drive for Change

回归官场以来，张居正的思想也发生了转变。明代立国后，朱元璋把道德作为治国之本，这对后代臣子影响极深。海瑞便是祖宗之法的极力推崇者；张居正曾经和海瑞一样，认为道明之术在于学术教化，但渐渐地，他改变了这个观点。

他认为，这个国家此时更加需要的是制度建设。身为儒家文化体系下成长起来的臣子，张居正和海瑞有着共同的精神内核，那就是忠君、爱民；只是他们在实现的方式上背道而驰。

隆庆皇帝在位六年，他打破了父亲近三十年不上朝的传统，登基后立刻开始接触群臣，处理前朝给他留下的棘手问题。朝堂上的他常常一言不发，以至于有大臣以为皇帝是个哑巴，但皇帝无微不至，实则推动了臣子的努力工作。

隆庆一朝，大明帝国解决了最棘手的两大外患：东南倭患 [Japanese pirate raids] 基本平定，与北方蒙古族也展开了官方贸易。开放的政策换来了边境和平，只是好景不长。

隆庆六年五月，朝会上的皇帝突然病重，消息在朝野上下弥漫开来。一天后，隆庆皇帝被宣布不治，十岁的太子朱翊钧即位，改元万历 [Wanli]。

随着新皇帝登基，新一轮的权力之争暗流涌动。明朝的内阁从来没有平静过，此时的内阁首辅已经变成高拱 [Gao Gong]。他对新上任的司礼监掌印太监冯保 [Feng Bao] 绕过内阁、直接批发皇帝手谕感到愤怒，准备以篡改遗诏为罪名除掉冯保。

张居正暗中支持着一切。高拱的计划刚刚启动，没想到被冯保抢先一步。他告诉太后、太妃，说先皇去世时，首辅高拱在内阁里教着十岁的孩子怎么做皇帝。这让太后、太妃选择了与冯保站在一起。

一场臣子之间的权力之争，演变成了弱君对强臣的一次反击。公元1572年6月，太后、太妃借着万历皇帝之名，革除了高拱的所有职位，限令他几日出京返回原籍。

这天张居正请了病假，可能是真的病了，也可能是为了避险。高拱是自己老师徐阶的政敌，自他介入那个以来，已有多名阁臣被迫离开。身为徐阶的得意门生，张居正没有受到牵连，靠的全是自己的营人和左右逢源。

张居正并不清楚冯保对高拱的构陷，但他乐见其成。高拱既败，首辅的位置再次空了出来。随后不久，定议名内阁成员李氏 [rankings and appointments within the Grand Secretariat]，大明帝国的内阁里形成了一种局面：张居正一人，加上他曾担任少保、兼太子太保 [Grand Tutor to the Crown Prince]，是万历皇帝的老师，由他接替高拱的职位，理所当然。

张居正走到了臣子生涯的最高点，他的首辅时代终于开始了。这一年他四十八岁。很长时间里，他看起来都像是一个没有态度的人；现在他可以逐渐亮出自己的态度了。从考成法问世以来，已经过去了二十五年。

他从那个容易冲动、容易愤怒的年轻人，变成了一个内敛谨慎、沉稳深沉的老臣。他见证了一轮又一轮的内阁竞争，看到了太多人前赴后继地在自己眼前倒下。张居正决心要结束这一切，方法很简单，那就是牢牢掌握住权力。但这并非他的终极目的，权力只是实现理想的工具。他要做的是一个有力量、能造福天下的臣子。

张居正认为，当时最严重的危机就是军主式衰，政令失控，所以他做的第一件事，就是整饬纲纪，加强军队臣的统属。

公元1573年，张居正向皇帝进献考成法 [performance evaluation system]，要求各级官员把工作任务登记在案，逐级考核后归总于内阁。由此，内阁掌握了国家机器运转的枢纽，提高了行政效率和执行力，松懈、懒散的官僚体系被激发出了活力。只是万历皇帝年幼，对官员生杀予夺的大权实际掌握在了张居正手中。

## Land Reform and State Power

四年后，张居正决定启动他心中酝酿多年的制度改革。他相信，拥有更多的权力才能做更大的事情。

明朝内阁和负责行政的六部 [Six Ministries] 关系原本相对独立。从太祖朱元璋开始，六部一直由皇帝直接管理。张居正担任首辅后，内阁即利用朝中的监察机制控制了六部，这是以往的历任首辅都未能做到的。张居正越过了权力的边界。

正式改革之前，张居正还做了一件事：对散漫不羁的明朝士风进行整顿。当时距离王阳明 [Wang Yangming] 去世不过数十年，他的心学思想仍广为流传，朝中官员大部分都是心学门人。张居正早期也深受影响，但渐渐地，他对空谈之风感到深恶痛绝。他认为，要实现更深层次的变革，必须统一思想，于是想到了强调天理的程朱理学 [Neo-Confucian orthodoxy]。

公元1577年9月，就在张居正即将实施新一轮行动时，突然传来一个消息：他的父亲病逝。按照儒家礼仪，张居正应当立刻回家守丧，二十七个月之后才能复职。这让张居正陷入两难：放弃，定有必会找来骂名；但是只要他离开，即将展开的政改大计必将功亏一篑。

有一个两全其美的办法，那就是夺情 [being allowed to remain in office during mourning]，即由皇帝出面剥夺大臣的孝亲之情，这样就可以名正言顺地留下来。但这种情况极其少见，明朝此前曾明文规定，内外大小官员丁忧者不许报请夺情。

最终，张居正没有走。名义上是年幼的皇帝以夺情的方式驳回了他回家的请求，但在一些大臣看来，是大权独揽的张居正有意促成了这样的结果。他们不相信张居正孝亲的诚意，反而怀疑夺情是否出自皇帝本心。他们联名上书，却遭到严厉的惩罚。

朝堂之外，对张居正的非议甚嚣尘上。一份以海瑞之名弹劾张居正的奏章在南方广为流传。这份奏章并非海瑞所写，但人们相信打着他的名号会更有说服力，也更能引起共鸣。面对汹涌的民意，张居正依然若无其事，他对言论毫不介意。他说，有余情故无二鼻 [an expression of unwavering resolve], 一欢喜失语狂 [unreadable phrase omitted]。

## The Final Years of Reform and the Fate of Two Men

在父亲去世的第二年，公元1578年，张居正下令清丈全国土地。三年后，他将一直在局部地区执行的一条鞭法 [Single Whip Reform] 推行至全国。

在此之前，赋役是分开的，赋税缴纳的是实物，徭役征发的是劳动力。一条鞭法将二者合而为一，统一改为征银。张居正的改革不仅清理出大量被富豪隐匿的田产，增加了国家税收，还简化了纳税方式，提高了行政效率，使农民和工商业者都可以专心从事生产与经营。

张居正全面政改之际，海瑞所谓的海瑞，已经在家赋闲十一年了。他的清廉和刚正未能给明朝官场树立榜样，却形成了极大的舆论影响。后来每到一处，就有地方官主动提出辞职或请求将他调离；而每一地的百姓都在期盼他。听说海瑞出发前往某地，往往呼号哭泣，痛惜不已。明帝国不得不屡次调整对他的任用，最终海瑞选择了致仕还乡。

海瑞对新学十分遵从，他相信新旧礼信奉精神的力量，希望凭着一颗本心改变社会、成就圣王政治。后人在评价他的纯粹时，用了这样一句话：尽忠如楼，廉俭似清 [a life of steadfast loyalty and purity]。一生都在竭力做一个清官的海瑞，在挫折中渐渐老去。

一代治国心帮 [statecraft-minded minister]、为理想的能臣张居正，也迎来了人生的巅峰。他出任首辅后的十年，是明帝国蓬勃发展的十年。正在走下坡路的明朝出现了中兴气象：政治上有考成法，经济上有一条鞭法，军事上他重用名将戚继光 [Qi Jiguang]，农业上他任用何道专家潘季驯 [Pan Jixun] 治理黄河，成效显著。一系列举措让沉疴日久的明帝国焕发出新的生机。

这也是张居正人生的最后十年。十年里，他主导的内阁几乎架空了皇权；他是一个能臣，同时也成了一个权臣。皇帝和太后都对他言听计从，若是张先生偶敢犯错，万历皇帝会亲手为他调制朝堂面 [unreadable phrase omitted]。他为少年皇帝制定了严格的成长计划，但他或许没有想到，小心翼翼的万历皇帝在这样的高压下，反而滋生了逆反之心，以至于万历一代成为大明走向衰微的转折点。

张居正要处理的事情实在太多，以至于积劳成疾。公元1582年6月，张居正病逝，这一年他58岁。在去世前九天，万历皇帝加封他为太师 [Grand Tutor]，这是文官序列中至高无上的职位，明朝两百年历史中从未有人在生前得到过这个荣誉。

张居正的去世让很多人忧惧，但也有很多人在私下谈论相亲。他病重时，半个中国都在为他祈福。随着他的离开，风向开始转变。弹劾张居正的奏书像雪片一般飞向万历皇帝的案前。

公元1584年4月，万历皇帝下诏查抄张居正，剥夺他生前获得的所有荣誉封号。奉命查抄的官员从张居正家中清点出不少财物。这个曾竭力将明王朝带入复兴的名臣，在死后被人们看到了他的另一面。

张居正被查抄的消息传来时，海瑞还在家乡清苦度日。十几年了，他一直在等待。张居正曾派人去考察他，看到海瑞在简陋的污舍里吃着粗粮，十分感动，但是张居正始终没有启用他。他们都希望这个国家变得更好，但该如何去建设，他们都只忠实于自己的理念。

海瑞从57岁等到了72岁，直到张居正死后，皇帝才想起还有这样一位忠心耿耿的臣子。公元1585年正月，海瑞接到任命，出任南京都察院 [Nanjing Censorate] 右佥都御史 [assistant censor-in-chief]。明朝实行两京制，北京、南京各有一套中央官职，地位相同，但南京的职位多为闲差。尽管如此，重回官场的海瑞仍然全情投入。

他的热情对明朝官员来说，却无意刮起了一场旋风。上书弹劾他的奏章层出不穷。海瑞感到朝廷并不真想用自己，只是需要一个楷模；他屡屡请辞，但每一次都未获批准。

上任的第二年，感到来日无多的海瑞卸下了人生最后一份奏书。他表示自己已经垂死，愿意效仿古人直言。他说，陛下立今图治，但是国家却没有变好，主要是对官吏的刑罚太轻了。他建议恢复洪武时期的严峻以言行军法 [strict rule through military-like discipline] 来治理官吏。

这份沉重的奏书背后，是海瑞对明朝官僚集团的极度失望。以他的道德标准，明朝几乎没有一个合格的臣子。

一年后，公元1587年10月，已经晋升南京都察院右都御史 [grand coordinator of the Nanjing Censorate] 的海瑞在任上去世，享年74岁。人们在整理这位二品官员的遗物时发现，仅有奉金八两、一数钱。最后大家不得不凑钱，才办理完海瑞的丧事。

这位几乎得罪了明朝整个官场的臣子，却收获了无数民心。聚集在他的灵柩从江上经过时，百姓白衣送葬，扶棺而哭，不忍离去。

海瑞就这样走完了充满遗憾又自恰自足的一生。

张居正与海瑞的人生始终没有真正的交集，但作为同时代的名臣，他们的命运似乎又是纠缠在一起的。一个是理性而务实的改革派，一个是激进的理想主义者；他们忠君爱民的方式完全不同，但他们都坚定地走完了自己选择的为臣之道。

张居正离世比海瑞早五年。海瑞这位敢骂皇帝的清流 [upright but severe official]，虽是最负盛名的直臣，却并未攻击过张居正。他对张居正的评价只有八个字：公余谋国，主余谋生 [for the public, he planned for the state; for the ruler, he planned for survival]。这又何尝不是海瑞的人生呢？

在这样的臣子走远之后，大明帝国也只剩下一个万丈景象。致敬中华文明。开启有机信箱 [presumably intended as a documentary sign-off]. 提醒您下期看电揭露商业化。明朝的土版业创造了历史的高峰，普通人有了住宿离数的机会，各个基层都成了书记的消费中图书，团波着四海八方的学习，中国的等化传统在今首部绵演不觉，中国人也在一天天打开面向世界的战口。
