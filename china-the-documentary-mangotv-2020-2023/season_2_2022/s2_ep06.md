## The Ideas Underneath

At the core of this episode is the late imperial conviction that knowledge exists to serve order. In the Confucian world, learning was not primarily about private curiosity. It was supposed to cultivate the self, regulate the family, govern the state, and bring peace to the world — the classic sequence of self-cultivation [修身, xiūshēn], family ordering [齐家, qíjiā], state governance [治国, zhìguó], and pacification of “all under Heaven” [平天下, píng tiānxià]. That is why Xu Guangqi could treat agriculture, astronomy, calendrics, and mathematics as morally serious pursuits. They were not distractions from governance. They were part of what good governance required.

This helps explain why agricultural writing mattered so much. In the Chinese political imagination, the people’s livelihood [民生, mínshēng] was the root of the state. If crops failed, taxes faltered; if taxes faltered, armies weakened; if armies weakened, dynasties fell. To write a great agricultural compendium such as the *Nongzheng Quanshu* [《农政全书》, Nóngzhèng Quánshū] was therefore not merely to collect practical information. It was to act on one of the oldest assumptions of Chinese statecraft: that feeding the people is the foundation of legitimate rule.

The same logic shaped the reception of Western learning. Matteo Ricci [利玛窦, Lì Mǎdòu] did not win trust in China by denouncing the native tradition. He succeeded because he recognised that Chinese civilisation was coherent, learned, and selective. To be heard by the scholar-official elite [士大夫, shìdàfū], he had to present himself as a man of letters, astronomy, mathematics, and ethical seriousness. He studied the Confucian canon, adapted to elite manners, and translated foreign ideas into terms that could be fitted into an existing moral universe. This was not a simple “opening” to the West. It was a highly filtered process of cultural translation.

Xu Guangqi’s importance lies in how seriously he took that translation. He did not see European mathematics or astronomy as a wholesale replacement for Chinese learning. He saw them as a supplement, a strengthening, a way of correcting technical weakness without surrendering civilisational confidence. That is a deeply Chinese pattern: absorb the useful, preserve the core. In later language, one might call it taking practical knowledge from outside while retaining cultural substance, but the instinct is already present here.

The printing boom of the late Ming also rested on a changing understanding of who knowledge was for. Earlier imperial culture had always valued texts, but by the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries commercial publishing made books more numerous, cheaper, and socially mobile. Knowledge was no longer confined to examination candidates memorising the Four Books and Five Classics [四书五经, Sìshū Wǔjīng]. Artisans, merchants, travellers, physicians, and local readers entered the textual world more fully. That did not erase hierarchy, but it widened the social life of learning. A civilisation long anchored in elite textual culture was becoming more densely literate and more commercially mediated.

Behind all this lies one more idea: the world as *tianxia* [天下, tiānxià], “all under Heaven.” Traditional Chinese political culture imagined civilisation radiating outward from the centre, with China as the normative core. Ricci’s world maps and the records of maritime travel disturbed that picture. They did not destroy it at once, but they forced educated Chinese to confront a larger, more plural world in which China was immense, but not geographically central in the literal sense. That shift in spatial imagination is one of the quiet revolutions of the episode.

## What Was At Stake

The central tension of this episode is whether late Ming China could enlarge its knowledge of the world without losing confidence in itself. That tension appears in two linked pivots.

The first pivot is outward movement. Zheng He’s voyages [下西洋, xià Xīyáng] demonstrated that the Ming state had the organisational and technological capacity to project itself across the Indian Ocean world. These expeditions reached Southeast Asia, South Asia, and the East African coast, displaying imperial grandeur and gathering information, goods, and diplomatic relationships. But they did not become the foundation of a permanent maritime transformation. The voyages showed what the dynasty could do; the fact that they were not sustained shows what it chose not to become. The possibility of a more ocean-facing Ming existed, and then receded.

The second pivot is intellectual inwardness opening toward the outside. When Ricci and Xu Guangqi collaborated on the Chinese translation of Euclid’s *Elements* [《几何原本》, Jǐhé Yuánběn], something larger than a translation was happening. A new kind of knowledge was entering China: formal, demonstrative, abstract, and cumulative in a way that many Chinese scholars found strikingly different. Xu chose not to reject it as foreign or heretical. He chose to domesticate it, to show that mathematics, astronomy, and calendrical science from Europe could strengthen the empire. That set in motion a new relationship between Chinese scholarship and external knowledge.

But this opening had limits. It depended on elite mediation, political permission, and practical utility. Ricci gained acceptance because he adapted; Xu embraced Western learning because it served governance, agriculture, calendrics, and military need. The deeper unresolved question was whether China would merely borrow techniques or also revise its assumptions about the world. The late Ming moved some distance toward the first, much less toward the second.

That is why the episode has a bittersweet quality. It presents a civilisation of immense intelligence and flexibility, but also one whose political and institutional fragility limited what these encounters could become. The stakes were not simply scientific. They were civilisational: could the Ming renew itself through knowledge before dynastic decline overtook it?

## Why This Still Matters

This period still matters because it helped shape a durable Chinese attitude toward learning from abroad: foreign knowledge may be valuable, but it must be translated, tested, and made useful within Chinese priorities. That instinct appears again and again in modern Chinese history, from late Qing [清朝, Qīngcháo] “self-strengthening” [自强, zìqiáng] reforms to twentieth-century scientific modernisation and contemporary state-driven technological ambition. The pattern is familiar: import methods, retain sovereignty, resist cultural subordination.

A person of Chinese heritage still encounters the legacy of Xu Guangqi’s world in very ordinary ways. One inheritance is the unusually high prestige of practical scholarship. Mathematics, engineering, agronomy, medicine, and technical learning are not always seen as culturally narrow or merely vocational; they can carry a moral seriousness rooted in the old belief that knowledge should benefit the people and strengthen the country. The modern respect for the scholar who also solves concrete problems has deep late-imperial roots.

Another inheritance is the continuing fusion of textual culture and social mobility. The late Ming printing boom widened access to books and made learning more socially diffuse. In later centuries this fed the broader Chinese reverence for education, examination performance, dictionaries, manuals, reference works, and disciplined study as a path to dignity and advancement. Even today, the household respect for books, tutoring, careful note-taking, and mastery through repetition belongs to a civilisational world in which texts were both moral instruments and ladders of life.

The episode also speaks to modern Chinese encounters with globalisation. Ricci’s success came not from demanding that China become Europe, but from understanding that genuine exchange requires respect for the integrity of the civilisation one enters. That remains a live issue in China’s relations with the wider world: what counts as openness, and what counts as intrusion? The late Ming offers an early template for selective engagement, admiration without surrender, and curiosity shaped by cultural self-consciousness.

Finally, this era matters because it preserves a memory of China as both inwardly rooted and outwardly connected. Zheng He’s voyages remain a potent symbol in Chinese public culture: proof that China once sailed far, traded widely, and engaged the world on a grand scale. Xu Guangqi and Ricci remain symbols of intellectual encounter at its best: not conquest, not imitation, but translation. Together they represent a possibility that still resonates — that Chinese civilisation can meet the wider world not by ceasing to be itself, but by expanding what “itself” contains.

## The Scholar's Work

明朝的历史中，知识分子始终扮演着重要角色。那些带着先民气息的思想与创造，几乎贯穿了大明王朝的整个历史，也构成了中华文明延续至今的重要部分。

## Xu Guangqi and Agriculture

徐光启出生在松江府上海县，四十三岁时被选拔为庶吉士 [an imperial academic post for promising scholar-officials]。他出身寒门，却一直埋头苦读，治学极其认真。

明太祖朱元璋强调“知国之要在教化”，认为教育是治国的根本。他建立起一套由上而下的学校体系，中央设国子学 [the Imperial College]，地方设府州县学 [prefectural, subprefectural, and county schools]，并与科举制度紧密相连。教学内容主要是儒家经典。

徐光启的成长和治学，正是在这样的文化背景下展开的。后来，他在杭州任职期间，开始深入观察民间社会。明朝以前，百姓多穿布衣，棉布并不普及；而到了明代，棉布逐渐成为日常用品，不仅提高了百姓生活水平，也带动了税收和手工业的发展。

徐光启意识到，国家的真正稳定，不只在于制度，也在于百姓的生计。他把自己对农业的思考写进《农政全书》 [Encyclopedia of Agricultural Administration]，希望借此改善民生。

徐光启在杭州写成的《知公对》 [an interview-style essay]，记录了他对织工生活的观察。织工们白天辛劳工作，夜晚还常常合唱解乏。徐光启采访其中一人时，对方说，虽然工作辛苦，但每天做工就能拿到工资，足以养家糊口；大家分工合作，各取所需，下班后还能一起放松。

这篇文字后来被认为记录了私营手工业的发展，也有人把它看作对新兴资本主义萌芽的观察。无论如何，它都说明，徐光启不再只是书斋中的学者，而是开始把目光投向真实的社会生活。

## Western Learning and Mathematical Translation

1604年，徐光启再次见到利玛窦。两人的交流范围很广，从上帝到天文地理，从历法到军事科技，无所不谈，但最终最集中的成果，是数学。

徐光启和利玛窦合作翻译欧几里得的数学著作《几何原本》 [Elements of Geometry]。他们在翻译过程中创造了许多沿用至今的中文数学词汇，如点、线、平面、四边形、多边形、平行线、对角线、直角、钝角等。

在徐光启看来，数学不仅是术，更是一种理解世界的方式。西方学者眼中的数学，代表着内在逻辑，是哲学的思辨，也是诸学科的基础。徐光启通过与利玛窦的合作，第一次系统接触到这一套知识体系。

## The Arrival of the Jesuits

利玛窦是意大利的天主教徒，也是受耶稣会委派来到中国传教的学者。他先到澳门，再到广东肇庆，后来又辗转多地。

于是，他开始研读儒家经典，甚至修改教规，以适应中国社会对祖先崇拜的习俗。他将自己打造成一位通晓儒学、精于天文历算的西方学者，逐渐赢得了一批支持者。对他来说，中国人的世界观是完整而自洽的，外来的思想必须经过本土化才能真正立住。

1595年，利玛窦写成第一本中文著作《交友论》 [On Friendship]，把西方思想家的言论和故事编成中文，并加以评析。这本书在士大夫中广受欢迎，也让利玛窦获得了来华以来最大的信任与赞赏。

1604年，徐光启与利玛窦的再次相见，让徐光启找到了新的人生意义。两人除了数学之外，还讨论上帝、天文、地理、历法和军事等问题。

## The Printing Boom and the Rise of New Books

文字与出版的繁荣，让普通人的价值被重新看见，也让明朝社会开始站在近代化的入口处。

这一次，船队走得更远。郑和下西洋 [Zheng He's voyages to the western seas] 不仅显示了明帝国的造船与航海能力，也加强了与海外各国的联系。船队用中国的瓷器、丝绸和茶叶，换回香料、染料、珠宝等物产。

马欢后来随郑和第六次、第七次下西洋，把沿途二十多个国家和地区的政治、人物、物产、风俗等见闻写成《瀛涯胜览》 [A General Survey of the Shores of the Ocean]。他希望把这些奇妙的经历讲给没有见过海外世界的人听。

这是全球大航海时代的先声，也是明帝国向外部世界发起的一次勇敢探索。

## Matteo Ricci in China

1601年，利玛窦进入北京。此前，他已经在中国生活多年，也逐渐理解中国社会的运作方式。

他发现，想要真正影响中国，必须先赢得士大夫的理解。曲泰素 [a Chinese scholar who helped Ricci] 便向他指出，儒学在中国社会拥有巨大影响力，要在中国立足，就必须得到儒家知识分子的认可。利玛窦听懂了这一点，于是更加努力研读儒家经典，并将自己塑造为一位学贯中西的西方学者。

他与徐光启、李之藻 [Li Zhizao, a Chinese scholar-official and Catholic convert] 等人交流密切，讨论天文、地理、数学乃至地图。中国人逐渐认识到，这位来自西方的传教士并非只会讲宗教，而是也掌握了实用的科学知识。

中国士大夫对他的接纳，远超利玛窦最初的预料。他意识到，中国并不是简单的传教对象，而是一个拥有深厚文化传统的文明世界。

## The Last Years of the Ming and New Visions

1610年10月，利玛窦去世后，葬于北京城外。朝廷批准了他的安葬请求，文武百官都来参加葬礼。他生前所求，终于得以实现。

徐光启与利玛窦的合作，不仅改变了中国知识分子的视野，也让明末中国与世界之间建立起更深的联系。历史的潮流仍在继续，而这些知识、翻译与见闻，成为一个时代最珍贵的遗产。
