# L1-02 · Tokenization and Context Windows

The first thing to unlearn about language models is the idea that they read. They don't. They process sequences of integers, and the bridge between your English sentence and those integers is a piece of machinery called a tokenizer. This sounds like an implementation detail you can safely ignore — the kind of thing a framework hides from you — but it isn't. Almost every architectural constraint you'll bump into as an AI engineer, from your monthly bill to the moment your chatbot starts forgetting what the user said ten minutes ago, traces back to this one conversion step. If you treat tokens as a curiosity, you'll build systems that behave strangely and you won't know why. If you treat them as the unit of account, the strangeness disappears.

A token is roughly four characters of English text, but that average hides a lot of variation. Common words like "the" or "and" are usually a single token. Rare words, proper nouns, code identifiers, and anything in a non-English script can blow up into many tokens per word. A URL might be ten tokens. A line of minified JavaScript might be thirty. This matters because tokens — not words, not characters, not requests — are the unit the model thinks in, the unit you're billed in, and the unit the model's working memory is measured in. Two prompts that look the same length to you can cost wildly different amounts depending on what's in them.

The mental model worth internalising is this: a model has a fixed-size window of tokens it can see at once, called the context window. Everything the model knows about your current interaction has to fit inside that window — the system message, the conversation history, the retrieved documents, the user's latest question, and the response it's about to generate. When people say a model has a "200K context window," they mean the entire conversation, top to bottom, must fit in 200,000 tokens. The window is not a memory; it's a viewport. Anything outside the viewport, the model cannot see, full stop. There is no scrolling. There is no "the model remembers our conversation from yesterday." There is only what you put in the window this call.

This is where the two halves of the topic — tokenization and context windows — collide into the central economic constraint of your system. You pay per token, both for what you send (input tokens) and what you receive (output tokens), and the pricing is usually asymmetric, with output tokens costing several times more than input. So every architectural decision you make is implicitly a decision about token spend. Stuffing every document your company owns into the prompt feels like the obvious way to make the model "know" things — and it works, briefly, until you realise you're paying for that entire dump on every single request, your latency has tripled because the model has to process all those tokens before it generates anything, and somewhere around the middle of that massive prompt the model has quietly started ignoring half of what you sent. This last failure mode, where models degrade as the context grows even within the official limit, is the unglamorous reality behind the marketing claims of million-token windows.

Once the constraint is real to you, the patterns that have grown up around it start to make sense. Retrieval-Augmented Generation exists because you can't fit everything in the window, so you fetch only the relevant slice at inference time. Conversation summarisation exists because long chats outgrow the window, so you compress the old turns into a paragraph and discard the rest. Streaming exists because users would rather watch tokens arrive one at a time than stare at a spinner for thirty seconds. Batching exists because if you're going to pay round-trip latency, you might as well send several requests at once. None of these are arbitrary engineering tricks. They are direct responses to the fact that tokens are finite, metered, and degrade in usefulness as they accumulate.

The practical skill this topic builds is the habit of counting before you send. Before you ship a prompt, you should know roughly how many tokens it consumes, how that scales with the size of the user's input or the retrieved context, and where in the request budget you're spending most of it. The tooling for this is mundane — libraries like tiktoken let you count tokens for a given model in a few lines of code — but the discipline is everything. Engineers who don't count are the ones who get a surprise bill at the end of the month, or who discover in production that their context window overflows on the longest customer messages, or who can't explain why one user's session costs ten times more than another's. Engineers who do count treat the prompt as a budgeted resource, the same way a backend engineer treats a database query or a network round trip. That shift in framing — from "the prompt is just text I write" to "the prompt is a metered payload with a hard ceiling" — is what this topic is really teaching you.

## Level 2 candidates

**Token Encoding (Tiktoken)** — How text becomes integers, why different model families use different tokenizers, and how to count tokens programmatically before you send a request. Worth a deeper dive because the encoding choices explain real puzzles — why non-English text is more expensive, why code tokenizes oddly, why a "short" prompt in your editor can blow your budget.

**Context Window Limits** — The maximum number of tokens a model can ingest in one call, how that figure has expanded from a few thousand to hundreds of thousands over a handful of years, and how it became a primary axis of differentiation between models. Worth depth because the headline window size and the *usable* window size are not the same number, and knowing the difference shapes how you design retrieval, summarisation, and conversation management.

**The Cost-Context Tradeoff** — Why stuffing more context into every prompt feels efficient but is actually expensive, latent, and quality-degrading. Worth depth because it's the central architectural decision behind RAG, prompt caching, and conversation summarisation, and getting it wrong is the most common way to ship a system that's both slow and broke.

**Batching and Streaming** — Requesting multiple completions in parallel versus receiving tokens one at a time, and when each is the right tool for latency, throughput, or user experience. Worth depth because the choice ripples into your application architecture — streaming changes how you write your frontend, batching changes how you structure your backend job queue.

**Context Degradation and the "Lost in the Middle" Problem** — The empirical finding that models attend more reliably to the start and end of long contexts than to the middle, even when everything technically fits. Worth depth because it forces a reframing: the context window is not a flat memory but a weighted one, and the position of information inside the prompt is itself an architectural decision.
