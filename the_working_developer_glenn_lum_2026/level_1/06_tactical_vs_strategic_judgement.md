# L1-06 · Tactical vs Strategic Judgment

Every engineer has a default mode, and most of them don't know what theirs is. One engineer reaches for the quick path by reflex — patch it, ship it, move on — and accumulates a quiet trail of shortcuts that some future version of themselves will have to live in. Another reaches for the careful path by reflex — generalize it, test it properly, leave it cleaner than you found it — and ships half as much as their peers while wondering why. Both think they're being professional. Both are wrong about half the time, and the half they're wrong about is invisible to them because they never made the call deliberately in the first place.

Ousterhout drew the line cleanly in *A Philosophy of Software Design*: tactical programming optimises for getting the current thing working; strategic programming invests in a design that will stay workable as the system grows. As a principle on the page it sounds like an obvious recommendation in favour of the strategic mode — of course you should invest in good design. But the principle isn't the skill. The skill is the calibration: knowing, at the moment of writing, which mode this particular piece of code deserves, and being honest with yourself about which one you're actually choosing. That decision is not made once when you start a project. It is made dozens of times a day, often under deadline pressure, and almost always implicitly.

The reason the calibration matters is that the right answer genuinely flips depending on what the code is. A throwaway script that runs once to migrate some data and is then deleted does not deserve abstraction, dependency injection, or unit tests; spending that effort on it is not professionalism, it's miscalibration. A load-bearing module that ten other systems depend on and that will be edited weekly for the next five years deserves exactly the opposite: the time you spend now thinking about its shape pays back compounding dividends every time someone touches it. Treating these two pieces of code the same way — in either direction — is the costly mistake. The engineer who applies strategic care to everything ships too slowly to matter. The engineer who applies tactical speed to everything eventually drowns in their own mess, because tactical debt accumulates non-linearly and the interest comes due at the worst possible time.

What makes this hard is that the decision is rarely framed honestly. Most engineers have a moral relationship with this choice rather than an economic one — they think of strategic work as virtuous and tactical work as a guilty compromise, or the inverse, that careful design is gold-plating and real engineers ship. Both framings hide the actual question, which is straightforwardly economic: how long will this code live, how many people will touch it, how central is it to the things that matter, and what is the cost of the shortcut compared to the cost of the investment? An honest tactical choice — "this is quick and dirty, I know it's quick and dirty, here is the marker I'm leaving so we revisit it" — is professional. The same shortcut taken by reflex and forgotten about is how messes accrete silently, one defensible decision at a time.

The mental shift that makes this a real skill rather than a vague preference is recognising tactical-vs-strategic as a meta-decision that sits upstream of nearly every other call you make on a task. Your estimate depends on which mode you're choosing. Your scoping depends on it. The amount of time you spend reading the surrounding code depends on it. Whether you write a test first, last, or at all depends on it. If you've already decided implicitly — by habit — then all the downstream decisions inherit your default, including its blind spots. If you decide explicitly, even briefly, you stay in control of where your effort actually goes.

The cost of getting this wrong compounds in two opposite directions, and recognising both is what protects you from over-correcting. Always-tactical engineers eventually find that every change in their codebase takes longer than the last, that they are afraid of the systems they themselves built, and that the shortcuts they took for speed have become the reason nothing is fast anymore. Always-strategic engineers find that they are reliably the bottleneck, that their colleagues route around them, and that work they did "properly" was thrown away before the careful design ever paid back. Neither failure mode is visible while it's happening; both are obvious in retrospect, which is why naming the meta-skill matters. You can't correct a habit you haven't noticed.

What mastery looks like, then, is not picking a side. It is making the call deliberately, holding both modes available, and being able to articulate to yourself — and to a reviewer, or a future maintainer, or yourself in six months — why this particular piece of code got this particular treatment. It looks like deliberately choosing the quick-and-dirty path with full awareness of the cost when speed is genuinely what the situation calls for, and deliberately choosing the careful path when the code's lifespan and centrality justify it. The instinct is fine; the instinct is probably already mostly right. What changes when you bring it from instinct into deliberate practice is that you stop being wrong by default in the cases where your habit doesn't fit, and you start being able to defend the call you made — which is most of what it takes to be trusted with bigger ones.

## Level 2 candidates

**Calibrating Investment to the Code's Lifespan** — How long the code will live and how many people will touch it should drive how much design effort it deserves. Worth a deep dive because it replaces a moral instinct ("always do it right") with an economic one, and most engineers have never actually framed the question this way.

**Recognising Load-Bearing Code** — Some code is structurally central to a system and some is peripheral or disposable, and the cues that distinguish them — fan-in, change frequency, blast radius — are learnable. Worth deeper treatment because the call is only as good as your ability to identify which kind of code you're touching, and that identification is itself a skill.

**The Honest Shortcut** — Taking the quick path knowing it's a quick path, leaving a marker, and intending to revisit it is professional; taking it by default and forgetting is how messes accrete. Worth its own post because the practices that make a shortcut honest — the marker, the ticket, the comment that names the debt — are concrete and underused.

**Good Enough Is a Real Engineering Target** — "Good enough" is not a compromise of standards but a calibration of them to context, and treating it as a legitimate target is what frees you from gold-plating work that doesn't warrant it. Worth deeper exploration because most engineers' relationship to this idea is uncomfortable, and the discomfort itself is what causes miscalibration.

**The Compounding Cost of Defaulting to One Mode** — Always-tactical and always-strategic are both failure modes, and each compounds differently over months and years. Worth a deep dive because seeing the long-term shape of each failure is what motivates the discipline of deciding deliberately rather than defaulting.

#### L1-06 · Tactical vs Strategic Judgment

**What it is and why it matters:** Ousterhout draws the line between tactical programming (get this working now) and strategic programming (invest in a design that stays workable) as a principle in *A Philosophy of Software Design* — but on the job it isn't a principle, it's a decision you make a dozen times a day under pressure. The real skill is calibration: knowing *when* the quick path is correct and when it's a loan you'll repay with interest, and being honest about which one you're actually choosing. A throwaway script and a load-bearing module deserve opposite answers, and the engineer who applies strategic care to everything ships too slowly while the one who applies tactical speed to everything drowns in their own mess. This is the meta-decision that governs how you spend effort on every other task on this map. Mastery looks like deliberately choosing the quick-and-dirty path with full awareness of the cost, rather than defaulting to whichever mode is your habit.
