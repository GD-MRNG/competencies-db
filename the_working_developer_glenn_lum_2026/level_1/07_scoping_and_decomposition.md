# L1-07 · Scoping and Decomposition

The largest single predictor of whether a change ships cleanly is not how well it's written or how thoroughly it's tested — it's how big it is. A change that takes a day to make and an hour to review lands. A change that takes three weeks to make and a day to review becomes a negotiation, then a standoff, then a merge nightmare, then — often — an abandoned branch and a quiet retreat. The size of the unit of work is itself an engineering decision, and most engineers make it by accident, taking on whatever size the task seems to naturally suggest and then suffering whatever consequences follow. Decomposition is the skill of making that decision deliberately.

The reframe that unlocks this is to stop thinking about a change as a single object you'll deliver when it's done, and start thinking about it as a sequence of states the system will pass through, each of which has to be coherent on its own. A big change made all at once is the riskiest object in software for reasons that compound: it's hard to review (the reviewer's attention degrades sharply with diff size), hard to test (every component change interacts with every other), hard to revert (rolling back means losing weeks of unrelated work entangled in the same branch), and it blocks everyone behind it until it lands. The same total work, sliced into a sequence of small, independently-shippable, individually-reversible steps — each of which leaves the system working — is a fundamentally different kind of object. It's not just less risky; it's risky in a way you can actually manage, because the blast radius of any one step is bounded.

The deepest move in this skill, and the one most engineers get backwards, is slicing vertically rather than horizontally. The instinct — especially for engineers trained to think in clean architectures — is to do all the database work first, then all the backend, then the API, then the frontend. This feels orderly. It is also the worst way to slice a feature, because it pushes every form of feedback to the very end: you don't know whether the schema is right until you try to expose it through the API, you don't know whether the API is right until you try to consume it from the frontend, and you don't know whether anything is right until a real user touches it. A vertical slice — one user-visible behaviour, end to end, however thin — delivers something real on day one and exposes every layer to scrutiny while it's still cheap to change. The horizontal version doesn't ship until everything ships. The vertical version ships repeatedly and learns as it goes.

This is what makes feature flags, incremental migration, and continuous delivery possible at all. None of these techniques work on a system where shipping requires a fortnight of integrated work to land coherently. They all assume you can decouple "the code is deployed" from "the feature is on," ship dark, ship behind a flag, ship one user at a time — and that assumption rests entirely on whether the work was decomposed in a way that allows it. Feature flags are not a workaround for bad decomposition; they're a tool that only pays off if you've decomposed well enough to use them.

The other half of decomposition is sequencing — in what order do you do the small steps. The instinct is to do the easy parts first, partly because they feel like progress and partly because the hard parts are intimidating. This is exactly backwards. The valuable thing about a sequence of small steps is that it lets you discover whether your plan is doomed while it's still cheap to change, and you only get that benefit if the riskiest, most uncertain step is near the front. Front-loading the unknown — a spike on the integration you've never done, a prototype of the algorithm you're not sure scales, a probe at the third-party API whose docs are vague — means that if your assumptions are wrong, you find out in week one rather than week six. Engineers who sequence by ease end up discovering the deal-breaker after they've already invested in everything that depended on it.

The cost of getting this wrong has a specific shape, and you'll recognise it. The long-lived branch is the canonical failure mode: a piece of work that started as "this should take a few days" and has now lived for three weeks, accumulating merge conflicts with main, drifting from the codebase's evolving conventions, growing in scope as new requirements get folded in because rebasing them out is too painful. Every day it lives, it gets harder to land, and the rational response — to land it as-is and clean up later — gets weaker because the diff is now too large for any single human to review with care. This is the disaster that decomposition prevents, and the reason engineers who can decompose are visibly faster than those who can't, even when their raw coding speed is identical.

The skill, ultimately, is a kind of taste — the ability to look at a piece of work and see the natural fault lines along which it can be cleaved into pieces that each leave the system in a working state. It's learnable, but it's learned by doing, and the way to start is to be suspicious of any change that's going to take more than a day or two as a single unit. That suspicion — the reflex to ask "what's the smallest version of this I could ship today?" — is what separates engineers whose work flows from engineers whose work piles up.

## Level 2 candidates

**Vertical Slices vs Horizontal Layers** — Why slicing work end-to-end through every layer beats completing each layer in turn, and what changes when you do. Worth deeper treatment because the horizontal instinct is deeply ingrained — it feels like good engineering — and unwinding it requires more than the high-level argument; it requires concrete examples of how a vertical slice actually gets shaped when the layers don't yet exist.

**Keeping the System Shippable at Every Step** — The discipline of decomposing such that main is releasable after every increment, never relying on "we'll fix it before release." Worth a deeper post because this is the precondition for continuous delivery and trunk-based development, and the techniques that make it possible (parallel implementations, expand-then-contract migrations, dark launches) are non-obvious and rarely taught directly.

**Feature Flags: Decoupling Deploy From Release** — The technique of separating code deployment from feature activation, and the operational discipline that makes flags an asset rather than a maintenance burden. Worth its own treatment because flags are widely used and widely abused — the failure modes (flag debt, untested combinations, flags that become permanent config) are as instructive as the wins.

**The Cost of the Long-Lived Branch** — A close look at why a branch that lives for weeks is a compounding liability — merge conflict, drift, scope creep, review fatigue — and why the cost is non-linear in time. Worth deeper coverage because the dynamics are quantifiable, and seeing the actual shape of the cost curve is what convinces engineers to pay the up-front cost of better decomposition.

**Sequencing for Early Risk Reduction** — The judgment of which step to do first, and why front-loading the riskiest unknown beats easing in with the parts you already understand. Worth its own post because this is where decomposition meets project planning, and the heuristics for identifying the dominant risk in a piece of work are a distinct skill from breaking the work down in the first place.

**Knowing When Not to Decompose** — The cases where the overhead of small steps exceeds the risk of the big change: throwaway scripts, atomic refactors that genuinely can't be split, time-boxed spikes. Worth a Level 2 because the discipline is most credible when its limits are explicit, and the engineer who decomposes everything as a matter of religion is its own failure mode.
