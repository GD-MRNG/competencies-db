# L1-10 · Technical Debt as a Managed Liability

The phrase "technical debt" has been so thoroughly diluted that it now means, in most engineering conversations, "code I don't like." That is not what Ward Cunningham meant in 1992, and the gap between what he meant and what the industry has done with the term is the reason most arguments about debt go nowhere. Cunningham was not making a moral point about messy code. He was making a financial argument to non-engineers about a specific, deliberate move: shipping software before you fully understand the domain, and treating the resulting mismatch between code and reality as a loan you took on consciously and intend to repay through refactoring as you learn. The metaphor was an instrument for talking to the business, not a label for engineers' aesthetic complaints.

Recovering that original meaning matters because it changes what debt is for. In the corrupted version, debt is a failure — something that accumulated because someone was lazy or rushed, something to feel guilty about and clean up when there's time (which there never is). In Cunningham's version, debt is a tool. Sometimes the right move is to borrow against the future to ship now: you don't yet understand the domain well enough to model it correctly, the market won't wait, and the only way to learn what the right design even looks like is to put something in users' hands. Taking on debt in that situation is not a failure of discipline; it is the discipline. The failure is taking the loan and then forgetting you took it.

The financial frame holds up under pressure because it forces you to name two things at once: the principal (the wrong-shape code you shipped) and the interest (the extra cost every future change pays because of it). Code with debt isn't broken — it works, that's why it shipped — but every modification in that area is more expensive than it should be, and the surcharge compounds silently. This is why systems slow to a crawl while everyone is working as hard as ever: the team isn't getting worse, the interest payments on accumulated debt are eating an ever-larger fraction of every sprint. Meanwhile, debt in code that nobody touches costs almost nothing — the principal is owed but no interest accrues. This is the distinction that tells you where cleanup actually pays.

Not all debt is the same kind, and the most useful refinement of Cunningham's frame is Martin Fowler's quadrant: debt is either deliberate or inadvertent, and either prudent or reckless. Deliberate-prudent is the canonical case — you knew the right design, judged that shipping now was worth the rework, and took the loan with intent. Deliberate-reckless is "we don't have time for design" as a permanent posture. Inadvertent-prudent is what you discover after the fact: "now that we've shipped, I see the model we should have used." Inadvertent-reckless is what people usually mean by tech debt — the mess that accreted because nobody knew better and nobody was tracking. These are four different things with four different remedies, and treating them as one category is how the conversation collapses.

The operational skill, separate from understanding the metaphor, is making debt visible. Debt that lives only in engineers' heads cannot be prioritised against features, because the people allocating time can't see it. The work here is unglamorous: tracking the shortcuts you took with markers in the code or the issue tracker, naming the regions of the system where changes are routinely two or three times more expensive than they should be, mapping that surcharge to delivery so the cost is in the same units as the features it competes with. None of this is exciting, and most engineers don't do it, which is why most teams' debt is invisible to everyone except the people paying it.

The hardest part is articulating debt's cost in terms the business can weigh. "This code is ugly" loses every argument it enters, and rightly so — it sounds like an aesthetic complaint, because it is one. "This module adds two days to every change in the checkout area, and we change it about twice a month" wins, because it is denominated in the currency the business already uses to think about its work. The reframe gets you out of asking permission to gold-plate and into proposing an investment with a calculable return. It also forces you to be honest with yourself about which cleanup actually matters. A lot of refactoring engineers want to do is in stable, rarely-touched code where the interest rate is approximately zero, and a lot of code that genuinely is costing the business is in regions that don't offend anyone's taste. The instinct and the economics often disagree.

What the topic builds, in the end, is a way of arguing for engineering quality that doesn't depend on the listener already valuing engineering quality. You stop pleading and start pricing. You stop labelling code as bad and start describing the surcharge it imposes on every story that touches it. You take debt on deliberately when the situation calls for it, and you keep a ledger so the loan doesn't disappear into folklore. The engineers who can do this get cleanup funded; the ones who can't watch their systems calcify while every quarterly plan has no room for the work that would unblock the next four quarters of plans. The debt metaphor is not just a way to talk about code — it is a way to keep code from becoming the thing nobody can change.

**Cunningham's Original Meaning vs the Common Misuse** — Recovers the 1992 framing where debt names the gap between shipped code and an evolving understanding of the domain, repaid by refactoring as you learn, rather than a synonym for sloppy code. Worth a deeper post because the original meaning changes when taking on debt is a wise move rather than a confession of laziness, and most engineers have only ever encountered the corrupted version.

**Deliberate vs Inadvertent, Prudent vs Reckless** — Walks through Fowler's quadrant with concrete examples of each cell and the different remedies they call for. Worth going deeper because the vocabulary lets you defend a specific shortcut as professional or condemn one as reckless without the conversation collapsing into "all debt is bad" or "all debt is fine."

**Making Debt Visible** — Covers the practices that surface debt out of engineers' heads and into a form the organisation can prioritise: code markers, dedicated tracking, mapping debt to slowed delivery in specific regions. Worth a deep dive because invisible debt is unprioritisable debt, and the mechanics of visibility are concrete enough to teach but rarely taught.

**Pricing Debt in the Business's Terms** — How to translate a refactoring case from aesthetic complaint into delivery cost — naming the surcharge per change, the frequency of change, and the resulting drag on roadmap velocity. Worth deeper treatment because this is the specific rhetorical move that gets paydown funded, and most engineers have never been shown how to make it.

**Interest vs Principal: When Paydown Is Worth It** — Distinguishes the principal owed (the wrong-shape code) from the interest paid (the surcharge on every change in that area), and uses the distinction to direct cleanup at hot paths rather than stable backwaters. Worth going deeper because it explains why the refactoring engineers most want to do is often the least valuable, and vice versa — a counterintuitive result with real consequences for where you spend cleanup budget.
