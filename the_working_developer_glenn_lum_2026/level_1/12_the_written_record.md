# L1-12 · The Written Record

The career ceiling for a strong engineer who can't write is lower than they think, and they almost never see it as a writing problem. They see it as politics, or bad luck, or a manager who doesn't get it. What actually happened is that the decisions that mattered were made in documents they didn't write, in threads they couldn't shape, and in tickets that framed the problem before they arrived to solve it. Software organisations run on writing far more than newcomers expect, and the engineers who treat writing as a soft skill discover, slowly, that it was the load-bearing one all along.

Start with where decisions actually live. The architecture of a system is not decided at the whiteboard; it is decided in the design doc that circulates the week before, and the whiteboard session merely ratifies what the doc has already converged on. The scope of a project is not decided in standup; it is decided in the ticket whose wording quietly determined what counted as done. The fix you shipped is not remembered as you described it in chat; it is remembered as the PR description, which is the only artifact anyone will read in six months when they're trying to understand why this code looks like this. If you are not present in the writing, you are not present in the decision. The room where it happens is, increasingly, a Google Doc.

Remote and async work have made this sharper, not different. When everyone sat in the same office, a poor writer could compensate by being persuasive in person and clarifying in conversation; the writing was a transcript of the meeting that mattered. In a distributed team, the writing *is* the meeting. A message that requires a follow-up to be understood has cost a round-trip across timezones. A design doc that doesn't anticipate the obvious objections has cost a week of back-and-forth comments. The quality of your writing is no longer a personal trait; it is the bandwidth of your collaboration with everyone you don't share a room with.

The deeper reframe — the one that changes how you write rather than just whether — is that a design doc is not documentation. Documentation describes a thing that exists. A design doc exists to surface a decision before it is made, while changing it is still cheap, and to expose it to the people who will challenge it. Its job is persuasion: to make the proposal, the alternatives, and the tradeoffs legible enough that disagreement can happen on the page instead of in a meeting after the code is written. The expensive argument is the one that arrives after implementation; the design doc is the cheap argument that prevents it. Engineers who write design docs as retrospective descriptions of what they've already decided to build have missed the point and will keep being surprised when alignment evaporates the moment the PR goes up.

The other half of writing is the part that serves your future self and the people who come after you. Code answers what; comments, decision records, and postmortems answer why. The why is the part that decays first and is missed most. A comment that explains the strange-looking branch — not what it does, which the code already says, but why this case exists at all — is the difference between a successor who removes it cautiously and one who removes it confidently. A decision record that captures why you chose this database over that one is what stops the team re-litigating the same question every eighteen months when someone new joins and finds the choice surprising. A postmortem that articulates the systemic lesson, not the chronology, is how an outage stops being trauma and starts being knowledge the org actually keeps. Writing of this kind is how knowledge survives the engineer who held it, and most of what makes a codebase legible to its tenth maintainer is the writing the first one bothered to do.

There is a craft to all of this that is unevenly distributed and rarely taught. The async message that doesn't need a follow-up — the one that states the ask, the context, and the constraints in a single pass — is a learned skill, not a personality trait. So is writing to be skimmed: structuring a document so that a busy reader who reads only the first sentence of each paragraph still leaves with the right impression, because that is in fact how most of your readers will read it. So is the discipline of writing the *why* in a PR description rather than restating the diff. None of these are exotic — they are just the moves you stop making when you treat writing as overhead rather than as the work itself.

The practical consequence is this: writing is a force multiplier in a way that almost no other skill is, because it scales to readers you will never meet and to times when you are not present to clarify. A good design doc is read by people who weren't in the original conversation and aligns them anyway. A good comment is read by an engineer who joins three years after you leave. A good postmortem is cited in a planning meeting you will never attend. The leverage is not that writing makes you faster at any single task; it is that what you write keeps working when you aren't there. Engineers who learn this stop treating writing as the thing they do after the work and start treating it as part of the work that does the most.

## Level 2 candidates

**The Design Doc as Persuasion, Not Documentation** — Covers what a design doc is actually for: surfacing a decision while it can still cheaply change, structuring the alternatives and tradeoffs, and writing it so the disagreement happens on the page. Worth a deep dive because most engineers write design docs as descriptions rather than arguments, and the shift in framing changes both what they include and when they circulate it.

**The Async Message That Doesn't Need a Follow-Up** — Covers the moves that make a single message land — anticipating the obvious questions, stating the ask explicitly, giving enough context without burying it. Worth deeper treatment because in a distributed team this is the highest-frequency writing you do, and small per-message gains compound across thousands of interactions.

**Decision Records: Capturing Why, Not Just What** — Covers the practice of recording the reasoning behind a decision so it survives the people who made it, including lightweight formats like ADRs. Worth its own post because the failure mode is specific and recurring — teams re-litigate settled questions every year because the why was never written down — and the techniques to prevent it are concrete.

**Comments That Explain Intent** — Covers the distinction between comments that restate the code and comments that capture the why the code can't, and how to tell the difference. Worth deeper exploration because it connects to a long-standing argument in the canon about what comments are for, and the practical heuristic — write what wouldn't be obvious to a careful reader of the code — is easy to teach and easy to get wrong.

**Writing to Be Skimmed** — Covers the structural craft of making a document survive the reader who scans it: leading paragraphs with their conclusion, using headings as a navigable spine, putting the ask up front. Worth a separate treatment because it is a distinct skill from writing to be read in full, and most engineers default to the latter while their actual readers are doing the former.

**The Postmortem as Durable Knowledge** — Covers how to write up an incident so the lesson outlives the immediate context — what to include, what to leave out, and how to extract the systemic finding rather than the chronology. Worth its own post because it sits at the intersection of writing and incident response and is where most of the learning value of an outage is either captured or lost.
