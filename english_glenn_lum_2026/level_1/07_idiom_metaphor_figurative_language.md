# L1-07 · Idiom, Metaphor, and Figurative Language

The mistake most fluent speakers make about figurative language is treating it as decoration — the optional spice on top of literal meaning, the part of English you can safely ignore if you just want to get your point across. This gets the relationship exactly backwards. A large portion of everyday English, and a larger portion of professional English, is figurative at its core. When your manager talks about moving the goalposts, drilling down into a problem, or getting buy-in from stakeholders, they are not reaching for ornament. They are using the only words available for what they mean. Strip the figurative layer out and you are not left with cleaner English — you are left with English that nobody actually speaks.

This matters in a specific way for a Singapore English speaker. You have spent your life immersed in global English media, which means a large amount of the figurative layer is already familiar to you — you parse it on the fly without noticing. But familiarity is not the same as system. You recognise idioms when you hear them; you cannot always generate them when you need them, and you cannot always tell which ones belong in which contexts. The opaque parts are also distributed unevenly: British idioms that never made it into Singapore exposure, American sports metaphors that assume baseball or American football literacy, business jargon that has crystallised in particular industries. The result is a layer of the language you operate inside but have never mapped.

The first move toward mapping it is to stop thinking of idioms as a list and start thinking of them as a system. Most figurative language in English is not random — it runs along a small number of deep tracks. Business English borrows heavily from sports: level playing field, ballpark figure, move the goalposts, hit it out of the park, drop the ball, game plan. Political and strategic language borrows from the military: rally the troops, on the front lines, take fire, hold the line, mission-critical. Abstract reasoning borrows from physical space: high-level overview, drill down, unpack the argument, the underlying issue, surface a problem, push back. Once you see the source domains, individual idioms stop being isolated items to memorise. They become predictable extensions of a logic you already understand.

This is what conceptual metaphor means in practice. English does not just have isolated metaphors; it has whole metaphor systems that structure how speakers reason about abstract things. Argument is treated as war (defend a position, attack a claim, indefensible, shoot down an idea). Time is treated as money (spend time, waste time, invest time, can't afford the delay). The mind is treated as a container (hold an idea in mind, empty your head, full of ideas). Once you notice the pattern, you can predict the idioms. You can also extend them in ways that sound natural rather than forced, because you are working with the grain of the language rather than against it.

The next distinction worth holding is between dead and live metaphor. Some figurative expressions are so embedded that no one feels them as figurative anymore — the head of an organisation, the foot of the page, the leg of a journey, the eye of a needle. You cannot extend these creatively without sounding strange, because the metaphor has hardened into literal usage. Other expressions are live — speakers feel the source domain, and you can play with it, mix it, push it further. Knowing where a given expression sits on this spectrum tells you whether you have room to be creative with it or whether you need to use it exactly as it comes. Mixed metaphors usually sound bad because the speaker treated a live metaphor as if it were dead, or vice versa.

The third thing the system gives you is register sensitivity. Figurative language is not register-neutral. Sports and military idioms are at home in business meetings, opinion writing, and casual professional conversation, but they jar in academic prose, legal writing, and formal correspondence. Spatial metaphors travel more widely — drill down and high-level work in almost any context. Body-part idioms (got cold feet, kept a straight face, lost my head) sit comfortably in conversation and journalism but rarely in formal writing. The figurative register that sounds sharp in one room sounds wrong in another, and the cost of getting it wrong is exactly the cost of any register error: you signal that you have not read the situation.

Finally, there is the Singapore-specific layer. Singlish has its own figurative vocabulary, some of which overlaps with international English and some of which does not. On the ball means something subtly different locally than it does in international business English. Concepts like kiasu and kiasi are figurative compressions that carry weight in Singapore and have no clean equivalent elsewhere. Knowing where the figurative boundaries are — which of your figurative defaults will land internationally and which will not — is a code-switching skill, not a correction project. The goal is not to strip out Singlish idiom; it is to know which figurative register you are operating in at any given moment.

The practical payoff of treating figurative language as a system is twofold. You become a better decoder: when you hit an unfamiliar idiom, you can often work out the meaning from the source domain rather than being stuck. And you become a better producer: you can reach for figurative language deliberately, knowing what it does and where it belongs, rather than either avoiding it or deploying it by feel and hoping it lands. Literal language is rarely the precise option in English. The figurative layer is where most of the real work happens.

## Level 2 candidates

**Conceptual metaphor** — Covers the deep metaphorical systems (argument is war, time is money, the mind is a container) that structure abstract reasoning in English. Worth a deeper pass because it converts idiom learning from list-memorisation into pattern recognition, which is the difference between knowing five hundred idioms and being able to generate and decode an open-ended set.

**Dead vs live metaphor** — Covers the spectrum from fully fossilised expressions (head of an organisation) to actively felt ones, and the rules for which can be extended creatively. Worth deeper treatment because mixed metaphors and forced extensions are one of the most common ways otherwise fluent writing falls apart, and the diagnostic is not obvious from feel alone.

**Register and idiom — when figurative language lands** — Covers which figurative registers belong where: sports and military in business, spatial across most contexts, body idioms in conversation, near-zero figurative density in formal academic and legal prose. Worth deeper treatment because misjudging figurative register is one of the more visible markers of someone who has not calibrated to the room.

**Singapore English idiom vs international idiom** — Covers the figurative expressions that Singlish has developed independently or repurposed (on the ball used locally, kiasu, kiasi, and a wider set of compressions) and where they transfer or fail in international contexts. Worth deeper treatment because the conscious code-switching skill is more useful than either suppressing Singlish idiom or assuming it travels.

**Source domain literacy — sports, military, finance, mechanics** — Covers the specific source domains that generate the bulk of professional English idiom and the cultural literacy each one assumes. Worth deeper treatment because gaps in source-domain knowledge (e.g. not knowing baseball) are often what makes a specific cluster of idioms opaque, and targeted fixes are more efficient than general idiom study.
