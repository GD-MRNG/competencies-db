# L1-05 · Modularity, Coupling, and Cohesion

Every time you split a system into modules, you are making a bet: that the seam you just drew will let you understand and change one piece without holding the others in your head. That bet is what modularity is actually for. It is not about file count, not about line budgets, not about which patterns are fashionable — it is about whether a future reader can reason about a part of the system in isolation. And the entire question of whether the bet pays off comes down to two old words from the 1970s that the field has never improved on: coupling and cohesion.

The vocabulary comes from the structured-design tradition — Constantine and Yourdon — and what is striking about it half a century later is how little it has been displaced. Languages have changed, paradigms have come and gone, the architectural fashions have cycled through layers and services and microservices and back, and the durable consensus underneath all of it remains the same: minimise coupling, maximise cohesion. That is not a slogan. It is the only structural principle the field has genuinely converged on, and the reason it has survived is that it ties directly to the deepest thing in the canon — the war on complexity. Modules exist to fight complexity by isolating it. Coupling and cohesion are the two ways that isolation succeeds or fails.

Coupling is the simpler of the two to grasp: it is dependency made concrete. Two modules are coupled to the extent that you cannot understand or change one without understanding or changing the other. Every coupling is, in effect, a thread tying two pieces of the system together so that pulling on one tugs the other. A small amount of coupling is unavoidable — modules have to talk to do useful work — but every additional thread is a future cost paid by everyone who has to reason about either end. The whole point of drawing a module boundary is to reduce the number of threads crossing it, because a loosely coupled module is one you can pick up, study, and modify on its own. A tightly coupled one drags its neighbours into every conversation about it. When people complain that a change "rippled through the codebase," what they are describing is coupling that was invisible until the change forced it into view.

Cohesion is the other half, and it is the one practitioners more often get wrong. Cohesion measures how single-purpose a module is — how much its contents belong together because they all serve one clear responsibility. A highly cohesive module does one thing, and that one thing has an obvious home: when a change comes in for that responsibility, you know exactly where to make it. A low-cohesion module is a grab-bag — a `utils` file, a `helpers` folder, a `Manager` class that accreted methods because nothing else seemed to want them — and the cost of that grab-bag is paid every time you need to change anything inside it, because to change one piece you have to mentally process the whole, and you can never be sure your change won't disturb something unrelated that happens to live in the same room. Cohesion is the property that makes "where does this code go?" answerable without negotiation.

The two work as a pair, and they tend to move together. When a module is incohesive, it almost always couples to too many other things, because a module that does several different things has reasons to talk to several different neighbours. When you tighten cohesion — when you split the grab-bag into modules that each do one thing — the couplings tend to sort themselves into the right places, because each thread now has a sensible home to attach to. This is why "low coupling, high cohesion" is treated as a single principle rather than two: in practice, the same redesign usually improves both, and a redesign that improves one while making the other worse is usually wrong.

The reason this all matters — the reason it sits at the foundation of structural judgment — is that most of the architectural problems you will ever meet are a coupling or cohesion problem wearing a costume. The "god class" is a cohesion failure. "Shotgun surgery" — having to change five files to make one logical change — is a coupling failure. "Spaghetti code" is both at once. Layering violations, circular dependencies, leaky abstractions, fragile tests that break on unrelated changes: trace any of them back far enough and you find a module that took on too many responsibilities, or a boundary that let too many threads through, or both. The vocabulary is so durable because the underlying physics has not changed: code that humans have to understand is hard in proportion to how much of it they have to hold in their head at once, and modularity is the discipline of letting them hold less.

What this topic gives you is not a rule but a diagnostic. When something in a codebase feels wrong and you cannot yet say why, "is this a coupling problem or a cohesion problem?" is the first question that will resolve the fog into a specific, fixable shape. The answer almost always points at a boundary that is in the wrong place — drawn around the wrong responsibilities, or porous to the wrong dependencies — and once you can see the boundary, you can move it. Everything else in the structural tier is a refinement of this: where exactly to cut, how deep each module should be, what the interface across the cut should promise. But the foundation is here. Get coupling and cohesion right and the rest of the tier becomes tractable. Get them wrong and no amount of clean naming or careful function decomposition will save the design — you will just have a tidy surface over a tangled structure.

## Level 2 candidates

**Coupling as Dependency Made Visible** — Drilling into how every coupling is literally a reason two pieces of code must be understood together, with concrete examples of forms coupling takes (data, control, content). Worth depth because reframing "reduce coupling" from slogan to mechanism changes how you read code — you start to see the threads, not just the modules.

**Cohesion and the One-Thing Module** — A closer look at why single-purpose modules give every change an obvious home, and how `utils` and `helpers` modules become dumping grounds that resist all reasoning. Worth depth because the failure mode is so common and so quietly destructive that naming it precisely changes day-to-day decisions about where new code goes.

**The Debate: When to Split and When to Merge** — Ousterhout's warning that over-splitting creates as much complexity as under-splitting, and the recognition that code that always changes together often belongs together. Worth depth because it tensions the reflex to decompose everything, and the synthesis — split by what changes independently, not by what looks separable — is genuinely non-obvious.

**Temporal and Logical Coupling** — Dependencies that live not in the code but in the requirement that things change at the same time, or in shared assumptions that no interface declares. Worth depth because hidden coupling is the explanation for changes that mysteriously break distant code, and the static view of a codebase will never reveal it.

**Connascence: A Finer Vocabulary for Coupling** — The connascence taxonomy grades coupling along axes of strength, degree, and locality, distinguishing forms (name, type, meaning, position, algorithm, timing, identity) that the binary "coupled or not" obscures. Worth depth because once you have the finer vocabulary, you can see which couplings are cheap to live with and which are quietly expensive — a sharper tool than the consensus principle alone provides.
