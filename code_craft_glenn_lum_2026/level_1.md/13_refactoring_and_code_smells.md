# L1-13 · Refactoring and Code Smells

Most developers refactor every week and have never once refactored in the technical sense. They mean "I rewrote that messy bit and it seems to still work," which is a different activity with a similar name and a much worse risk profile. The word that Opdyke gave the field in his 1992 thesis, and Fowler popularised in 1999, has a precise meaning, and the precision is the whole point: refactoring is a behaviour-preserving transformation of internal structure, made in steps small enough that you can prove, after each one, that behaviour didn't change. Strip any of those qualifiers — behaviour-preserving, small steps, proof — and you are no longer refactoring. You are rewriting and hoping.

The reason the precise definition matters is that it identifies the dependency the practice rests on. You cannot prove behaviour is unchanged by squinting at the diff; you prove it by running tests that exercise the behaviour and watching them stay green. Tests are not a nice-to-have alongside refactoring — they are the mechanism that makes refactoring a different activity from rewriting. Without them, every structural change is a small bet that you understood the code well enough not to break anything, and those bets compound into the kind of disasters that get refactoring blamed for breakage. With them, you can improve a design continuously, in tiny increments, instead of letting design debt accumulate until the only remaining option is the big-bang rewrite that everyone knows is going to be terrifying and is going to take three times as long as planned.

The small-steps discipline is the part most often discarded under deadline pressure, and it's the part that does the most work. A refactoring you can describe as a single named move — extract method, inline variable, replace conditional with polymorphism — can be performed, tested, and committed in minutes, and if something breaks, the blast radius is one move. Five moves stacked together before the test run is not five times as risky; it is the kind of risky where you can no longer tell which move broke things and your only recovery is to revert the lot. The small step isn't a stylistic preference. It's how you keep the safety net taut.

The companion gift from Beck and Fowler is the catalogue of code smells, and its value is almost entirely linguistic. Before the catalogue, you had the intuition that something was wrong with a piece of code but no way to say what — which meant you couldn't argue for fixing it, couldn't get a colleague to agree with you about it, and often couldn't even articulate it to yourself well enough to act. After the catalogue, you have words: long method, feature envy, shotgun surgery, primitive obsession, divergent change. Naming a smell turns "this feels off" into "this is feature envy because the method spends more time talking to another object's data than its own," which is a discussable, fixable, communicable diagnosis. A team that shares the vocabulary can debate design without falling back on taste.

What ties this topic into the rest of the canon is that smells are not their own category of problem. They are symptoms of the complexity from Tier 1 — dependencies and obscurity, change amplification and cognitive load — surfacing in code at a scale you can see. Long method is cognitive load made local. Shotgun surgery is change amplification with a name. Feature envy is misplaced cohesion. Primitive obsession is missing abstraction. Once you see this, the catalogue stops being a list of pet peeves and becomes a diagnostic instrument: each smell points at which complexity is leaking and roughly which refactoring drains it. The named refactorings are the moves; the named smells are the cues that tell you which move to make.

Two heuristics govern when to apply this in practice, and they pull in opposite directions in a useful way. The rule of three says don't extract on first sight — wait until a pattern has shown up three times before you abstract it, because the wrong abstraction is more expensive than the duplication it was meant to fix. The boy scout rule says leave the code a little cleaner than you found it every time you touch it. Together they describe the only cleanup strategy that reliably survives contact with deadlines: not the dedicated refactoring sprint that perpetually slips, but small, opportunistic improvements made in the area you were already changing for some other reason, paid for out of the budget you already had. This is how design debt actually gets paid down in real codebases. Anything else is a plan, and plans for cleanup lose to feature work every time.

The discipline also carries a sharp edge that the literature is quieter about: refactoring stable, rarely-touched, working code is often a net negative. The risk of breakage is real even with tests, the cleanup yields no near-term value because nobody is reading or changing that code anyway, and you are spending review and cognitive budget that could go to code that does change often. Refactor where you and your colleagues are already going. Leave alone what is working and untouched. The judgment of when not to refactor is as much a part of the skill as the moves themselves.

What this topic gives you, in the end, is a way to improve a design without having to stop and rebuild it. The rewrite-from-scratch fantasy is so attractive precisely because it is the only option visible to a team that doesn't refactor — when small change is impossible, large change becomes the only change. The discipline of behaviour-preserving steps, with a shared vocabulary for what's wrong and a tests-backed proof that you didn't break it, is what keeps the small change available. That, more than any individual move in the catalogue, is what the practice exists to protect.

## Level 2 candidates

**Refactoring as Behaviour-Preserving Change** — The technical definition of refactoring as small, behaviour-preserving transformations verified by tests, distinguished from rewriting. Worth a deep dive because the discipline of small steps is exactly what gets discarded under pressure, and the failure mode (refactoring blamed for breakage that came from undisciplined rewriting) is widespread and confusable.

**Code Smells as a Shared Vocabulary** — A tour through the canonical smells (long method, feature envy, shotgun surgery, primitive obsession, divergent change) and what each one diagnoses about the underlying complexity. Worth depth because the catalogue is large enough that a single Level 1 pass can only gesture at it, and fluency with the names is what enables real design conversation on a team.

**Tests as the Refactoring Safety Net** — How tests function as the mechanism that makes structural change safe, and why this is the dependency that ties refactoring to the testing topics in this tier. Worth a deep dive because it explains why untested code resists improvement and why the testing investment pays back in design freedom, not just regression catching.

**The Boy Scout Rule and Opportunistic Cleanup** — The strategy of small improvements made within the scope of changes you were already making, contrasted with the dedicated cleanup sprint that perpetually slips. Worth depth because it is the only cleanup approach that reliably survives real deadlines, and most teams have never explicitly chosen it over the alternatives.

**When NOT to Refactor** — The judgment that stable, rarely-touched, working code is often net-negative to refactor, and the heuristics for telling that situation apart from genuine design debt. Worth a deep dive because the literature underweights it, and the instinct to clean everything you see is one of the more expensive habits a competent refactorer can develop.

**The Rule of Three and Hasty Abstraction** — The heuristic of waiting until a pattern appears three times before extracting it, and how it interacts with DRY and the wrong-abstraction problem. Worth depth because it operationalises the duplication-vs-abstraction debate from L1-04 and is one of the most-cited and least-understood rules in the canon.
