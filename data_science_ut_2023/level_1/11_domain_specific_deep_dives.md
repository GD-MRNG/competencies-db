# L1-11 · Domain-Specific Deep Dives

The temptation, once you've learned the core machinery of data science, is to believe the machinery is the job. It isn't. A gradient boosting model trained on credit card transactions and a gradient boosting model trained on Netflix viewing history share an algorithm and almost nothing else. The features are different. The success metrics are different. The failure modes are different. The adversaries — when there are adversaries — behave differently. The reason senior data scientists command a premium is not that they know more algorithms; it's that they know which problems in their domain look easy but aren't, and which look hard but have a known recipe.

You should think of domain knowledge as the layer that sits between your generic toolkit and the actual decision being made. Supervised learning gives you a way to predict; causal inference gives you a way to act; pipelines give you a way to deploy. But none of those tell you that fraud labels arrive with a six-week delay because chargebacks take that long to process, or that a recommendation system needs to balance exploration against exploitation because if you only show users what they already like, you starve the catalog and your metrics rot from the inside. These are not facts you derive from first principles. They are facts you absorb from working in the domain or from reading what people who have worked in it wrote down.

The good news is that domains are not infinite. Most data science work in industry falls into a handful of recurring shapes, and each shape has a literature, a set of canonical pitfalls, and a small number of techniques that practitioners have converged on. Recommendation systems are about predicting preference and ordering items, and they live or die on cold start, feedback loops, and how you handle the gap between implicit and explicit signals. Fraud and anomaly detection are about finding the rare and the adversarial, where the base rate is so low that accuracy is a meaningless metric and your adversary is actively trying to look normal. Time series forecasting is about data where the order matters and the future is correlated with the recent past, which breaks almost every assumption that cross-sectional methods make about independence. NLP is now mostly the art of using pre-trained models well — knowing when to fine-tune, when to prompt, when to embed and retrieve. Computer vision is similar in shape: the heavy lifting has been done by foundation models, and your job is to know what transfers and what doesn't.

What changes between domains is rarely the model. It is the structure of the data, the structure of the decision, and the structure of the cost. In recommendation, the cost of a bad prediction is a missed click, recoverable. In fraud, the cost is a fraudulent transaction that clears, often unrecoverable, and the cost of a false positive is a legitimate customer locked out of their account, which is also expensive. In forecasting demand for a supply chain, an underestimate stocks you out and an overestimate fills the warehouse — these are not symmetric, and your loss function should know that. The lesson is that the metric you optimize is a domain decision, not a technical one, and getting it wrong means optimizing the right model for the wrong objective.

You do not need to learn every domain. You need to learn one well — the one your job sits in — and you need to know enough about the others to recognize them when you see them, so you can pick up the right book rather than reinventing a solved problem. If you work in tech or media, that probably means recommendation systems. If you work in finance or e-commerce risk, it means fraud and anomaly detection. If you work in logistics, retail, or operations, it means time series and forecasting. If you work in any field touching documents, support tickets, or unstructured customer data, NLP is now table stakes. If your data is images or video, computer vision, but with the strong caveat that almost everything starts with transfer learning from a pre-trained model.

The trap to avoid is treating these domains as interchangeable applications of the same toolkit. A practitioner who has only built recommendation systems will reach for collaborative filtering when given a fraud problem, and it will quietly fail because fraud has no notion of "users who liked similar items." A practitioner who has only done forecasting will treat a recommendation problem as a regression on engagement and miss the entire ranking and exploration structure. The algorithms generalize; the framing does not.

Treat this topic, then, as a directory rather than a curriculum. The Level 2 entries below are pointers — places to go when your job hands you a problem in that shape. Read the one that matches your work now. Skim the others enough to know what they look like. The skill you are building is not breadth for its own sake; it is the ability to recognize, on day one of a new project, what kind of problem you've actually been given.

## Level 2 candidates

**Recommendation systems** — Covers how to predict and rank what users will engage with next, including collaborative filtering, content-based methods, hybrid approaches, and the role of context and sequence. Worth a deep dive because the surface problem ("what will this user like?") hides hard structural issues — cold start, feedback loops, exploration vs. exploitation, popularity bias — that determine whether the system creates value or quietly degrades the catalog.

**Fraud detection and anomaly detection** — Covers how to find rare events in highly imbalanced data where adversaries adapt to your model, including why precision-recall curves replace accuracy, how to handle delayed labels, and the role of unsupervised and semi-supervised methods. Worth going deeper because the constraints (imbalance, adversarial drift, asymmetric costs, label delay) make this one of the few domains where standard supervised learning practice will actively mislead you.

**Time series and forecasting** — Covers data where order matters and observations are not independent, including seasonality, trend decomposition, autocorrelation, and modern approaches from classical ARIMA-style methods to gradient boosting on lag features to transformer-based forecasters. Worth a deep dive because cross-sectional intuitions about train-test splits, cross-validation, and feature independence all break here, and the failures are subtle.

**NLP and text data** — Covers how language tasks are now built on pre-trained embeddings and transformer models, when to fine-tune vs. prompt vs. retrieve, and how to evaluate systems whose outputs are open-ended. Worth going deeper because the field has reorganized around foundation models since 2023, and the practical workflow — embedding, retrieval, generation — is now a distinct skill from classical text classification.

**Computer vision and images** — Covers how convolutional and now transformer-based architectures handle visual data, why transfer learning from large pre-trained models is the default starting point, and where vision problems hide subtleties (label noise, distribution shift between training and deployment cameras, adversarial examples). Worth a deep dive only if your data is actually visual, but indispensable then.
