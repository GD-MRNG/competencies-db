# Structural Companion Prompts

A structural companion is a discussion piece that follows a piece of
content — an article, a documentary transcript, a historical essay, a
primary text — and moves the reader from surface familiarity to a
working internal model of how the underlying system operates. The
companion does not summarise the content; it reconstructs the argument
with more scaffolding and more explicit connective tissue.

The premise behind all companion prompts in this family is the same:
systems — whether technical, social-political-economic, or
civilisational — have real mechanics, and surface familiarity with
terminology is not the same as understanding how those mechanics
operate. The companion's job is to make the mechanics visible.

---

## The Five-Section Structure

Every companion follows the same structure. Section names vary by
audience, but the function of each section is invariant:

1. **Why this matters** — establishes the stakes before asking the
   reader to understand anything. Names the tension, condition, or
   consequence the reader should hold in their head throughout.
2. **What you need first** — two to four prerequisite concepts,
   each explained just enough to remove the blocker. The discipline:
   if a prerequisite needs more than a short paragraph, the companion
   is going too deep.
3. **The key ideas, connected** — the central ideas of the content
   walked through in the order in which they build on each other.
   A chain, not a list. Each concept earns the next.
4. **Handles and anchors** — two or three portable instruments the
   reader can carry into future situations: analogies, comparisons
   to parallel cases, single-sentence captures, or diagnostic
   questions.
5. **What this changes** — three to five specific things the content
   has shifted or added to the reader's understanding, written with
   an example-sentence template that forces specificity over
   generality.

---

## The Discipline Instructions

Several instructions do more work than they appear to, and must be
preserved across any variant:

- **Numerical bounds.** Two to four prerequisites; three to five
  implications; two to three handles. These bounds force selection
  decisions rather than enumeration, and prevent the companion from
  truncating or ballooning.
- **The "going too deep" test.** If a prerequisite needs more than
  a short paragraph, the companion is going too deep. Stops the
  background from consuming the foreground.
- **Chain, not list.** Each concept in the central section should
  feel like it earns the next. Prevents the section from collapsing
  into disconnected paragraphs.
- **The five-minute test.** A handle passes if the reader could
  use it to explain the mechanism to someone else in five minutes
  without referring back to the content. Forces handles to be
  genuinely portable, not just memorable.
- **The example-sentence template.** The closing section names an
  explicit sentence form and lists by name the phrases to avoid
  ("this is important," "this enriches your understanding"). Naming
  the bad phrases is more effective than asking for specificity in
  the abstract.

---

## What Not To Add

- A "balanced perspectives" or "alternative views" section. The
  companion has a point of view — that the content's structural
  reading is worth holding. Adding balance dissolves the pedagogical
  function.
- A "summary" or "key takeaways" section. The companion is a
  reconstruction, not a digest.
- Open-ended invitations ("what questions do you have?", "further
  reading"). The companion is a closed pedagogical unit.

---

## How To Create A New Variant

Write an orientation paragraph at the top that names what kind of
understanding the companion is trying to produce and why that
framing is the right one for this audience. State it explicitly —
contested premises need to be named, not implied.

Then tune four things:

- **The opener** — what does "stakes" mean for this domain? Failure
  modes, material conditions, historical consequence, methodological
  risk?
- **The prerequisite kinds** — what categories of background
  knowledge matter here? Mechanisms, institutions, methods,
  materials, actors, belief systems?
- **The chain logic** — what does "this concept earns the next"
  look like in this domain? Causal, institutional, methodological,
  experiential?
- **The closing verb** — the final section name should name the
  reader's primary action: Build, Read, Investigate, Design, Map.

Add domain-specific instructions only if they are load-bearing for
the pedagogy — not as embellishment. Domain features that are
genuinely structural belong in the prompt; stylistic preferences do
not.
