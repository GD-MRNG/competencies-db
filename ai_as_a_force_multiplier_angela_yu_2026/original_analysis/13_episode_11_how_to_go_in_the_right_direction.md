# How to Go in the Right Direction

Speed and depth are useless if you're aimed at the wrong target. Most career advice quietly assumes the target is fixed — a profession, a ladder, a destination — and then optimises the climb. That assumption is breaking. In an economy where AI is collapsing the value of narrow expertise on a timescale shorter than a career, the harder question is no longer "how do I get better at my job?" but "how do I make sure the thing I'm getting better at still matters in five years?" Direction is the meta-skill, and it has its own logic.

The first thing to internalise is that your career is not a linear path. The mental image most people carry — law school, bar exam, junior associate, partner — is a fiction propped up by survivorship bias. Real trajectories are a series of detours, lateral moves, and crossovers, and the crossovers are where the value lives. Steve Jobs took a calligraphy class in college and it ended up shaping the typography of the Macintosh. A doctor who learns to teach ends up better at explaining code. The pattern holds because AI is extraordinarily good at depth within a single domain and surprisingly poor at synthesising across unrelated ones. "Jack of all trades" used to be a slur. In the age of AI, it's a defensible position — the master of one is increasingly competing with a model that has read everything ever written about that one thing.

This reframes how you should evaluate a job you're unhappy in. Before you leave, ask two questions: how much am I learning day to day, and what percentage of my time am I actually using my unique capabilities? If you're still learning, the discomfort might just be the friction before mastery, and leaving is premature. If you're not learning and not using what's distinctive about you, that's data. But "running away from a bad job" is a worse strategy than "running toward better information," and the way you get better information is not by thinking harder — it's by moving. The British SAS have a saying: when you don't know what to do, move, because movement gives you information. In careers, that means side projects, MVPs, taking on the thing your boss didn't ask for. Probing the environment beats modelling it.

The reason movement works is that effort almost always produces results — just not always the results you expected. If you put ten units of work into something and it fails, you still walk away with ten units of something: skills, contacts, evidence about what you actually like. Occasionally those ten units get transformed by a system you've built — a startup, a body of work, a reputation — into a hundred units of reward, and that non-linear scaling is the only way out of the trade-time-for-money equation. But even when it doesn't scale, no learning is wasted. The skills don't expire when you leave the field; they recombine.

To navigate well, you need a working sense of your own unique capability — the specific thing you find effortless that other people find hard. This is not the same as your job description, and it often doesn't look like an asset at first. Deep empathy can feel like a liability when you're absorbing other people's awkwardness in a meeting; it turns out to be the engine of good teaching. The exercise is to notice what you do without strain that others labour at, and then start looking for places to apply it. This is your differentiation, and it compounds, because it's the part of you that AI is least likely to commoditise.

Two psychological skills sit underneath all of this. The first is uncertainty tolerance — the ability to act without complete information. Most of us systematically overestimate risk. Whole industries (insurance being the obvious one) exist to monetise that bias. Successful people are not reckless; they're calibrated. They evaluate probability honestly and accept that no decision is zero-risk, including the decision to do nothing. The second is learning velocity, and this is the one that matters most over a long horizon. The ability to learn is a muscle. It atrophies in comfortable jobs where your day no longer requires you to figure anything new out. The grandparents who can't use an iPad and the senior professionals who can't get traction with AI didn't lose intelligence — they lost the habit of learning. If you stay good at learning, it doesn't matter how the game changes.

Two more habits are worth naming. Don't reinvent the wheel: when you're trying to figure something out, find someone five or ten years ahead of you and ask for their framework. They're close enough to remember the problem; people who are decades ahead can't relate to it anymore. And learn to say yes when other people overestimate you. The "hell yeah or no" rule works for people who are already drowning in opportunity; for everyone else, the more common failure mode is declining the thing that would have stretched you because you didn't think you were ready. If someone with skin in the game thinks you can do it, they're seeing something you're not.

The final piece is the one most people skip. All of this — the crossovers, the movement, the unique capability, the risk tolerance, the learning — is in service of a life you actually want, not the one you're supposed to want. Self-sacrifice gets coded as virtue, and it's a slow trap; if you keep overriding your own deep wants for long enough, you start to sabotage yourself in ways you can't see. Going in the right direction means the direction is yours. The tools and frameworks in the rest of this course are downstream of that choice. Get the direction right, and speed and depth start to compound. Get it wrong, and you'll just arrive somewhere you didn't want to be, faster.

## Why This Conversation Is Happening

It is possible to become more efficient, more disciplined, and more technically capable while still moving toward the wrong life or the wrong career bet. That is the core problem this piece is trying to solve. In a world where AI can rapidly compress the value of narrow, repeatable expertise, “getting better at the current game” is no longer enough. You also need to ask whether the game itself is still worth playing.

What breaks when people miss this is not just career performance but compounding. They invest years in a path that looked sensible locally but was misaligned globally — misaligned with how the market is changing, with what they are naturally strong at, or with what they actually want. Then all their speed and effort deepen the investment in the wrong direction.

So this conversation matters because direction is upstream of both productivity and skill. If direction is wrong, speed becomes expensive. If direction is right, even imperfect movement tends to generate useful learning, better opportunities, and stronger long-term leverage.

## What You Need To Know First

**1. Compounding**  
Compounding means small gains building on each other over time so that the later returns are much larger than the early ones. Careers compound when skills, reputation, relationships, and judgment reinforce each other. This matters because the article is really about choosing a direction where that reinforcement can happen.

**2. Optionality**  
Optionality is having multiple future paths available to you instead of being trapped in one narrow lane. Skills that transfer across contexts increase optionality. The article relies on this because crossover skills and learning velocity help you stay adaptable when industries shift.

**3. Probe**  
A probe is a small action taken mainly to get information: a side project, a pilot, a test offer, a conversation, a prototype. The point is not immediate success but learning. This matters because the article prefers movement that generates data over pure analysis.

**4. Differentiation**  
Differentiation is the thing that makes your contribution meaningfully distinct from what others — or a model — can do. The article argues that your best long-term direction usually leans into this rather than away from it.

## The Key Ideas, Connected

**1. The main career problem is no longer just improvement, but directional accuracy.**  
The article starts by challenging a stable-era assumption: that the destination is known, and your task is just to climb effectively. In a fast-changing environment, especially one shaped by AI, that assumption weakens. You can become highly competent at something whose value is falling. So before optimizing execution, you need to evaluate whether the path itself still makes sense. That leads directly to a new model of careers.

**2. Careers are better understood as nonlinear paths with valuable crossovers, not straight ladders.**  
Most real careers involve detours, side interests, adjacent skills, and unexpected recombinations. The article uses this to argue that breadth is no longer obviously inferior to specialization. If AI becomes very strong within narrow domains, human advantage shifts toward combining domains in ways that are less standard and less easily replicated. Once that is true, dissatisfaction with your current role should not be evaluated only in terms of prestige or pay, but in terms of what it is helping you build.

**3. A useful test for a job is whether it is still teaching you and still using what is distinctive about you.**  
The article suggests two questions: am I learning, and am I using my unique capabilities? These are good diagnostic questions because they separate temporary discomfort from structural stagnation. A hard role can still be right if it is stretching you. A comfortable role can still be wrong if it no longer develops you or uses what makes you different. Once you see that, the next issue is how to get better information rather than just thinking harder about the problem.

**4. When direction is unclear, movement is often better than analysis because movement reveals information.**  
This is the SAS principle in career form. A side project, MVP, stretch assignment, or adjacent responsibility tells you more than prolonged internal debate. That is because action creates feedback: from the market, from other people, and from your own motivation. This matters because uncertainty about direction is often not solved by introspection alone. It is solved by running probes. And those probes are valuable even when they do not “work” in the obvious sense.

**5. Effort rarely disappears; it usually converts into skills, evidence, and recombinable assets.**  
One of the article’s reassuring claims is that failed efforts are not empty if they produce transferable learning, relationships, or proof of preference. This is important because fear of wasted effort traps people in inaction. If you understand that outputs can recombine later in unexpected ways, then exploratory action becomes cheaper psychologically. That naturally raises a deeper question: what exactly are you trying to compound?

**6. Long-term direction should increasingly align with your unique capability — what you do easily that others find hard.**  
The article distinguishes this from your formal role. Unique capability often feels ordinary from the inside because it is effortless for you. But that ease is exactly the clue. It marks a potential source of differentiation, and differentiation becomes more valuable as generic competence becomes cheaper. Once you identify this, it becomes easier to choose work that is not only available, but defensible and energizing. Beneath that, though, are two psychological capacities that determine whether you can navigate uncertainty at all.

**7. Uncertainty tolerance and learning velocity are the meta-skills that keep direction adjustable.**  
You cannot choose perfectly in advance, so you need tolerance for acting without complete information. That is uncertainty tolerance. And because the environment keeps changing, you need to keep being the sort of person who can learn new tools, domains, and rules quickly. That is learning velocity. Together they make career navigation dynamic instead of brittle. Once you accept that, two more practical habits follow.

**8. Borrow frameworks from nearby people, and say yes more often when stretch opportunities appear.**  
The article argues that people a few years ahead of you are often the best source of usable guidance because they still remember your problem space clearly. It also argues that many people under-reach by declining opportunities they could grow into. These points fit together: navigation improves when you use local guidance and allow external belief in your potential to pull you forward. That closes into the final anchor of the article.

**9. The “right direction” is not just strategically robust; it is personally yours.**  
The final claim is that even strong frameworks are in service of a life you actually want. If you continually optimize for status, duty, or other people’s expectations against your own deeper preferences, you may eventually sabotage the path from within. This completes the chain: direction is about market reality, transferable advantage, active probing, and personal ownership all at once.

## Handles and Anchors

**1. Don’t just climb fast; make sure the ladder is leaning on the right wall.**  
This is the simplest way to remember the whole piece. Efficiency is irrelevant if the destination is wrong.

**2. Move to learn.**  
When uncertain, a small real-world probe usually teaches more than another week of abstract career thinking.

**3. Your edge is often the thing that feels easiest to you.**  
What feels ordinary from inside your own head may be exactly what is rare and valuable outside it.

They will ask not just “is this stable?” but “is this still increasing what I can do?”

They will take adjacent skills seriously instead of dismissing them as distractions from a single identity.

**A builder who understands this will run more small probes when uncertain because side projects, pilots, and stretch assignments generate better directional information than prolonged analysis alone.**  
They will try to learn from reality sooner instead of waiting for total confidence.

**A builder who understands this will invest in learning velocity because the ability to learn new tools and domains quickly is itself a long-term career asset in unstable environments.**  
They will treat ongoing adaptation as part of the job, not as something separate from it.

**A builder who understands this will choose with both strategy and ownership in mind because durable direction depends not only on market logic but on whether the path is actually aligned with what they want to build their life around.**  
That alignment is what makes sustained effort feel like compounding rather than self-betrayal.
