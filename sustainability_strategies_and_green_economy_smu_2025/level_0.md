# Sustainability Strategies and Green Economy — Level 0: Course Map

> **Intent:** To develop genuine analytical power over the relationship between economic systems, ecological limits, and organisational behaviour — and to distinguish between frameworks that manage that relationship and those that could transform it.
>
> **Your angle:** You are working through an executive education programme that speaks the language of competitive strategy. That language is worth learning, but its framing is load-bearing: it positions sustainability as contingent on market conditions rather than grounded in obligation. Your task is to move between the official curriculum and the deeper analytical layer beneath it — understanding what each framework is *trying* to solve at the first-principle level, where it succeeds, and what it structurally cannot address. The annotations are your primary material; the slides are context.

---

## How to use this map

**Level 1 topics** are the conceptual units worth understanding in their own right — each one names a problem, not just a subject area. Work through them in the order below; the dependency chain is real.

**Level 2 candidates** are the sub-concepts that reward closer examination. They are not exhaustive lists — they mark the points where the analysis gets interesting or where the official curriculum and the critical layer diverge most sharply. Descend to Level 2 when a Level 1 topic feels unresolved, when the programme's framing feels incomplete, or when you want to understand the mechanism rather than the label.

The document splits into two layers that must be read together: the **official curriculum** (what the programme teaches and why) and the **first-principle critique** (what problem each topic is actually attempting to solve, and what it structurally cannot). You will get the most out of this map by tracking both simultaneously.

## Topic Inventory

---

### I. The Structural Problem: Why Sustainability Is Hard

These topics establish the underlying mechanics of why ecological destruction occurs and why voluntary business-level responses are structurally limited. They are not explicitly on the SMU syllabus — but they are the analytical ground on which every module in the programme implicitly stands. Without them, the official curriculum produces competence without comprehension.

---

#### L1-01 · Externalisation and the Cost Attribution Problem

The foundational mechanism driving ecological and social harm is *externalisation* — the ability of economic actors to transfer the costs of their activity onto parties who have no voice in the transaction: ecosystems, low-wage workers in distant supply chains, future generations. This is not a failure of individual ethics; it is a structural feature of markets that price goods without incorporating the full costs of producing them. Every framework in this programme — Triple Bottom Line, ESG, circular economy, carbon accounting — is an attempt to make externalised costs visible or to attribute them to their source. Understanding externalisation is understanding what all of these frameworks are actually for, and why voluntary versions of them are systematically weaker than mandatory ones.

- **True-cost accounting** — drilling here reveals why market prices are structurally incomplete and what it would take to make them accurate; this concept recurs across the entire programme under different names.
- **The social production of consumer subjectivity** — reveals that overconsumption is not a psychological error but a structural output of an economy characterised by chronic overproduction that must manufacture its own demand.
- **Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions** — the choice of which scope to treat as standard embeds assumptions about cost attribution with significant political and distributional consequences.
- **Planned obsolescence** — shows how product life cycles are deliberately shortened to sustain throughput, making circular economy claims contingent on whether firms have incentives to reverse this.
- **The externality of waste** — explains why waste is not a disposal problem but a cost attribution problem, and why industrial symbiosis addresses symptoms while regulatory cost internalisation addresses cause.

---

#### L1-02 · Capital Accumulation as Structural Imperative

The programme treats growth as a "dominant worldview" or a "cherished goal" — a set of ideas society has collectively chosen. The materialist reading inverts this: under capitalism, growth is not an intellectual choice but a structural imperative. Firms must compete to survive; to compete they must reinvest to expand; the global financial system is built on debt that requires future growth to service. This is why "green growth" — the conjunction of economic expansion and environmental benefit that the programme treats as its central claim — is empirically contested. If growth is structurally compelled, sustainability frameworks that leave the growth imperative intact are optimising the efficiency of a fundamentally incompatible system. This is the most important framing decision in the entire programme, and it operates invisibly.

- **Absolute vs. relative decoupling** — understanding the difference between these reveals why efficiency gains have historically not reduced absolute resource throughput, and what that implies for green growth claims.
- **The systemic necessity of capital accumulation** — drilling here replaces the idealist account of growth with a materialist one, making the limits of voluntary sustainability legible.
- **Degrowth and sufficiency economics** — Serge Latouche, Tim Jackson, and others pose the question the programme cannot ask: how much is enough? Understanding this tradition defines the outer boundary of what managerial sustainability can address.
- **The thermodynamic constraint** — Herman Daly and Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen establish why infinite growth in a finite, entropic biosphere is physically impossible, not merely politically inconvenient.
- **Green capitalism's empirical track record** — the gap between 30 years of corporate sustainability commitments and measured ecological outcomes is itself the most important data point in the programme.

### II. The Official Curriculum: Frameworks, Their Principles, and Their Limits

These are the programme's core content areas. Each is presented here at the level of the problem it is designed to solve — which is more useful than the programme's own framing of each as a managerial opportunity.

---

#### L1-03 · Circular Economy and Throughput Reduction

The circular economy emerged from industrial ecology and systems thinking in the late 20th century — Ellen MacArthur Foundation popularised the contemporary business framing after 2010. Its core insight is that linear production systems (extract-produce-discard) convert resources to waste faster than the biosphere can absorb. The circular model attempts to reduce throughput by keeping materials in use through reuse, remanufacturing, and recycling. This is a genuine systemic insight. Its limit is that it can be applied at the margins of a growth-dependent system — producing efficiency gains that are absorbed by increased volume rather than reduced impact. The question to carry into this module is whether circular principles, applied within the current system, reduce absolute throughput at the speed required.

- **ReSOLVE and Regenerate-Rewire-Inspire frameworks** — proprietary toolkits from Ellen MacArthur Foundation; the question they cannot answer is whether they redesign systems or optimise existing architectures.
- **Industrial symbiosis (Kalundborg model)** — one of the few concepts in the programme that operates at the level of network architecture rather than individual firm behaviour; reveals what system-level waste elimination looks like vs. waste reduction.
- **The rebound effect (Jevons paradox)** — efficiency gains in resource use historically increase total consumption by making the activity cheaper; drilling here reveals the structural limit of efficiency-led sustainability.
- **Material flow analysis** — the accounting method that makes throughput visible at the economy level; reveals why firm-level circularity can be consistent with increasing aggregate extraction.
- **Biological vs. technical cycles** — the conceptual core of circular economy design; distinguishes between materials that can safely re-enter biological systems and those that must remain in closed technical loops.

---

#### L1-04 · Extended Producer Responsibility and Regulatory Instruments

Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) is one of the few binding regulatory instruments discussed in this programme, and it warrants disproportionate attention for that reason. The principle — that producers bear the end-of-life cost of the products from which they profit, rather than transferring that cost to the public — is an instance of mandatory cost internalisation rather than voluntary commitment. EPR was first implemented in Germany in 1991 (the Packaging Ordinance) and has since spread across product categories and jurisdictions. It demonstrates empirically that regulatory attribution of costs changes producer behaviour in ways voluntary frameworks do not. Use it as a reference point against which to measure every other framework in the programme.

- **Command-and-control vs. market-based instruments** — the distinction between direct regulatory bans and market mechanisms (taxes, cap-and-trade) reveals what level of structural change each can and cannot produce.
- **Regulatory capture (George Stigler, 1971)** — the mechanism by which regulated industries use political power to neutralise regulations that threaten their interests; explains the consistent underperformance of sustainability policy.
- **The Packaging Ordinance (Germany, 1991)** — the first modern EPR system; drilling here reveals the political conditions under which binding cost attribution becomes possible.
- **Scope of producer responsibility** — the difference between take-back obligations, deposit systems, and full life-cycle liability reveals how far cost attribution can extend before industry resistance becomes effective.
- **International regulatory arbitrage** — the structural mechanism by which binding national regulations are partially nullified when production migrates to lower-regulation jurisdictions; defines the limit of firm-level and nation-level regulatory solutions.

---

#### L1-05 · ESG Reporting, Disclosure, and Accountability

ESG reporting addresses *information asymmetry* — the gap between what firms know about their social and ecological impacts and what investors, regulators, and communities can observe. Without disclosure, accountability is impossible; with selective or inconsistent disclosure, accountability is performative. The framework emerged from socially responsible investment traditions in the 1970s–90s and was formalised by the UN Global Compact (2000) and GRI standards (1997-). The critical question it raises is the distinction between voluntary self-reported frameworks (current mainstream), mandatory standardised frameworks (ISSB, CSRD), and independently audited disclosure. These are materially different instruments, and the gap between them is where the accountability problem lives.

- **Voluntary vs. mandatory disclosure standards** — the regulatory gap between GRI-style self-reporting and mandatory ISSB/CSRD frameworks reveals why ESG data quality is systematically unreliable.
- **Greenwashing as rational corporate behaviour** — when voluntary disclosure allows selective reporting and there is reputational benefit to sustainability signalling, misrepresentation becomes the rational strategy; drilling here explains the mechanism rather than treating greenwashing as individual bad faith.
- **Independent verification and auditing** — the absence of third-party verification in most ESG frameworks reveals the gap between reporting and accountability.
- **Materiality definitions** — who decides which social and ecological impacts are "material" for disclosure embeds significant political choices about whose interests count.
- **Scope 3 emissions disclosure** — the most complete form of carbon attribution; reveals supply chain emissions that firms systematically prefer not to report and regulators are only beginning to mandate.

---

#### L1-06 · Green Finance and Capital Allocation

Green finance addresses the question of *capital allocation* — if capital flows toward ecologically destructive activities because they are more profitable, redirecting capital requires either changing relative profitability (through regulation or taxation) or creating instruments that make sustainable activity financially competitive. Green bonds, sustainability-linked loans, ESG investing, and carbon markets are the main instruments. The critical question is whether these instruments redirect capital at the scale and speed required, or whether they allow capital markets to price sustainability as a premium signal without changing the underlying allocation of capital to fossil fuel expansion, deforestation, and extractive industry. This module is where the gap between financial innovation and structural change is most visible.

- **Green bonds and use-of-proceeds requirements** — reveals how the integrity of green finance instruments depends entirely on the strength of their verification and use-of-proceeds conditions.
- **Carbon markets (cap-and-trade vs. carbon tax)** — the two primary market mechanisms for carbon pricing; understanding the design choices reveals why carbon markets have consistently under-delivered on emissions reduction.
- **ESG fund composition and fossil fuel exposure** — empirical examination of what ESG investment funds actually hold reveals the gap between the label and the underlying capital allocation.
- **Fiduciary duty and the sustainability obligation** — the legal framework that governs institutional investor decisions; reveals the structural tension between return maximisation and sustainability mandates.
- **The carbon price required for 1.5°C** — empirical anchor: modelling consistently suggests carbon prices of $100–200+/tonne are required; current average prices are far below this threshold, revealing the scale of the political failure.

---

#### L1-07 · Sustainable Operations and Supply Chain Accountability

Supply chain sustainability addresses *distributed harm* — the structural feature of global production that allows firms to claim domestic sustainability credentials while their suppliers externalise environmental and labour costs in lower-regulation jurisdictions. This is not a failure of supplier management; it is a feature of global trade architecture. Investment treaties, trade agreements, and regulatory arbitrage create incentives for firms to locate harmful production where accountability is weakest. No operational framework within a single firm can resolve this without the regulatory architecture changing. The module's frameworks (sense-response sourcing, sustainable hierarchy, industrial symbiosis) address what firms can do within existing structure; the more important question is what structure would need to change.

- **Supplier auditing and its limits** — the gap between supplier certification and actual on-the-ground conditions reveals why auditing-based supply chain accountability consistently fails to prevent harm.
- **The race to the bottom in labour and environmental standards** — the structural mechanism by which global competition drives regulatory standards downward; explains why firm-level supply chain commitments cannot substitute for binding international regulation.
- **Industrial symbiosis at scale** — the Kalundborg Symbiosis reveals what is required for inter-firm material exchange networks to function; mostly, this is proximity, trust, and institutional infrastructure that markets alone do not produce.
- **Sense-response sourcing** — the proprietary framework in the module; worth understanding as an operational tool and interrogating as to whether it addresses distributed harm or manages reputational risk.
- **Border carbon adjustment mechanisms** — the policy instrument that attempts to prevent regulatory arbitrage by pricing the carbon content of imports; reveals the political economy of closing regulatory gaps at the trade level.

### III. Power, Change, and What the Programme Cannot Say

These topics are not on the syllabus. They are the analytical layer that makes the official curriculum legible — explaining why the frameworks above exist, why they are insufficient, and what kinds of change have historically produced the structural outcomes they are trying to achieve.

---

#### L1-08 · Political Economy of Ecological Crisis

Karl Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* (1944) and the tradition of political economy that follows from it explain how the historical creation of the "self-regulating market" required turning land, labour, and nature into "fictitious commodities" — things that behave as if they were produced for sale but are not. The ecological consequences of this fictionalisation — treating the biosphere as a costless input and a limitless sink — are the structural origin of every problem this programme is addressing. Understanding this tradition replaces the programme's idealist account (ecological crisis as a management challenge) with a materialist one (ecological crisis as a structural output of a specific economic arrangement). This is not an ideological preference; it is an analytical prerequisite for understanding why voluntary frameworks have not worked.

- **Polanyi's "double movement"** — the historical oscillation between market expansion and societal self-protection that Polanyi identifies; reveals the structural conditions under which regulation becomes politically possible.
- **Commodification of non-reproducible natural resources** — the first-principle mechanism by which markets systematically underprice and over-extract non-renewable commons.
- **Primitive accumulation and the enclosure of commons** — the historical mechanism by which shared resources are privatised; recurs in contemporary forms including intellectual property enclosure and land grabbing.
- **The state's role in constituting markets** — reveals that markets are not natural phenomena but political constructions maintained by legal and coercive state power; foundational for understanding what regulation can and cannot achieve.

---

#### L1-09 · Regulatory Theory and Institutional Failure

George Stigler's theory of regulatory capture (1971) explains why regulatory agencies — the institutions nominally responsible for constraining corporate ecological harm — consistently serve the interests of the industries they regulate. This is not a story of corruption; it is a structural analysis. Industries are concentrated, well-resourced, and have intense interests in regulatory outcomes. The public is diffuse, under-resourced, and faces collective action problems. The result is systematically predictable: regulations are captured, weakened, delayed, and selectively enforced. Understanding this mechanism explains the consistent gap between the sustainability commitments of international frameworks (the Paris Agreement, the SDGs, the CBD) and measured outcomes — without requiring conspiracy theories or bad faith.

- **Regulatory capture (Stigler, 1971)** — the canonical mechanism; drilling here reveals why COP summits, voluntary standards bodies, and national sustainability agencies consistently under-deliver.
- **The revolving door between industry and regulator** — the institutional mechanism by which industry expertise enters regulatory bodies, producing structural alignment between regulator and regulated.
- **International regime theory and its limits** — reveals why international agreements without enforcement mechanisms are structurally similar to voluntary corporate commitments.
- **The IMF and World Bank conditionality problem** — the mechanism by which international financial institutions make regulatory weakening a condition of access to capital; explains why developing nations face structural pressure against ecological regulation.

---

#### L1-10 · Structural Change: Historical Mechanisms and Leverage Points

If voluntary frameworks are structurally insufficient, and regulatory capture explains the consistent failure of state-level intervention, the question becomes: what has historically produced structural change? The evidence is consistent. Large structural reforms — the 8-hour workday, child labour abolition, workplace safety standards, the creation of the EPA and Clean Air Act — were won not through better managerial values or consumer awareness campaigns, but through organised collective power: strikes, mass mobilisation, litigation, and political pressure that threatened the legitimacy of existing institutions. This is not a left-wing thesis; it is a historical observation. It defines the difference between *leadership within a structure* and *leadership that changes a structure* — the distinction the stewardship module cannot make within its own frame.

- **Erik Olin Wright's transformation strategies** — ruptural, interstitial, and symbiotic strategies of structural change; provides a systematic framework for thinking about what kind of action produces what kind of outcome.
- **The labor movement as historical case** — the mechanism by which organised workers forced binding regulation of working conditions; reveals the structural leverage of organised collective disruption over capital.
- **The US environmental movement (1970s)** — the political conditions under which mass mobilisation forced the creation of the EPA and landmark environmental legislation; reveals what was required and what has been absent since.
- **Mondragon cooperative network** — the empirical case that the link between economic activity and private capital accumulation is a political choice, not a natural law; worker-ownership as a structural mechanism for aligning firm incentives with long-term welfare.
- **Nordic co-determination** — mandatory worker representation on corporate boards in Sweden and Germany; a structural mechanism that provides material check on financialised decision-making without requiring ownership transfer.

## Sequencing Note

The dependency chain in this map runs in two directions simultaneously.

**The official curriculum** follows a roughly additive logic: sustainable consumption → circular economy → operations → reporting → finance → leadership → innovation. This is a reasonable pedagogical sequence — each topic adds a domain of application. You can follow it without disruption.

**The analytical layer** has a different dependency structure. Start with **L1-01 (Externalisation)** and **L1-02 (Capital Accumulation)** — these are the load-bearing analytical foundations. Without them, the frameworks in L1-03 through L1-07 are tools without a theory of the problem they are solving. Then work through the official curriculum topics (L1-03–07) using the first-principle framing: what problem is this framework trying to solve, where does it succeed, and what does it structurally cannot address? Finally, use **L1-08–10** as the closing analytical frame — the political economy tradition that explains why the gap between frameworks and outcomes is not accidental.

**The highest-leverage entry points** for someone with professional experience and existing organisational authority:

1. **L1-04 (EPR)** — the one regulatory instrument in the programme with binding force. Understanding it thoroughly gives you a precise model of what mandatory cost attribution looks like and what it requires politically. Use it as the reference standard for everything else.
2. **L1-05 (ESG and Disclosure)** — the domain most immediately actionable in most organisational contexts, and the one where the gap between voluntary and mandatory frameworks is most visible and most consequential.
3. **L1-08 (Political Economy)** — Polanyi is the single highest-leverage external text. One careful reading of *The Great Transformation* will reframe the entire programme and make its analytical structure visible in a way no module in the curriculum does directly.

The annotation in Section 9 of the source material is worth taking seriously on its own terms: the frustration of a rigorous reader encountering clean optimism in the face of accelerating collapse is not cynicism — it is a signal that the analysis has reached the limits of the framework being offered. The purpose of this map is to give you somewhere to go when that signal fires.
