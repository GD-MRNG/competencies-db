# ESG Reporting, Disclosure, and Accountability

The cleanest way to understand ESG reporting is to start with what it is *not*. It is not a measure of how sustainable a company is. It is not a verdict on corporate behaviour. It is not, in most of its current forms, even a reliable description of impact. It is an attempt — and an incomplete one — to solve a specific problem: the gap between what a firm knows about its own social and ecological footprint and what anyone outside the firm can see. Once you hold that frame, the entire ESG landscape rearranges itself. The interesting question stops being "is this company's ESG score good?" and becomes "what kind of disclosure regime produced this number, and what can it actually tell you?"

The underlying problem is information asymmetry. A firm's operations generate effects — emissions, labour conditions, water use, supply chain harms — that the firm itself can observe with reasonable granularity. Investors, regulators, communities, and customers cannot. Without some mechanism that forces the inside information out, accountability is impossible: you cannot hold an actor responsible for harms you cannot see. ESG disclosure exists to close that gap. But the closure is only as good as the disclosure regime, and disclosure regimes vary enormously in how much pressure they actually put on the firm to tell the truth.

This is where the historical arc matters. ESG reporting emerged from socially responsible investment traditions in the 1970s through 90s — investors who wanted to screen out tobacco, weapons, apartheid-era South Africa, and needed information to do so. It was formalised through the UN Global Compact in 2000 and the Global Reporting Initiative standards from 1997 onwards. Both were voluntary. Firms chose whether to report, what to report, and how to frame what they reported. This is the genealogy of mainstream ESG: it grew up as a voluntary self-reporting tradition, and most of the practices, scoring methodologies, and rating agencies you encounter today still operate inside that paradigm. That genealogy is load-bearing. It explains why the data is uneven, why apples-to-apples comparisons are difficult, and why two ESG ratings agencies routinely disagree about whether the same company is good or bad.

The critical analytical move is to recognise that "ESG disclosure" names three materially different instruments masquerading as one. The first is voluntary self-reported disclosure: the firm decides what counts as material, gathers its own data, frames its own narrative, and publishes. The second is mandatory standardised disclosure: a regulator specifies what must be reported, in what format, on what timeline — the European CSRD and the ISSB standards are the contemporary moves in this direction. The third is independently audited disclosure: the reported numbers are verified by a third party with professional liability, the way financial statements are audited. Most of what currently circulates in markets is the first kind. Almost none is the third. The gap between these three is not a technical detail; it is where the accountability problem lives.

Consider what each regime actually produces. Voluntary self-reporting produces narrative — sustainability reports that read more like marketing documents than financial filings, because that is exactly what they are optimised to be. When disclosure is voluntary and reputational benefit accrues to looking sustainable, selective reporting is not a moral failure of individual executives; it is the rational response to the incentive structure. This is why "greenwashing" is better understood as a feature of the regime than a defect of character. Mandatory standardised disclosure changes the cost calculus by making non-disclosure or false disclosure legally consequential, but its quality depends entirely on the standard's scope (what must be reported), its specificity (how it must be measured), and its enforcement (what happens when a firm gets it wrong). Independently audited disclosure is the only form that produces something analogous to financial accountability — and it is the rarest form in the field.

A handful of friction points reveal where the regime is weakest. Materiality definitions — who decides which impacts are "material" enough to report — embed a quiet political choice about whose interests count; "financial materiality" (impacts on the firm) and "double materiality" (impacts of the firm on the world) generate dramatically different disclosure obligations. Scope 3 emissions, the supply chain emissions that typically dwarf a firm's direct operational footprint, are systematically under-reported because they are hard to measure and unflattering to disclose. The major ratings agencies use proprietary methodologies that weight indicators differently and produce divergent scores for the same firm, which is structurally unlike credit ratings, where two agencies will largely agree. None of these are problems that better corporate intent can fix. They are problems of regime design.

What this means in practice is that your ability to read ESG outputs critically depends on a single discipline: always asking what regime produced the number. A 2020 sustainability report from a US oil major and a 2026 CSRD-compliant filing from a European industrial firm are not comparable artefacts, even if both have a section labelled "emissions." A high MSCI ESG score and a low Sustainalytics score for the same company are not contradictory — they are evidence that proprietary methodologies disagree about what to measure. An ESG fund that holds fossil fuel majors is not necessarily lying; it is operating inside a definition of ESG that the underlying disclosure regime permits. The skill this topic builds is not learning the frameworks. It is learning to read what any given disclosure can and cannot tell you, and to recognise that the most important features of ESG reporting are decided not in the report but in the rules that govern what the report has to say.

## Level 2 candidates

**Voluntary vs. mandatory disclosure standards** — The structural differences between GRI-style self-reporting, the emerging mandatory ISSB and CSRD regimes, and the older UN Global Compact tradition. Worth a deeper dive because the move from voluntary to mandatory is the single most consequential shift currently underway in this space, and understanding the design choices in CSRD (double materiality, assurance requirements, value-chain scope) gives you a precise model of what serious disclosure looks like.

**Greenwashing as rational corporate behaviour** — The mechanism by which voluntary disclosure plus reputational reward systematically produces misrepresentation, independent of individual intent. Worth deeper treatment because the standard moralised framing of greenwashing obscures the structural cause and therefore the structural fix; once you see it as an incentive problem, the policy response becomes legible.

**Materiality and double materiality** — Who decides what impacts must be disclosed, and the difference between disclosing impacts on the firm versus impacts of the firm on the world. Worth going deeper because this is the quiet political choice that determines what an entire disclosure regime can see, and the financial-materiality vs. double-materiality split is the live battleground between US and European regulatory traditions.

**Independent verification and assurance** — The professional infrastructure (or absence of it) that would make ESG data comparable in rigour to audited financial statements. Worth examining because the gap between "reported" and "assured" is where most of the current data quality problem actually lives, and the assurance industry's slow movement into ESG is reshaping the field.

**Scope 3 emissions disclosure** — The supply chain emissions category that typically dominates a firm's true footprint and is most often missing from voluntary reports. Worth deeper treatment because Scope 3 is the test case for whether modern disclosure regimes can attribute costs across the full production chain or whether firm boundaries will continue to define what counts.

**ESG ratings divergence** — The empirical phenomenon that major ESG raters routinely disagree about the same firm, in contrast to credit raters. Worth a deeper look because the divergence is not noise; it reflects unresolved methodological disagreements about what ESG is measuring, and understanding the divergence is the antidote to treating any single score as authoritative.
