# L1-08 · Political Economy of Ecological Crisis

Most sustainability education treats ecological crisis as a management problem — a challenge of better metrics, better incentives, better leadership. This framing is comfortable because it implies the tools you already have are the right ones; you just need to apply them more skilfully. It is also the single most consequential analytical error you can make in this field. The crisis is not a management failure inside an otherwise functional economic arrangement. It is a predictable structural output of that arrangement working as designed. Until you can see this clearly, every framework in the rest of the programme will look more powerful than it is.

The sharpest tool for seeing it is Karl Polanyi's *The Great Transformation*, published in 1944. Polanyi's central claim is historical, not ideological: the "self-regulating market" we now treat as natural is a recent and engineered phenomenon, and its construction required something specific and strange. To make a market society work, three things had to be turned into commodities — that is, into objects produced for sale on a market. Those three things are land, labour, and money. None of them are actually produced for sale. Land is nature, which precedes any economy. Labour is human life and time, which is not manufactured. Money is a token of social trust, created by states and banks. Polanyi calls these fictitious commodities, and the word "fictitious" is doing precise analytical work. It does not mean fake. It means that we treat them *as if* they obeyed the logic of commodities, while their actual nature does not.

The ecological consequence of fictionalising land — that is, of treating nature as a commodity input — is that the biosphere shows up in the economy in two roles, both of which are catastrophic at scale. It is treated as a costless input: forests, fish stocks, fossil fuels, fresh water, topsoil, atmospheric stability, and biodiversity all enter production with their replacement cost set to roughly zero. And it is treated as a limitless sink: emissions, effluent, plastics, and waste exit production into ecosystems that are also priced at roughly zero. This is not an oversight. It is the operational definition of what a "self-regulating market" requires: nature must behave like a commodity, which means it must be priceable, ownable, and substitutable. The biosphere does none of these things, but the economy proceeds as if it did. The gap between how the biosphere actually behaves and how the economy treats it is exactly the gap that all the frameworks in this programme — externality pricing, true-cost accounting, ESG disclosure, carbon markets, EPR — are trying to close.

Once you see this, the architecture of the entire field becomes legible. Externalisation (L1-01) is not a market imperfection that careful design could eliminate; it is what fictitious commodification produces by construction. The growth imperative (L1-02) is what happens when an economy organised around commodified labour and money must continuously expand to remain solvent. Regulatory capture (L1-09) is what happens when the political institutions nominally tasked with constraining the fiction are themselves embedded in the economy that depends on it. The frameworks in the official curriculum — circular economy, ESG, green finance, sustainable supply chains — are all attempts to make particular pieces of the biosphere visible to a price system that was constructed precisely to ignore them. Some of these attempts work better than others. None of them dissolve the underlying fiction.

Polanyi adds a second move that matters as much as the first. He argues that societies do not passively accept the damage that market expansion inflicts on land, labour, and human life. They push back. He calls this the double movement: the historical oscillation between phases of market expansion and phases of social self-protection, in which states are forced — by mass mobilisation, political crisis, or sheer economic catastrophe — to re-embed the market in social and ecological constraint. The labour standards of the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the welfare states of the post-war period, and (briefly) the environmental legislation of the 1970s are all examples. The double movement is the historical mechanism by which fictitious commodities are partially re-fictionalised in the other direction — pulled back out of the market and treated again as the social or ecological substrates they actually are. This is also why the first-principle question in sustainability is not "what should firms do?" but "under what conditions has structural re-embedding historically occurred, and are those conditions present now?"

This is what it means to replace the idealist account of ecological crisis with a materialist one. The idealist account says: people did not understand or did not care, and the solution is education and incentives. The materialist account says: the system is producing exactly what it is structured to produce, and changing the output requires changing the structure. Notice that nothing in this analysis requires anyone to be a villain. There is no conspiracy, no failure of individual virtue, no shortage of well-meaning executives. The structural account explains the consistent gap between thirty years of corporate sustainability commitments and measured ecological outcomes without needing any of those moves. That is its analytical power: it predicts the outcome you actually observe.

The skill this topic builds, and the reason it sits where it does in the dependency chain, is the ability to ask of any sustainability framework a single diagnostic question: does this framework attempt to make a fictitious commodity behave more like a real one through pricing and disclosure, or does it attempt to re-embed that thing in non-market constraint? Both are legitimate moves. They are not equivalent, and they have very different track records. Pricing carbon is the first move; banning a class of activity is the second. Voluntary ESG disclosure is the first move; mandatory audited disclosure with legal liability is the second. Once the distinction is in your hands, the official curriculum stops being a sequence of equally valid tools and starts being a map you can read. That is what Polanyi gives you, and it is why one careful reading of *The Great Transformation* will do more for your analytical capacity in this field than most of the modules that follow.

## Level 2 candidates

**Polanyi's "double movement"** — Covers the historical oscillation between market expansion and societal self-protection, and the specific conditions under which the second movement has historically been triggered. Worth deeper treatment because it is the operational answer to "what produces structural change?" and because the conditions it identifies — crisis, organised pressure, legitimacy threat — are diagnostic for evaluating whether current sustainability politics are converging on real reform or stalling.

**Fictitious commodities — land, labour, money** — Covers the specific mechanism by which each of the three is rendered tradable and what fails when each is treated as a real commodity. Worth deeper treatment because the three have different failure modes (ecological collapse, immiseration, financial crisis) and because seeing them as a connected triad rather than separate problems reorganises how you read every other topic in the programme.

**Commodification of non-reproducible natural resources** — Covers the first-principle mechanism by which markets systematically underprice and over-extract non-renewable commons, as distinct from renewable ones. Worth deeper treatment because the standard externality-pricing response (carbon tax, resource royalties) interacts very differently with non-reproducible stocks than with flows, and the distinction is usually elided in managerial framings.

**Primitive accumulation and the enclosure of commons** — Covers the historical mechanism by which shared resources were privatised in early capitalism and its contemporary recurrences in intellectual property, land grabbing, data, and genetic material. Worth deeper treatment because it reveals that commodification is not a one-time historical event but an ongoing process, which reframes contemporary disputes over biodiversity, indigenous land rights, and digital commons.

**The state's role in constituting markets** — Covers the analytical point that markets are not natural phenomena that states intervene in, but political constructions that states create and maintain through legal and coercive power. Worth deeper treatment because it directly contradicts the framing implicit in most business education (state as external constraint on private activity) and because it changes what regulatory possibility looks like.
