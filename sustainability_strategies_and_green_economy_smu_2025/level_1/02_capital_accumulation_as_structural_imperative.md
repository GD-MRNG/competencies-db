# L1-02 · Capital Accumulation as Structural Imperative

Most sustainability curricula treat economic growth as an idea — a worldview, a cultural preference, a "cherished goal" that modern societies have collectively chosen and could, in principle, collectively unchoose. This framing is comforting because it locates the problem in the realm of values, where executive education is fluent. Change minds, change behaviour, change outcomes. The trouble is that the framing is wrong, or at least load-bearingly incomplete, and the entire architecture of corporate sustainability rests on the part it gets wrong.

The materialist reading inverts the picture. Under capitalism, growth is not something firms choose to pursue; it is something they are compelled to pursue on pain of extinction. A firm that does not reinvest its surplus to expand productive capacity is, over time, outcompeted by firms that do. A firm that does not seek efficiency gains loses margin to those that do. A firm that does not service its debt — and almost all firms carry debt, as does the financial system that surrounds them — fails. The compulsion is not psychological; it operates through the price mechanism, the credit system, and the discipline of capital markets. You can have a CEO who personally believes in degrowth and runs a firm that nonetheless must grow, because the alternative is being acquired, refinanced, or liquidated.

Once you see growth as structural rather than ideational, the question of "green growth" looks different. Green growth is the proposition that GDP expansion and environmental impact can be decoupled — that the economy can keep getting bigger while resource throughput and emissions get smaller in absolute terms. This is the central empirical claim on which most of the programme implicitly rests. Every framework you will encounter — circular economy, ESG, sustainable finance, supply chain optimisation — assumes that economic activity can continue to expand while its ecological footprint shrinks fast enough to matter. If that assumption is false, those frameworks are not solutions; they are efficiency improvements to a system whose total impact is determined elsewhere.

The empirical record on decoupling is what makes this analytically serious rather than rhetorical. Relative decoupling — impact per unit of GDP falling — is well documented and uncontroversial; it is what efficiency does. Absolute decoupling — total impact falling while GDP rises, at the speed and scale required to stay within planetary limits — has not been demonstrated at the global level over any sustained period. The pattern instead is that efficiency gains are absorbed by volume growth: cheaper, cleaner production becomes more production, and total throughput rises. This is the rebound effect operating at the level of the whole economy. It is not a quirk of measurement; it is what you would predict if growth is structurally compelled.

This is why the framing matters for everything that follows. If you treat growth as a worldview, then a sustainability framework that improves corporate behaviour without questioning growth looks like progress — you are nudging the worldview in the right direction. If you treat growth as a structural imperative, the same framework looks different: it is making a fundamentally incompatible system more efficient, which under the rebound effect can mean making it more destructive at higher absolute volume, not less. The TBL report, the ESG disclosure, the circular initiative, the green bond — all of these can be entirely sincere and still produce this outcome, because the mechanism that produces ecological damage is not located inside any individual firm's decisions. It is located in the competitive and financial pressures that make those decisions, in aggregate, point in one direction.

The traditions that take this seriously are worth knowing by name, because the official curriculum will not introduce them. Herman Daly and Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen established the thermodynamic argument: a finite, entropic biosphere cannot host indefinite growth in physical throughput, regardless of how that growth is organised. Tim Jackson's *Prosperity Without Growth* and Serge Latouche's degrowth literature ask the question executive education cannot ask without dismantling its own premise: how much is enough? These are not fringe ideological positions; they are the analytical outer boundary against which the claims of green growth must be measured. The programme will not place its frameworks against that boundary. You will have to.

What this gives you is not a position to argue but a diagnostic to apply. When you encounter a sustainability framework, ask: does this framework reduce absolute throughput, or does it improve the throughput-to-output ratio while leaving total throughput to be determined by competitive expansion? Does it constrain the growth imperative, or does it ride on top of it? Most of what you will study does the latter. That is not a reason to dismiss it — efficiency gains matter, and managerial frameworks operate at the layer they operate at. It is a reason to be precise about what each framework can and cannot do, and to stop confusing the optimisation of an incompatible system with the resolution of the incompatibility. The skill this topic builds is the ability to hold both readings at once: to engage seriously with corporate sustainability without losing sight of the structural ceiling above which it cannot rise.

## Level 2 candidates

**Absolute vs. relative decoupling** — The technical distinction between impact-per-GDP falling (relative) and total impact falling while GDP rises (absolute), and what the empirical record shows about each. Worth a deep dive because the entire credibility of green growth hinges on this distinction, and most public discussion conflates the two in ways that obscure what the data actually says.

**The systemic necessity of capital accumulation** — The mechanism by which competitive markets, the credit system, and shareholder governance combine to compel reinvestment and expansion at the level of the firm. Worth deeper treatment because the Level 1 post asserts the compulsion; a Level 2 should walk through the specific channels (competition, debt servicing, equity returns, M&A discipline) through which it operates, so the reader can identify it in concrete corporate situations.

**Degrowth and sufficiency economics** — The intellectual tradition (Latouche, Jackson, Hickel, Kallis) that takes seriously the question of how an economy could organise around sufficiency rather than expansion. Worth exploring because it defines the analytical outer boundary of what managerial sustainability can address, and because most executives have never encountered a serious version of the argument.

**The thermodynamic constraint** — Daly and Georgescu-Roegen's argument that entropy and finite biospheric sinks place a physical ceiling on material throughput regardless of economic organisation. Worth deeper treatment because it grounds the growth critique in physics rather than politics, which changes what kinds of counter-arguments are available.

**Green capitalism's empirical track record** — The thirty-year gap between corporate sustainability commitments and measured ecological outcomes (emissions, biodiversity, material throughput). Worth a deep dive because the gap itself is the most important piece of evidence in the programme, and a careful look at the numbers does more analytical work than any theoretical argument about why voluntary frameworks should be expected to underperform.
