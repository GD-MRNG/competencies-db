# L1-10 · Structural Change: Historical Mechanisms and Leverage Points

The sustainability literature has a quiet preference: it would like the answer to be leadership. It would like the people already in charge of large organisations to be the agents through whom the necessary changes happen, guided by better frameworks, better metrics, and better values. This is an attractive proposition because it is the only one that requires nothing of you that you are not already doing. It is also, on the historical evidence, wrong. The structural reforms that actually changed how economies treat workers, communities, and ecosystems were not produced by enlightened management. They were forced — and the mechanism by which they were forced is specific, repeatable, and almost entirely absent from the curriculum you are being taught.

Look at the reforms that constitute the floor of what we now consider non-negotiable: the 8-hour workday, the abolition of child labour, workplace safety standards, the creation of the US Environmental Protection Agency, the Clean Air Act. None of these were granted. Each was the political residue of organised collective power — strikes, mass mobilisation, litigation, and sustained pressure that made the existing arrangement more costly to defend than to change. The pattern is consistent enough that it should be treated as the default explanation for structural reform, with managerial values and consumer awareness campaigns as the exceptions that need to prove themselves. They have not.

This is not a left-wing thesis. It is a historical observation, and the test is empirical: produce a comparably scaled environmental or labour reform that was won primarily through corporate goodwill and voluntary commitment, without a credible threat of regulatory or political consequence behind it. The list is short and the cases tend to dissolve under scrutiny — most "voluntary" industry standards were adopted in the shadow of imminent regulation, which is to say they were not voluntary in any analytically useful sense. The frameworks you are being taught — ESG reporting, sustainability-linked finance, circular economy commitments — exist in a space that has been carefully cleared of that kind of pressure. They are what corporate sustainability looks like when no one is making it cost anything not to comply.

The distinction this opens up is the one the stewardship module cannot make from inside its own frame: the difference between leadership within a structure and leadership that changes a structure. Leadership within a structure optimises behaviour given the existing rules — better supplier audits, more accurate emissions disclosure, more efficient material flows. It is real work and it is not nothing. But it operates at the level the structure permits, which is the level at which the structure has already determined the harm to be acceptable. Leadership that changes a structure operates on the rules themselves — what is priced, what is mandated, what is prohibited, who has standing, who decides. These are different activities. They draw on different capabilities. They have different histories, and the second one has historically been the work of organised pressure from outside the institutions being changed, not strategic planning inside them.

The components of historical structural change are worth naming concretely because they are unfamiliar in this register. There is the labour movement, which forced binding regulation of working conditions through the structural leverage of organised workers — the ability to disrupt production gave them something corporate sustainability has no analogue for, which is the power to make the existing arrangement unworkable. There is the US environmental movement of the early 1970s, which produced the EPA and the major environmental statutes through mass mobilisation under specific political conditions that have not recurred. There are the structural alternatives that demonstrate the link between economic activity and private capital accumulation is a political choice rather than a natural law: the Mondragon cooperative network in the Basque Country, where worker-ownership aligns firm incentives with long-term welfare; Nordic co-determination, where mandatory worker representation on boards in Sweden and Germany provides a material check on financialised decision-making without requiring ownership transfer. These cases matter because they show, at scale, that the configurations the curriculum treats as fixed are in fact contingent.

Erik Olin Wright's typology of transformation strategies — ruptural, interstitial, and symbiotic — is the clearest framework for thinking about which kinds of action produce which kinds of outcome. Ruptural strategies break with existing institutions; interstitial strategies build alternatives in the gaps (Mondragon is interstitial); symbiotic strategies use existing institutions to expand democratic power within them (co-determination is symbiotic). The point of the typology is not to choose one but to be honest about what each can and cannot do. Most of what the executive sustainability curriculum teaches is not on this typology at all — it is neither rupture, nor alternative-building, nor democratisation. It is optimisation, which is a fourth category that historically has not produced structural change.

The practical implication is that you need to hold two separate maps simultaneously and stop confusing them. One map is what you can do inside your organisation given its existing incentive structure — and that work is worth doing well, because the marginal differences are real and the people affected are real. The other map is what would actually have to change for the aggregate trajectory to bend, and the honest answer is that it lives outside the frame of managerial action: in regulation that has not yet been passed, in collective leverage that has not yet been organised, in ownership and governance structures that the existing economy is configured to prevent. The skill this topic builds is the discipline to distinguish these two maps and to refuse the temptation — which the curriculum will press on you constantly — to mistake one for the other. Knowing the difference is the precondition for choosing where, with the time and authority you actually have, you want to spend yourself.

## Level 2 candidates

**Erik Olin Wright's transformation strategies** — Wright's distinction between ruptural, interstitial, and symbiotic strategies of structural change provides a systematic vocabulary for asking what kind of action produces what kind of outcome. Worth deeper treatment because it is the most rigorous available framework for evaluating where any specific intervention — including the reader's own — actually sits, and most readers will not have encountered it.

**The labour movement as historical case** — The mechanism by which organised workers forced binding regulation of working conditions, from the 19th century through the New Deal era. Worth going deeper because it is the cleanest empirical case of structural reform produced by organised disruption rather than enlightened leadership, and the parallels and disanalogies with contemporary ecological politics are instructive.

**The US environmental movement of the 1970s** — The specific political and social conditions under which mass mobilisation forced the creation of the EPA, the Clean Air Act, the Clean Water Act, and the Endangered Species Act in a remarkably compressed period. Worth examining closely because understanding what was present then and what has been absent since is the most direct way to see why the last fifty years have produced commitments without comparable structural reform.

**Mondragon cooperative network** — The Basque worker-owned industrial federation as an empirical demonstration that the link between economic activity and private capital accumulation is a political choice rather than a natural law. Worth a closer look because the case is concrete, large-scale, and durable enough to function as a counterexample to the claim that current ownership structures are economically necessary.

**Nordic co-determination** — Mandatory worker representation on corporate boards in Sweden and Germany as a structural mechanism that provides a material check on financialised decision-making without requiring ownership transfer. Worth deeper treatment because it is the most accessible symbiotic strategy in Wright's sense — a structural reform achievable within broadly capitalist economies — and it shifts the question from whether change is possible to which configuration is being chosen.

**The distinction between leadership within and leadership that changes a structure** — The analytical line that separates optimisation from structural reform, and the criteria for telling which kind of work a given activity actually constitutes. Worth its own treatment because the executive curriculum systematically blurs this line, and being able to draw it cleanly is the central skill the topic is trying to build.
