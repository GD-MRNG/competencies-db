## 1. Comprehensive Summary

In this lecture, Arnoud De Meyer presents a framework for integrating sustainability into corporate leadership, arguing that modern business leaders must transition from passive risk management to active stewardship. The central thesis is that sustainability is no longer a peripheral concern or a public relations exercise, but a core strategic imperative. De Meyer structures his argument around the dual forces of risk and opportunity. He asserts that organizations face severe physical, transitional, regulatory, and legal risks if they neglect ecological and social realities. Conversely, those that proactively embrace sustainability can capture market opportunities driven by shifting consumer preferences—particularly among millennials—and enhance their ability to attract top-tier talent.

To navigate this landscape, the lecture outlines a progressive strategic methodology. The first step requires an organization to define its fundamental purpose, which De Meyer distinguishes from merely describing its products or services. A true corporate purpose answers why the work is socially important and establishes the enterprise's contribution to society. The underlying principle here is the social justification for an enterprise's legal charter and resource consumption—a tension that predates modern corporate branding. The second step is stakeholder analysis, where leaders map out various groups based on their level of interest and influence. This mapping dictates how the organization should engage with each group, ranging from simple monitoring to close collaboration. This process addresses the underlying principle of managing conflicting claims over an enterprise's surplus and operational impacts by non-owner groups.

The lecture then introduces the concept of stewardship, drawing heavily on the framework developed by the Stewardship Asia Centre. Stewardship is defined as a values-driven commitment to creating a collective better future by safeguarding the needs of future generations. This is operationalized through a compass of four core values: a long-term view, an ownership mentality, a recognition of interdependence, and creative resilience. De Meyer references academic models suggesting that stewardship behavior is produced by a combination of structural factors, such as collaborative control and intrinsic reward systems, and psychological factors, such as cognitive other-regarding perspectives and affective commitment.

Finally, the lecture argues that addressing systemic sustainability challenges requires a departure from traditional, hierarchical command-and-control leadership. Because the necessary technologies and solutions are often distributed across a wide network of partners, leaders must learn to operate within and influence "business ecosystems." This collaborative leadership relies on soft power, active networking, consensus-building, and the ability to manage diversity and dilemmas across organizational boundaries. The lecture concludes with a call to action, urging leaders to move from risk neutralization to proactive strategic development, clarify their organizational purpose, map stakeholders, measure themselves against stewardship values, and leverage collaborative ecosystems to make sustainability happen.

## 2. Key Takeaways & Learning Points

*   **The Quadrant of Sustainability Risks**: Organizations face four distinct categories of risk when neglecting ecological and social realities:
    *   *Physical Risks*: Direct threats to physical assets and supply chains from catastrophic events (e.g., flooding, typhoons) and long-term systemic changes (e.g., rising sea levels creating stranded assets).
    *   *Transition Risks*: The economic disruption of moving from high-carbon to low-carbon business models (e.g., gasoline distribution becoming a sunset industry).
    *   *Regulatory Risks*: The impact of new legal frameworks and bans on hazardous substances (e.g., the global restriction of PFOS).
    *   *Legal Risks*: Direct litigation from civil society groups or activist investors challenging corporate climate plans or misleading environmental claims.
*   **Strategic Integration vs. Risk Neutralization**: True leadership requires moving beyond defensive risk neutralization—which relies on compliance, due diligence, and carbon offsetting—to active strategic integration. This involves:
    *   *Emissions Accounting*: Actively measuring and reducing Scope 1 (direct), Scope 2 (indirect energy), and Scope 3 (value chain) emissions with immediate, concrete targets.
    *   *Carbon Shadow Pricing*: Internalizing the cost of carbon into capital expenditure decisions to evaluate the true long-term return on investment.
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: This framework attempts to address the **internalization of ecological externalities into corporate balance sheets**, a structural tension arising from the market's historical failure to price the consumption of common-pool resources.
*   **Purpose-Driven Strategy**: A business must define its "purpose" as the fundamental reason for its existence and its contribution to society, rather than what it produces.
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: This concept addresses the **social legitimacy of private capital accumulation**, resolving the tension between private profit-making and the collective welfare of the society that hosts the enterprise.
*   **Stakeholder Mapping and Integration**: Utilizing interest-and-influence matrices to categorize and manage relationships with external actors (e.g., local communities, regulators, activist NGOs).
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: This tool is an attempt to address the **asymmetric distribution of power and voice among those who bear the consequences of corporate activity but do not own its shares**.
*   **Stewardship Leadership**: A leadership model based on safeguarding resources for future generations, driven by a long-term view, ownership mentality, interdependence, and creative resilience.
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: This model attempts to address **intergenerational resource preservation**, the ethical and physical necessity of maintaining the biosphere's carrying capacity across time.
*   **Ecosystem and Collaborative Leadership**: The practice of leading beyond organizational boundaries through soft power, consensus-building, and loose partner networks to deploy distributed sustainability solutions.
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: This concept addresses the **coordination of production and resource allocation across legally distinct but economically interdependent entities** in the absence of centralized state planning or direct corporate ownership.

## 3. Notable Mentions

### Named References and Case Studies

*   **Royal Dutch Shell (Milieudefensie et al. v. Royal Dutch Shell)**: Cited as a landmark legal risk. The Hague District Court ordered Shell to cut its CO2 emissions by 45% by 2030, demonstrating that courts can enforce emissions reductions beyond a company's voluntary targets.

*   **Rio Tinto (Juukan Gorge Debacle)**: Invoked as a cautionary tale of neglecting stakeholder values. The destruction of a 46,000-year-old sacred Aboriginal site for short-term iron ore extraction led to an investor backlash that forced the resignation of CEO Jean-Sébastien Jacques and other executives.
*   **Jamie Dimon (JPMorgan Chase / Business Roundtable) & Larry Fink (BlackRock)**: Cited to legitimize "stakeholder capitalism." Their public letters and statements are used to show that the highest echelons of global finance endorse a shift away from exclusive shareholder primacy.

*   **Dassault Systemes, Viva Energy Australia, & Banyan Tree Group**: Invoked as exemplary models of companies that have successfully integrated sustainability and social responsibility into their core organizational purpose statements.

### Conspicuous Absences
*   **Milton Friedman**: While De Meyer dismisses the "mantra of exclusively focusing on shareholder value" as "one from the past," he conspicuously omits any engagement with Friedman's structural argument: that corporate executives are agents of the shareholders and have a fiduciary duty to maximize profits within the law. Omitting Friedman obscures the reality that corporate law and capital markets are still structurally organized around shareholder primacy, regardless of changing cultural attitudes.
*   **Karl Polanyi**: The lecture describes the rise of regulatory and legal risks as external disruptions. Including Polanyi’s concept of the "double movement"—the inevitable social and political backlash against the market's attempt to commodify and destroy nature and labor—would ground these risks as systemic, predictable reactions to market expansion rather than random external shocks.
*   **Indigenous Sovereignty and Land Rights Frameworks**: In discussing Rio Tinto and Juukan Gorge, the lecture frames the issue as a failure of "stakeholder engagement" and "internal accountability." It omits any discussion of the structural legal frameworks that allowed Rio Tinto to legally obtain permission to destroy the site in the first place. This omission obscures the first principle of **asymmetric property rights**, where state-backed colonial legal structures systematically privilege corporate resource extraction over indigenous land tenure.

## 4. Internal Contradictions & Stress Points

### The Voluntary Stewardship vs. Fiduciary Duty Contradiction
The lecture praises Larry Fink of BlackRock and Jamie Dimon of JPMorgan Chase for championing "stakeholder capitalism." However, the core mechanism of their power is the management of private capital with the legal and structural mandate to maximize risk-adjusted financial returns for their clients. The lecture fails to explain how a corporate manager can prioritize non-shareholder interests when capital markets can instantly divest from underperforming firms or launch hostile activist campaigns to restore short-term profitability. 

*   *Underlying First Principle*: **The structural contradiction between private capital accumulation and social/ecological reproduction.** Under capitalism, the primary directive of capital is self-expansion ($M-C-M'$). Any voluntary diversion of surplus toward ecological preservation that reduces competitive returns is structurally penalized by the market.

### The "Win-Win" Ecosystem vs. Zero-Sum Resource Extraction Contradiction
The Stewardship Compass rejects the notion of a "zero-sum game," asserting that steward leaders "develop the win-win habit." Yet, the very examples used to illustrate the risks of neglecting sustainability are fundamentally zero-sum. In the Rio Tinto case, the company blew up the Juukan Gorge caves because doing so allowed them to access higher-grade iron ore. There was no "win-win" compromise; either the 46,000-year-old sacred site was preserved, or the high-value ore was extracted. By insisting that sustainability is always a "business opportunity" or a "win-win," the lecture avoids addressing how leaders should make decisions when interests are irreconcilably opposed.

*   *Underlying First Principle*: **The physical and spatial limits of capital expansion.** The physical extraction of material resources from the earth is inherently destructive to localized ecosystems and cultural geographies; it cannot be harmonized with preservation through managerial rhetoric.

### The Greenwashing vs. Capitalist Growth Contradiction
De Meyer critiques BP and Marks & Spencer for greenwashing, suggesting that the solution is "transparent reporting" (using frameworks like TCFD, GRI, or ISSB) and "good governance." However, the lecture's own data on BP reveals that 96% of its capital expenditure was in fossil fuels. Greenwashing is not a failure of "transparency" or "culture"; it is a rational marketing strategy to attract ESG-conscious capital and consumers while maintaining the highly profitable, carbon-intensive operations that underpin the firm's financial valuation. If BP had aligned its capital expenditure with its marketing, it would have had to write off the vast majority of its assets, destroying its market value.

*   *Underlying First Principle*: **The divergence between symbolic representation (reporting) and material reality (resource throughput).** In a market economy, the pressure to maintain financial valuation incentivizes the manipulation of symbols and narratives to obscure the material reality of ecological degradation.

## 5. Ideological Audit

### The Market as the Ultimate Arbiter of Ecological Value
The lecture frames the transition to sustainability as a response to "opportunities" such as millennial consumer demand and talent attraction. This takes for granted that ecological survival must be validated by market profitability and consumer willingness-to-pay to be worthy of pursuit. 

*   *Why this is ideological*: It subordinates the physical survival of the biosphere to the logic of consumer preference and market demand. It implies that if consumers were *not* willing to pay more, or if workers did not care about values, there would be no rational business case for preventing ecological collapse.
*   *Who benefits*: Corporate executives and capital owners, as it positions them as responsive servants of the market rather than active agents of ecological destruction, while keeping the power to allocate resources firmly in private hands.
*   *What it makes impossible to think*: The necessity of **de-commodifying** essential resources (like water, energy, and stable climates) and managing them through democratic, non-market planning.
*   *Suppressed First Principle*: **The intrinsic value of life-support systems independent of market valuation.**

### The Neutrality and Benevolence of Corporate Power
The lecture treats the corporation as a neutral, malleable vehicle that can be steered toward "stewardship" if its leaders adopt the right "mindset" or "compass." It presents the state, activist NGOs, and local communities merely as "stakeholders" to be managed, rather than as classes or groups with structurally antagonistic interests.

*   *Why this is ideological*: It ignores the class dynamics of corporate ownership. A corporation is not a neutral community asset; it is private property structured to extract surplus value from labor and nature for the benefit of shareholders.
*   *Who benefits*: The ruling managerial and investor class, by presenting corporate leadership as a form of public service ("stewardship") and obscuring the undemocratic nature of corporate governance.
*   *What it makes impossible to think*: That the corporate form itself is structurally incompatible with ecological stability, and that genuine sustainability requires the democratic socialization of the means of production.
*   *Suppressed First Principle*: **The asymmetric distribution of class power and decision-making authority inherent in private ownership.**

### The "Ecosystem" as a Harmonious, Power-Free Network
The lecture uses biological metaphors like "business ecosystems" to describe supply chains and partner networks, suggesting that leadership within these networks is a matter of "soft power," "listening," and "consensus-building."

*   *Why this is ideological*: It obscures the coercive economic power that lead firms (like multinational retailers or energy giants) exercise over their suppliers, particularly in the Global South. These are not voluntary, harmonious ecosystems; they are highly unequal, hierarchical value chains where risk and exploitation are systematically outsourced to the weakest links.
*   *Who benefits*: Multinational corporations, by shielding them from accountability for the labor exploitation and environmental degradation occurring in their supply chains under the guise of "collaborative, loosely coupled networks."
*   *What it makes impossible to think*: The structural necessity of international labor solidarity and state-enforced supply chain liability to dismantle unequal ecological exchange.
*   *Suppressed First Principle*: **The imperialist division of labor and unequal ecological exchange.**

## 6. Materialist Reframe

### The Material Reality of "Stewardship" Mindsets
The lecture presents "stewardship" as a psychological and cognitive orientation (the Hernandez & Morela model). In material reality, corporate managers are employees of capital. Their tenure, compensation, and social status are tied to the performance of the firm's stock and its profitability. If a "steward leader" attempts to prioritize long-term ecological health in a way that materially reduces quarterly dividends or capital growth, the financial markets will punish the firm. Activist investors (like Engine No. 1 or Elliott Management) will buy up shares, pressure the board, and replace the executive with someone focused on cost-cutting and profit maximization. The "stewardship mindset" can only exist as long as it does not interfere with the material imperative of capital self-expansion.

```
[Material Structure of Capital Markets]
  │ (Demands maximum risk-adjusted returns)
  ▼
[Corporate Board & Executive Compensation]
  │ (Tied to stock price & quarterly profitability)
  ▼
[Managerial Action] ──(Voluntary "Stewardship" allowed ONLY if profitable)──► [Greenwashing / Selective Compliance]
  │
  └─(If genuine stewardship reduces profits)──► [Market Punishment: Divestment / Hostile Takeover / CEO Fired]
```

*   *First Principle at Stake*: **The separation of ownership and control, and the structural imperative of capital accumulation.**

### The Material Basis of Greenwashing
Greenwashing is not a moral failing or a lack of "clear governance." It is a rational material response to a structural contradiction: the need to access cheap capital (which is increasingly tied to ESG mandates by institutional investors like BlackRock) while maintaining high-yield, carbon-intensive assets that underpin the firm's actual financial valuation. For a company like BP, writing off its fossil fuel reserves to match its "green" rhetoric would result in immediate insolvency. Greenwashing is therefore a material necessity to bridge the gap between the physical reality of resource extraction and the financial reality of ESG-driven capital markets.

*   *First Principle at Stake*: **The structural necessity of cost externalization under competitive market pressures.**

### The Material Reality of "Stakeholder Engagement"
The stakeholder mapping tool (Mendelow's Matrix) categorizes actors by "influence" (power) and "interest." This is not an ethical framework; it is a risk-mitigation tool designed to protect corporate operations from disruption. 
*   "Low influence, low interest" groups (e.g., marginalized communities in the Global South) are merely "monitored."
*   "High influence, high interest" groups (e.g., powerful state regulators or institutional investors) are "managed closely."

The matrix does not distribute power; it maps existing power dynamics so the corporation can neutralize threats to its capital accumulation. The Rio Tinto debacle occurred not because the company lacked "stakeholder engagement," but because the indigenous owners lacked the material and legal power to halt the mining lease until *after* the destruction caused a global public relations crisis that threatened the company's access to capital.

*   *First Principle at Stake*: **The translation of economic power into political and operational dominance.**

## 7. What This Means for Making Change

### Real Levers of Change
If we reject the idealist assumption that better values and "stewardship compasses" produce better outcomes, we must identify the material levers that actually force structural change:

*   **Regulatory Coercion and State Bans**: The lecture notes that 3M was forced to abandon PFOS because it was added to the Stockholm Convention. This was not a voluntary "stewardship" decision; it was a state-enforced ban backed by the threat of criminal and financial penalties. The primary lever of change is the state's power to legally prohibit ecological destruction and enforce those prohibitions through the courts.
*   **Militant Litigation and Legal Precedent**: The Shell case in the Netherlands demonstrates that organized civil society can use the coercive power of the judiciary to override corporate strategy. Change is achieved by establishing legal precedents that hold corporate entities and their executives personally liable for ecological damage.
*   **Disruptive Counter-Power**: The resignation of Rio Tinto's CEO was not caused by a sudden moral awakening on the board, but by intense pressure from institutional investors who feared a collapse in brand equity and subsequent divestment. This pressure was generated by public outcry, investigative journalism, and indigenous mobilization. The lever of change is the creation of disruptive counter-power that threatens the material reproduction of capital.

### Structural Obstacles to Change
*   **Fiduciary Duty and Corporate Law**: The legal structure of the joint-stock corporation mandates that directors act in the best financial interests of the shareholders. This legal barrier prevents voluntary, systemic resource preservation.
*   **Global Regulatory Arbitrage**: In a globalized economy, capital can easily bypass national regulations by relocating production to jurisdictions with weaker environmental and labor standards. This material mobility undermines local regulatory efforts.

### Historical Precedents of Structural Reform
Historically, significant improvements in environmental and labor conditions have never been achieved through voluntary corporate stewardship. 
*   The abolition of child labor, the establishment of the eight-hour workday, and the creation of environmental protection agencies (like the US EPA) were the results of decades of militant labor struggles, mass social movements, and state intervention. 
*   Capitalists did not voluntarily adopt these reforms because they developed a "long-term view"; they were forced to adopt them as the price of social peace and the preservation of the system as a whole.

### Action for Individuals Inside and Outside the System
For a student, professional, or citizen operating within this system, taking this knowledge seriously means abandoning the illusion of "changing the system from within" through voluntary corporate social responsibility (CSR) roles. Instead, realistic action involves:

*   **Organizing Collective Counter-Power**: Inside the firm, this means organizing workers (unions) to demand democratic control over operational decisions, safety standards, and environmental impacts.
*   **Whistleblowing and Tactical Leaking**: Utilizing access to corporate information to expose greenwashing, regulatory non-compliance, and externalized costs to investigative journalists, regulators, and activist groups.
*   **Building Non-Market Alternatives**: Directing professional skills toward the development of cooperative, public, or municipal enterprises that operate on non-capitalist logics (e.g., public transit authorities, municipal energy grids, worker cooperatives).
*   **Advocating for Structural Regulation**: Working outside the corporate sphere to build political coalitions that can capture state power and implement hard, legally binding limits on capital, such as carbon bans, supply chain liability laws, and the de-commodification of natural monopolies.

*   *First Principle at Stake*: **The democratic subordination of private property rights to social and ecological limits.**

## 8. The Knowledge You Actually Need

To gain genuine analytical power over this subject, a reader must look beyond managerial frameworks and engage with the following disciplines:

### 1. Political Economy
To understand how corporate law, financial markets, and state power co-produce the structural imperatives of capital accumulation, and why voluntary ethical frameworks are systematically neutralized by market competition.
*   *What it answers*: Why do well-intentioned CEOs routinely make ecologically destructive decisions?
*   *Precise Entry Point*: **Karl Polanyi, *The Great Transformation***. Polanyi explains how the fiction of treating land, labor, and money as commodities inevitably destroys the social and natural fabric of society, triggering a necessary "double movement" of social protection.
*   *First Principle*: **The commodification of nature and labor.**

### 2. Ecological Economics
To understand the economy as a open subsystem of a finite, thermodynamic biosphere, demonstrating why "green growth" and decoupling are physical impossibilities within a system requiring infinite expansion.
*   *What it answers*: Why is "carbon shadow pricing" and "offsetting" insufficient to halt ecological degradation?
*   *Precise Entry Point*: **Herman Daly, *Steady-State Economics***. Daly outlines the physical limits of material throughput and argues for an economy organized around biophysical boundaries rather than abstract financial growth.
*   *First Principle*: **The thermodynamic limits of material throughput.**

### 3. Regulatory Theory and Policy Studies
To understand how corporations actively capture, weaken, and rewrite the state regulations designed to control them, and how to design resilient regulatory frameworks that resist capture.
*   *What it answers*: Why do regulatory frameworks (like the Stockholm Convention or carbon trading markets) often take decades to address obvious ecological harms?
*   *Precise Entry Point*: **Daniel Carpenter and David Moss, *Preventing Regulatory Capture***. This text provides an empirical analysis of how industries subvert public interest regulation and offers strategies for building independent regulatory capacity.
*   *First Principle*: **Regulatory capture and the asymmetry of lobbying power.**

### 4. Labour History and Social Movement Theory
To understand how structural reforms have historically been won through collective struggle, disruption, and the building of counter-power, rather than elite benevolence.
*   *What it answers*: What are the actual historical mechanisms that have successfully forced capital to internalize its social and environmental costs?
*   *Precise Entry Point*: **Erik Olin Wright, *How to Be an Anti-Capitalist in the Twenty-First Century***. Wright offers a clear framework for understanding how social movements can erode, tame, and escape capitalist structures through symbiotic and interstitial strategies.
*   *First Principle*: **The necessity of collective counter-power for structural change.**

### Through-Line
These disciplines form an integrated analytical capacity that shifts the focus from **individual psychology** (the "stewardship mindset") to **structural dynamics** (how rules, power, and physical limits shape human behavior). By combining the biophysical realism of *Ecological Economics* with the power analysis of *Political Economy*, the institutional realism of *Regulatory Theory*, and the strategic history of *Labour Economics*, the reader learns to see the corporate form not as a vehicle for moral redemption, but as a highly constrained node in a global system of resource extraction and capital accumulation. This integrated capacity allows one to analyze any corporate sustainability initiative and immediately identify its material limits, its ideological function, and the real levers required to force genuine change.

## 9. A Note to the Reader

The frustration you may feel while sitting in lectures that present voluntary corporate stewardship as a viable solution to global ecological collapse is not a personal failing. It is not cynicism, and it is not defeatist. It is the rational, healthy response of someone who is paying attention to the material reality of the world. You are being asked to study frameworks that treat the symptoms of a systemic crisis as if they were merely managerial inefficiencies, while the underlying drivers of that crisis—the structural necessity of infinite growth, the commodification of the biosphere, and the undemocratic concentration of economic power—remain completely unnameable within the curriculum. 

This education can produce a profound sense of helplessness, but that helplessness is functional. A student who believes that the current corporate-led, market-validated arrangement is the only possible operating system for a complex society will not attempt to build anything else. The despair is manufactured to keep you compliant. Recognizing that this framework is an ideological construction designed to protect specific material interests is the first step toward reclaiming your agency.

The problems these corporate frameworks claim to address are real. The externalization of ecological costs onto the powerless, the exclusion of unrepresented interests from consequential decisions, and the divergence between GDP growth and human welfare are the defining challenges of our era. The failure of "stewardship compasses" and "ESG reporting" to solve these problems does not make the problems disappear; it makes the need for rigorous, structural thinking more urgent. You are not studying something irrelevant; you are studying the official, institutional response to these crises, and learning to see exactly why that response is structurally inadequate. That is a powerful place to start.

The horizon of human organization is not fixed. The assumption that liberal capitalism is the only viable way to run a modern society is empirically false. Around the world, and throughout history, people have built and maintained highly complex, functioning alternatives that solve these exact structural tensions through different mechanisms:

*   **The Mondragon Cooperative Network** in Spain has sustained worker ownership and democratic governance at scale across industrial, financial, and retail sectors for over sixty years, proving that enterprises can operate successfully without private shareholders.
*   **Kerala’s Development Model** in India has achieved human development outcomes (in literacy, life expectancy, and healthcare) comparable to wealthy Western nations at a fraction of the GDP, through sustained public investment, land reform, and collective mobilization.
*   **Nordic Co-determination Frameworks** have historically institutionalized worker power by legally mandating employee representation on corporate boards, demonstrating that the absolute authority of capital owners can be structurally curtailed.

These models are not perfect; they contain their own internal contradictions and are constantly forced to negotiate with the global capitalist market. But they prove empirically that the relationship between economic activity and social welfare can be organized around principles other than the maximization of shareholder value. They show that when we design institutions that prioritize democratic representation and resource preservation, we get different, more resilient outcomes.

You are being trained in the language, logic, and tools of institutions that wield immense power. That training is not useless. Fluency in the grammar of capital is a powerful asset, provided you do not mistake it for the limits of your own imagination. The question is not whether you should engage with these systems, but how you engage without being absorbed by them. By grounding yourself in first principles rather than branded corporate frameworks, you maintain your analytical independence. You learn to see the difference between what a framework says it does and what its material incentives actually produce. 

Take this knowledge seriously. Do not use it merely to critique, but to orient yourself toward action. The task is not to find a better corporate brand or a more sophisticated reporting standard. The task is to build the material, political, and social counter-power necessary to subordinate private profit to the survival of the biosphere and the flourishing of humanity. 

As Karl Marx famously observed in his eleventh thesis on Feuerbach: *"The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways; the point is to change it."* Use your education to understand the machinery of the world, so that you can join the collective effort to dismantle what is destructive and build what is necessary. That is the most honest form of hope available.

Video 5: Sustainability’s Role in Business Strategy
Following the discussion, we had about risks and opportunities, it is quite clear that you have two ways
of reacting. Either you can attempt to neutralise the risks, or you embed sustainability in your business
strategy. Neutralising the risks can be done in four steps. You exercise due diligence, you comply with
regulations and disclosure requirements, you mitigate the risks, and you apply carbon off setting. In
fact, you probably will need to apply these four steps anyway. But if you want to be a leader in
sustainability, this will not be enough. Sustainability, as a business strategy, requires at least three
additional actions. First, put in place programmes to actively account for and reduce scope one, two
and three emissions. As will be discussed in more detail in module 8, the green gas house emissions
have been categorised into three groups. Scope one emissions are those that come from sources that
are directly from the site, that is producing a product or delivering a service. For the industry, these
would be the emissions related to, for example, burning a fuel on site. On the individual level,
emissions from personal vehicles or gas-burning stoves would fall under scope one. Scope two
emissions are the indirect emissions associated with the purchase of electricity, steam, heat or cooling.
Although scope two emissions physically occur at the facility where they are generated, they are
accounted for in an organisation's greenhouse gas inventory because they are the result of the
organisation's energy use. And then, scope three emissions include all other indirect emissions that
occur in a company's value chain. Typical examples are purchased goods and services, business travel,
employee commuting, waste disposal, use of sold products, transportation and distribution, up and
downstream, investments, leased assets, and franchises, etc. I suggest that you carefully analyse what
your scope one, two, and three greenhouse gas emissions are and that you launch a few programmes
to reduce these over time. And not with a lofty goal to reduce your greenhouse gas emissions by X
percent by 2050, but with concrete goals for next year. Secondly, actively search for business
opportunities in sustainability. We already referred to this earlier, when we gave the example of the
French software company Dassault Systemes, but there are myriads of other examples of companies
that take advantage of for example, the energy transition to develop new businesses in the design and
production of wind turbines, the development of electric vehicles, the production of sustainable food,
etc. And thirdly, apply carbon shadow pricing to understand the true cost of your activities and
investments. In this graph, you see how the EU carbon trading prices have surged over the years. That
means that if you re-calculate your return on investment on taking into account the carbon offset
pricing for the emissions, you will produce that some of the currently attractive investments may
become lesser when you include the environmental impact of the investment.
