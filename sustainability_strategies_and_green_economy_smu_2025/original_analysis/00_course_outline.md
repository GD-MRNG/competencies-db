# SMU Sustainability Strategies and Green Economy Programme Outline

## Summary

This programme is a 14-week online executive education course offered by Singapore Management University (SMU) Lee Kong Chian School of Business, in collaboration with Emeritus. It is fundamentally about equipping senior business professionals and leaders with the knowledge and frameworks needed to embed sustainability into their organisations' strategic decision-making — spanning operations, finance, marketing, governance, and leadership.

> **First principle at stake:** How do organisations internalise costs they are currently externalising onto ecosystems, communities, and future generations — and who decides whether and how that happens?
>
> **What to watch for:** The programme is addressed to senior leaders and organisations. This means its proposed solutions will almost exclusively operate at the level of managerial decision-making and organisational strategy. Solutions that require structural change — binding regulation, ownership reform, international enforcement — will be underrepresented or absent. Note where the programme's proposed mechanism for change (better-informed leaders) meets the limits of what individuals inside organisations can actually do against structural incentives.

---

The course addresses a pressing business reality: sustainability has shifted from a compliance concern to a core competitive imperative. Consumers increasingly reward sustainable behaviour, regulators are tightening environmental standards, and global frameworks such as the UN Sustainable Development Goals are reshaping how organisations define value. The programme responds to leaders who need to act on these pressures but lack structured knowledge of how to do so — translating concepts like circular economy, carbon footprint, green finance, and ESG reporting into practical organisational strategies.

> **Ideological flag:** The framing of sustainability as a "competitive imperative" is doing significant work here. It positions sustainability as something businesses should pursue because it is profitable — not because ecological destruction and human harm are wrong. This framing has a consequence: it makes sustainability contingent on market conditions. If sustainable behaviour stops being competitively advantageous — if consumers stop rewarding it, if regulators weaken, if green finance dries up — the business case evaporates. The underlying principle — that we have obligations to ecosystems and future generations that exist independently of whether honouring them is profitable — cannot be stated within this framing.
>
> **First principle suppressed:** The obligation to not destroy shared resources that others depend on is not contingent on competitive advantage. It is a principle of common stewardship — the recognition that some goods are held in common and cannot be legitimately destroyed for private gain regardless of market signals.

---

Learners completing this programme should emerge able to assess their organisation's sustainability posture, design and implement sustainability strategies aligned to the Triple Bottom Line (People, Planet, Profit), understand the circular economy and extended producer responsibility, evaluate green financial instruments and sustainable investment, develop marketing approaches for green products, and exercise sustainability leadership under changing environmental and regulatory conditions.

> **Framework inventory — first principles beneath the brands:**
>
> - **Triple Bottom Line** → underlying principle: *externalised costs*. Firms routinely transfer costs onto parties who have no voice in the transaction — workers, communities, ecosystems, future generations. The Triple Bottom Line is an attempt to make those costs visible. Its limitation is that it is voluntary and therefore cannot compel firms to internalise costs when doing so reduces profit.
> - **Circular Economy** → underlying principle: *throughput reduction*. Linear production systems extract, produce, and discard — converting resources into waste at a rate the biosphere cannot absorb. The circular economy addresses the throughput problem. Its limitation is that it can be applied at the margins of a growth-dependent system without addressing the growth imperative itself.
> - **Extended Producer Responsibility** → underlying principle: *cost attribution*. Who bears the cost of a product's end of life — the producer who profited from it, or the public who must dispose of it? EPR attempts to return that cost to its source. It is one of the few mechanisms in this programme with binding regulatory force.
> - **ESG Reporting** → underlying principle: *information asymmetry*. Investors and the public cannot make informed decisions about the real costs and risks of corporate activity without disclosure. ESG reporting attempts to reduce this asymmetry. Its limitation is that voluntary and inconsistent reporting standards allow selective disclosure that signals virtue without requiring accountability.
> - **Green Finance** → underlying principle: *capital allocation and incentive alignment*. If capital flows toward activities that destroy ecosystems because those activities are more profitable, redirecting capital requires either changing relative profitability (through regulation or taxation) or creating new instruments that make sustainable activity financially competitive. Green finance attempts the latter without the former.

## Topics

---

**Sustainable Consumption** — Examines why overconsumption occurs, the importance of sustainable consumption as a social and business issue, and how organisations can influence consumers to make more sustainable choices.

> **First principle:** The question underneath sustainable consumption is not primarily about consumer behaviour — it is about *who bears the cost of consumption patterns*. Overconsumption occurs because the full costs of production and disposal are not borne by the consumer or producer at the point of transaction. They are distributed onto ecosystems, low-wage workers in supply chains, and future generations who have no voice in the exchange. Framing this as a problem of consumer choice — solvable by influencing individual decisions — locates the solution at the wrong level.
>
> **What the topic can and cannot address:** Behavioural change at the consumer level is real but operates at the margins. The structural drivers of overconsumption — planned obsolescence, advertising, the absence of true-cost pricing, the externality of waste — require systemic rather than behavioural intervention. Watch for whether the module addresses these structural drivers or focuses primarily on how businesses can *market* sustainable consumption.
>
> **Concept to carry forward:** *True-cost accounting* — the practice of calculating the full social and ecological cost of a product including externalities. When the price of a product reflects its true cost, consumption patterns change without requiring behaviour change campaigns. This concept will recur across multiple modules under different names.

---

**Business Sustainability Strategies and Circular Economy** — Covers the strategic frameworks businesses use to pursue sustainability, with a focus on the circular economy model — how reducing, reusing, and regenerating materials can drive both economic growth and environmental benefit.

> **First principle:** The circular economy addresses *throughput* — the rate at which resources are extracted from and returned to natural systems. The underlying tension it is trying to resolve is between an economic model premised on continuous growth and a biosphere with finite regenerative capacity. This tension is real and important. The question the module is unlikely to ask is whether circular economy principles, applied within a growth-dependent capitalist system, can reduce throughput fast enough to matter — or whether they optimise the efficiency of a fundamentally incompatible system without resolving the incompatibility.
>
> **Phrase to interrogate:** "drive both economic growth and environmental benefit." This conjunction — growth *and* environmental benefit — is the central claim of green capitalism and the central tension of this entire programme. It is an empirical claim, not an axiom. The evidence for absolute decoupling of economic growth from resource throughput and emissions at the scale and speed required is, at best, contested. Note each time this conjunction appears and ask: under what conditions does it hold, and under what conditions does growth win?
>
> **First principle suppressed:** The question of *sufficiency* — how much is enough — cannot be asked within a framework committed to growth. Sufficiency economics, associated with thinkers like Serge Latouche and the degrowth movement, names this as the foundational question. It will not appear in this module.

---

**Sustainable Operations** — Explores how organisations can operationalise sustainability within their supply chains and production processes, including Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) legislation, industrial symbiosis, the sustainable hierarchy framework, and the sense-response framework for sourcing.

> **First principle:** Supply chain sustainability addresses the problem of *distributed harm* — the fact that production systems distribute their costs across multiple jurisdictions, time horizons, and populations, making accountability difficult to establish and enforce. A firm can claim sustainability credentials for its domestic operations while its suppliers in lower-regulation jurisdictions externalise environmental and labour costs that the firm's pricing captures as margin.
>
> **EPR as the exception worth noting:** Extended Producer Responsibility is one of the few regulatory instruments in this programme — a binding rule that attributes end-of-life costs to producers rather than the public. It is worth studying carefully because it exemplifies the principle of *cost internalisation through mandatory attribution* rather than voluntary commitment. Ask how EPR compares in effectiveness to the voluntary frameworks that dominate the rest of the programme.
>
> **Industrial symbiosis** → underlying principle: *waste as misallocated resource*. One firm's waste is another firm's input. This is a genuine systemic insight — it moves beyond waste reduction toward waste elimination through network design. It is also one of the few concepts in this programme that operates at the level of system architecture rather than individual firm behaviour.
>
> **What the module is unlikely to address:** The structural reason supply chains externalise costs is that global trade architecture — trade agreements, investment treaties, regulatory arbitrage — creates incentives for firms to locate harmful production in low-regulation jurisdictions. No operational framework within a single firm can resolve this without the regulatory architecture changing. Watch for whether this structural context is named.

---

**Sustainability Reporting and Governance** — Addresses how organisations measure, report, and govern their sustainability performance, including carbon footprint analysis, national and international regulatory frameworks, and critique of existing sustainability policies.

> **First principle:** Reporting and governance address the problem of *information asymmetry and accountability*. If stakeholders — investors, regulators, communities — cannot observe what firms are actually doing to ecosystems and people, they cannot hold firms accountable. Reporting is an attempt to make the invisible visible. Its limitation is structural: voluntary reporting allows selective disclosure, inconsistent standards allow comparison to be gamed, and the absence of independent verification allows misrepresentation. Mandatory, standardised, independently audited disclosure is a different instrument from voluntary ESG reporting — and the gap between them is where the accountability problem lives.
>
> **Carbon footprint analysis** → underlying principle: *attribution of emissions responsibility*. Who is responsible for a given unit of carbon — the producer, the consumer, the investor, the state that permitted the activity? Carbon footprint methodology embeds assumptions about attribution that have significant political and distributional consequences. Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions represent progressively more complete attribution. Watch which scope the module treats as standard and which it treats as optional.
>
> **The critique of existing policies** is the most valuable part of this module's description. If the module genuinely subjects sustainability policy to critical scrutiny — asking why policies that have existed for decades have not produced the outcomes they promised — it will be doing the most important analytical work in the programme. If "critique" means identifying implementation gaps rather than structural inadequacies, it will be doing something less useful. Hold this module to the higher standard.

---

**Leading as a Steward for Sustainability** — Focuses on the leadership dimensions of sustainability, including purpose and stakeholder analysis, collaborative and steward leadership models, and how to assess risks and opportunities associated with different leadership approaches to sustainability.

> **First principle:** Leadership modules in sustainability programmes are addressing the question of *agency within constraint* — what can an individual in a position of institutional authority actually do, given the structural incentives the institution operates under? This is a genuinely important question. Its limitation is that it can slide into the assumption that better leadership is the primary lever for structural change — when the evidence suggests that structural change (regulation, ownership reform, binding standards) is the primary lever, and leadership operates within the space that structure permits.
>
> **Steward leadership** → underlying principle: *fiduciary responsibility to non-shareholders*. The steward model asks leaders to act as trustees for stakeholders beyond shareholders — employees, communities, ecosystems, future generations. This is a meaningful ethical commitment. The structural question it cannot answer within its own frame is: what happens when steward leadership conflicts with the capital market's demand for return? Who wins, and why? The answer is almost always structural — the capital market wins — and no leadership model changes this without changing the ownership structure or the regulatory environment.
>
> **What to carry into this module:** The distinction between *leadership within a structure* and *leadership that changes a structure*. The most transformative leaders in history — those who moved large structures — did so not primarily through better values or collaborative models but through building organised power: coalitions, movements, political pressure, and institutional redesign. Ask whether this module can name that kind of leadership, or whether its conception of stewardship remains bounded by what is currently permissible within existing institutions.

---

**Driving Innovation through Impactful Solutions** — Examines how sustainability can be a driver of innovation, using frameworks such as ReSOLVE and Regenerate-Rewire-Inspire to develop sustainable innovations across business models and operations.

> **First principle:** Innovation modules address the question of *whether the system can generate solutions to the problems it creates* — whether market-driven innovation, responding to competitive and regulatory pressures, can produce the scale and speed of transformation the ecological crisis requires. This is a genuinely open empirical question. The historical record suggests that market innovation is effective at incremental efficiency improvements and at solving problems where there is a clear profit motive — and less effective at the kind of structural, systemic transformation that does not fit existing business models.
>
> **ReSOLVE and Regenerate-Rewire-Inspire** are proprietary frameworks from the Ellen MacArthur Foundation and related organisations. The underlying principle they are attempting to operationalise is *system redesign* — moving from optimising existing systems to redesigning them at the level of architecture. This is a meaningful distinction. The first-principle question to ask of any innovation framework is: does this redesign the system, or does it optimise the system's current architecture? Most corporate sustainability innovation does the latter.
>
> **What the module is unlikely to name:** The possibility that the most impactful sustainable innovations have come not from corporate R&D but from public research, state investment, and regulated markets — solar and wind cost reductions, battery technology, the internet itself. The assumption that innovation is primarily a private sector activity is embedded in the programme's address to business leaders, and it is worth holding critically.

---

**Marketing Green Products** — Covers consumer behavioural models as applied to green products, life cycle assessment of environmental impact, and the full marketing mix for green products including segmentation, positioning, packaging, pricing, and distribution.

> **First principle:** Marketing modules address the problem of *preference formation and demand signalling* — how consumer choices are shaped and how those choices communicate value to producers. The deeper first principle underneath green marketing is: *who is responsible for the ecological consequences of consumption — the individual consumer, the firm that produces and markets the product, or the regulatory system that sets the conditions under which both operate?* Green marketing as a discipline implicitly locates significant responsibility with the consumer. This has political consequences: it shifts attention from producer responsibility and regulatory failure toward individual lifestyle choice.
>
> **Life Cycle Assessment** → underlying principle: *full-cost attribution across a product's existence*. LCA attempts to account for environmental impact from material extraction through production, use, and disposal. This is a genuinely useful analytical tool. Its limitation is that it measures impact without necessarily attributing responsibility or creating accountability — an LCA can show that a product is environmentally destructive without this having any consequence for whether it is produced or sold.
>
> **Greenwashing** — the gap between green marketing claims and actual environmental performance — is the constitutive risk of this entire module. It emerges from the same structural condition as regulatory capture: the party making the claim controls the information, the standards are often voluntary, and verification is weak. The first-principle question is: what enforcement architecture would make green claims reliable? The answer requires mandatory standards, independent verification, and meaningful penalties — none of which are primarily a marketing problem.
>
> **What to carry forward:** The distinction between *demand-side* and *supply-side* intervention. Green marketing is demand-side — it tries to shift what consumers choose. Supply-side intervention — regulation, taxation, EPR — changes what producers can offer regardless of consumer preference. The historical evidence suggests supply-side intervention is more effective at scale. Ask whether this module can name that asymmetry.

---

**Sustainable Finance** — Introduces sustainable finance as a field, covering green financial instruments, sustainable investing and lending, climate risk management, and the evolving reporting standards that govern environmental financial disclosure.

> **First principle:** Sustainable finance addresses the problem of *capital misallocation* — the fact that under current market conditions, capital flows toward activities that destroy ecosystems because those activities generate higher risk-adjusted returns than sustainable alternatives. The solution sustainable finance proposes is to change the risk-return calculus — by pricing climate risk into asset valuations, creating instruments that make sustainable activity financially competitive, and redirecting institutional capital through ESG mandates. This is a meaningful intervention. The structural question it cannot answer within its own frame is: what if sustainable activity is genuinely less profitable than destructive activity, even after climate risk is priced in? In that case, capital allocation follows profit, and sustainable finance produces green labelling on business-as-usual.
>
> **Green bonds, sustainability-linked loans, ESG funds** → underlying principle: *incentive alignment through financial architecture*. These instruments attempt to make the financial return from sustainable activity comparable to the return from unsustainable activity — through subsidies, risk guarantees, preferential rates, or investor preference. Their effectiveness depends entirely on the integrity of the underlying standards — which returns us to the information asymmetry and greenwashing problem identified in the reporting module.
>
> **Climate risk** is the most structurally significant concept in this module. Repricing climate risk into asset valuations — making stranded assets, physical climate risk, and transition risk legible to capital markets — has the potential to redirect capital at scale without requiring regulatory mandate. It is also the mechanism most likely to be captured by financial industry interests in ways that produce the appearance of risk management without the substance. Watch for the gap between climate risk disclosure and climate risk *action*.
>
> **What this module cannot name:** The possibility that the scale and speed of capital reallocation required to address ecological breakdown exceeds what market mechanisms — even reformed ones — can achieve in the time available, and that direct public investment, state ownership of strategic energy infrastructure, and mandatory divestment from fossil fuels are the necessary complements to green finance rather than alternatives to it.

---

**Low-Carbon Economy** — Explores carbon footprint calculation methodologies (including EIO-LCA), renewable energy sourcing options and their trade-offs, and the broader environmental performance implications of an organisation's products and services.

> **First principle:** The low-carbon economy module addresses the problem of *energy system transition* — moving the material basis of economic activity from fossil fuels to renewable sources without collapsing the productive capacity that current energy systems support. This is a genuine and urgent engineering and political challenge. The first-principle questions it raises are: who pays for the transition, who benefits, who bears the stranded asset losses, and who decides the pace and architecture of change?
>
> **EIO-LCA (Economic Input-Output Life Cycle Assessment)** → underlying principle: *systemic emissions attribution*. Standard LCA attributes emissions at the level of individual products and processes. EIO-LCA attempts to attribute emissions at the level of economic sectors, capturing supply chain interdependencies that product-level analysis misses. It is a more complete picture of the emissions embedded in economic activity. Its limitation is that it describes the problem at a level of aggregation that can obscure where the actionable interventions are.
>
> **Renewable energy sourcing trade-offs** are where the most honest analysis in this module will appear. Renewable energy is not impact-free — land use, mineral extraction for batteries and panels, grid intermittency, and the energy cost of manufacturing renewable infrastructure are all real trade-offs. A module that presents renewables as simply better than fossil fuels without addressing these trade-offs is providing incomplete analysis. A module that addresses them is doing genuine first-principle work.
>
> **What to watch for:** Whether the module treats the low-carbon transition as primarily a technical and financial problem — solvable through better calculation methodologies and cleaner energy sourcing — or as a political economy problem — requiring decisions about who bears transition costs, whose assets are stranded, and which communities bear the physical burden of renewable infrastructure siting. The latter framing is more accurate and more useful.

---

**Sustainability Leadership and Future Trends** — Capstone module addressing how leaders embed sustainability into organisational culture, manage sustainability-related conflicts and disputes, lead through environmental uncertainty, and anticipate emerging technologies and trends shaping the future of sustainable business.

> **First principle:** Capstone modules synthesise what the programme has built — but they also reveal what the programme has taken as its horizon. Watch for what counts as a "future trend" in this module. If the future is defined as better technology, better reporting standards, and better-informed leaders, the programme's horizon ends where structural change begins. If the future includes the possibility of fundamentally different ownership models, binding international regulation, or the political conditions for mandatory decarbonisation, the programme is operating with a wider conception of what is possible.
>
> **Embedding sustainability into organisational culture** → underlying principle: *institutional change through norm formation*. Culture change is real and necessary — institutions that have internalised sustainability as a genuine operating principle behave differently from those that treat it as a compliance exercise. The limitation is that culture operates within structure: a culture of sustainability inside a firm that is structurally required to maximise return will produce sustainability where it is profitable and abandon it where it is not. Culture is not a substitute for structural change; it is a complement to it.
>
> **Managing sustainability-related conflicts** is the most politically honest topic in this module's description. Sustainability is not a consensus project — it involves genuine conflicts of interest between shareholders and communities, between current consumption and future welfare, between producing nations and consuming nations, between the holders of fossil fuel assets and the populations that will bear the costs of their extraction. A module that teaches conflict *management* within existing institutional frameworks is doing something different from a module that names who holds power in these conflicts and what it would take to shift it.
>
> **The question to carry into this capstone:** After fourteen weeks of frameworks, strategies, and leadership models — what has changed structurally? The answer, honestly examined, is probably: very little, without regulatory compulsion. The frameworks are real. The intentions may be genuine. But the structural constraints identified at the beginning of this programme — capital market primacy, competitive pressure, information asymmetry, regulatory weakness — will still be operating when the certificate is awarded. The most important thing a capstone can do is name this honestly and ask: given that, what is actually worth doing, and what is the most useful thing you learned here for doing it?

---

**A final annotation for the programme as a whole:**

The first principle running beneath every module in this programme is this: **the ecological and social costs of economic activity are currently externalised onto those with the least power to resist them — ecosystems, low-wage workers, future generations, and communities in low-regulation jurisdictions.** Every framework this programme teaches is an attempt to address some dimension of that problem. None of them, individually or collectively, is sufficient to resolve it — because resolving it requires structural changes to ownership, regulation, and the political conditions under which markets operate that are outside the programme's frame.

This does not make the programme useless. It makes it a map of the official response to a structural problem — and a map of the official response is exactly what you need before you can identify where the response is adequate, where it is captured, and where the real work remains to be done. Study it on those terms and it will serve you well.
