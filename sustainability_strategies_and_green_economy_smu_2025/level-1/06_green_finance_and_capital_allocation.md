# L1-06 · Green Finance and Capital Allocation

Capital allocation is the quiet variable that determines whether any of the rest of this works. You can design the most sophisticated circular economy, write the most rigorous ESG disclosure standard, pass the most ambitious EPR regulation — and if capital still flows preferentially toward fossil fuel expansion, deforestation, and extractive industry because those remain the most profitable places to put money, the rest is decoration. Green finance is the part of the sustainability conversation that names this directly: nothing changes at scale until the money moves, and the money moves toward returns.

This is the problem green finance is trying to solve, and it has only two structural levers. Either you change the relative profitability of destructive versus sustainable activity — through regulation that bans or taxes the harmful side, or through subsidy that lifts the sustainable side — or you build financial instruments that route capital toward sustainability without waiting for that profitability gap to close. Almost everything you will encounter under the green finance label is the second kind of intervention. Green bonds, sustainability-linked loans, ESG funds, carbon markets: all of them are instruments designed to operate within existing return expectations. None of them, on their own, change the underlying calculation that fossil expansion is more profitable than its alternatives. This is the load-bearing distinction to carry through the module.

Green bonds are debt instruments where the proceeds are earmarked for environmentally beneficial projects. Their integrity depends entirely on two things: the rigour of the use-of-proceeds requirements, and the strength of independent verification that proceeds were actually used that way. A green bond with weak verification is, financially, just a bond — the green label adds reputational value to the issuer without constraining what the capital does. Sustainability-linked loans work differently: the borrower's interest rate is tied to performance against sustainability targets, with rate reductions for hitting them. The mechanism is elegant; the question is whether the targets are material enough that hitting them would have required real change, or soft enough that they were going to be hit anyway.

ESG investing is the most visible instrument and the most analytically slippery. The premise is that investors integrate environmental, social, and governance criteria into portfolio construction, redirecting capital away from harmful firms toward responsible ones. The empirical reality is that many ESG funds hold significant fossil fuel exposure, often through firms that score well on governance or disclosure metrics while continuing to expand extractive operations. The label and the underlying allocation are not the same thing. This is not necessarily fraud — it is a predictable consequence of allowing self-defined ESG criteria, methodological inconsistency between rating providers, and fiduciary frameworks that still privilege return over impact. The instrument can work; the question is whether the version that has scaled is the version that does.

Carbon markets sit in their own category because they attempt to do what the other instruments do not: change relative profitability directly by attaching a price to carbon emissions. Cap-and-trade systems set an aggregate emissions ceiling and let firms trade allowances; carbon taxes set a price per tonne and let quantity adjust. Both are designed to make fossil-intensive activity progressively more expensive until alternatives become competitive. The mechanism is sound. The empirical record is that carbon prices have consistently been set far below the level required to drive the transition. Modelling for a 1.5°C trajectory suggests prices in the range of $100–200 per tonne or higher; current global averages are a fraction of that. The gap is not technical — it is political. Carbon markets under-deliver because the actors with the most to lose from a meaningful price are also the actors with the most influence over the design.

This is where you have to hold two things at once. Green finance instruments are not useless; they have moved real capital, lowered borrowing costs for genuinely sustainable projects, and created infrastructure that mandatory frameworks can later build on. But they have also allowed capital markets to price sustainability as a premium signal — a marketing layer on top of a portfolio whose underlying allocation has not fundamentally changed. The growth of ESG-labelled assets under management has not been matched by a corresponding decline in capital flowing to fossil fuel expansion. Both numbers have grown. The instruments and the destruction are coexisting, which is precisely the outcome you would expect if the instruments are operating at the level of signalling rather than reallocation.

The skill this topic builds is the ability to read a green finance instrument and locate its actual mechanism. Where does the money come from, where does it go, what verifies that, and what would have happened in its absence? An instrument that lowers a sustainable project's cost of capital below what it would otherwise have been is doing something. An instrument that lets an investor describe an unchanged portfolio in greener language is doing something else. The same vocabulary covers both. Your job is not to dismiss the category but to develop the analytical reflex that distinguishes redirection from relabelling — and to notice that the gap between them is where most of the assets currently live.

## Level 2 candidates

**Green bonds and use-of-proceeds requirements** — Covers how green bond integrity is constructed: certification standards, use-of-proceeds tracking, post-issuance reporting, and independent verification. Worth a deeper look because the instrument is genuinely useful when the verification is strong and reputationally laundering when it is not, and the difference is technical enough to be invisible from the label alone.

**Carbon markets: cap-and-trade vs. carbon tax** — Covers the design choices behind the two main carbon pricing mechanisms, including allowance allocation, price floors, offset eligibility, and revenue recycling. Worth deeper examination because the design choices — not the choice between the two mechanisms — are what determine whether a carbon market drives transition or generates tradable paper at a price that changes nothing.

**ESG fund composition and fossil fuel exposure** — Covers the empirical examination of what major ESG funds actually hold, the methodological inconsistencies between rating providers, and the gap between best-in-class scoring and absolute impact. Worth going deeper because this is the single clearest case study in the programme of how a sustainability label can decouple from its underlying allocation, and the analytical move generalises to every other voluntary framework.

**Fiduciary duty and the sustainability obligation** — Covers the legal framework governing institutional investor decisions, the evolving interpretation of fiduciary duty under climate risk, and the structural tension between return maximisation and sustainability mandates. Worth deeper examination because fiduciary duty is the legal mechanism through which the growth imperative reaches into pension funds and insurance pools, and any serious capital reallocation has to pass through its reinterpretation.

**The carbon price required for 1.5°C** — Covers the modelling that produces the $100–200+/tonne estimate, the assumptions behind it, and the gap between modelled requirement and implemented prices across major jurisdictions. Worth deeper examination because the gap between required and actual carbon prices is one of the cleanest empirical anchors in the programme for the scale of political failure, and it makes the limits of market-based instruments concrete in a way no qualitative argument can.
