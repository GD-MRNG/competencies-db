# L1-09 · Regulatory Theory and Institutional Failure

When sustainability commitments fail to produce sustainability outcomes, the instinctive explanation is bad faith — corporations lying, politicians corrupted, regulators asleep. This explanation is satisfying and almost always wrong. The persistent gap between what international frameworks promise and what ecological data measures is not a story about individual moral failure. It is a story about predictable institutional behaviour under predictable structural conditions. Once you see the mechanism, the gap stops looking scandalous and starts looking inevitable — which is the more uncomfortable position, because it means better people in the same roles would produce roughly the same outcomes.

The mechanism has a name and a date: regulatory capture, formalised by George Stigler in 1971. Stigler's claim was deceptively simple. Regulation, he argued, tends over time to be acquired by the industry it is meant to constrain and operated for that industry's benefit. He was not making a moral observation. He was describing what happens when you look honestly at the asymmetry of interests between regulators and the regulated. Industries facing regulation are concentrated — a manageable number of firms, often organised through trade associations, with enormous and specific stakes in regulatory outcomes. The public, in whose name the regulation nominally exists, is diffuse, poorly informed, and faces a classic collective action problem: each individual citizen has only a fractional interest in any particular regulation, and almost no individual incentive to invest the time required to track and contest it.

Run this asymmetry forward and the outcome is structural, not moral. The industry has full-time staff studying every line of every proposed rule. It has lobbying budgets, legal teams, and the technical expertise that regulatory bodies often lack and must therefore borrow. It has long memories and longer time horizons. The public has, at best, an episodic news cycle. Over years and decades, the rules get written, amended, and enforced in an environment where one side is always in the room and the other side is mostly not. You do not need anyone to do anything corrupt for the regulation to drift toward the regulated industry's preferences. You just need time and the ordinary functioning of motivated, well-resourced parties.

This is why the gap between international sustainability frameworks and measured outcomes is so consistent. The Paris Agreement, the Sustainable Development Goals, the Convention on Biological Diversity — these are documents written in negotiations that fossil fuel exporters, agribusiness conglomerates, and industrial trade associations attend with full delegations and detailed positions. The countries and constituencies whose interests would require binding constraints arrive less prepared and leave with weaker text. The result is agreements with ambitious targets, soft enforcement mechanisms, voluntary national commitments, and review processes that depend on self-reporting. The capture is built into the architecture before the ink is dry. None of this requires bad faith from any individual negotiator; it requires only that the structure of who-is-organised-to-care-about-what operates the way it always operates.

The same mechanism explains domestic phenomena that look puzzling otherwise. Why do environmental agencies hire from the industries they regulate, and why do their senior staff so often move to those industries afterward? Because the technical expertise lives there, and because the career incentives of regulatory staff are shaped by the industry they will likely work for next. Why do mandatory disclosure rules emerge weaker than their drafters intended, with carve-outs and phase-ins and materiality thresholds that reduce their bite? Because the drafting process is one in which industry submissions outnumber and outweigh public ones by orders of magnitude. Why do enforcement actions cluster on small actors and rarely touch the largest firms? Because the largest firms have the legal capacity to make enforcement expensive and the political capacity to make it career-limiting.

What this analysis gives you is a diagnostic, not a counsel of despair. Once you understand capture as structural, you can ask the more useful question: under what conditions has it been overcome? The historical answer is consistent. Capture is not undone by appointing better regulators or writing better rules; it is undone by changing the asymmetry of organisation that produced it. The environmental legislation of the early 1970s in the United States — the EPA, the Clean Air Act, the Clean Water Act — emerged in a window when the public side of the equation was, briefly, organised: mass mobilisation, sustained media attention, and political pressure that made resisting regulation more costly than accepting it. The window closed, and capture resumed. The pattern is the same across labour law, financial regulation, and food safety. Binding regulation is achievable, but it requires the diffuse interest to become temporarily concentrated, and it tends to erode once that concentration dissipates.

This is why the regulatory theory layer matters for anyone trying to think clearly about sustainability frameworks. It tells you what to expect from voluntary commitments (slow erosion toward the convenience of the committed), what to expect from mandatory frameworks without enforcement (selective compliance and creative accounting), and what to expect from international agreements without binding mechanisms (a ratchet that runs in only one direction, and not the one the preamble announces). It also tells you what to look for when something does work: not a virtuous regulator, but an organised constituency that made capture more costly than compliance, at least for a time.

Carry this with you into every other module in the programme. When ESG disclosure standards are being debated, ask who is in the room. When carbon pricing schemes are designed, ask whose preferences shaped the cap, the floor, and the exemptions. When supply chain regulations are written, ask whose lobbyists wrote which sub-clause. The answer will not always be cynical, but it will almost always be structural — and structural is where the leverage is.

## Level 2 candidates

**Regulatory capture (Stigler, 1971)** — The canonical economic theory of why regulators come to serve the regulated, including the formal model of concentrated benefits versus diffuse costs. Worth deeper treatment because the original argument is more nuanced than the popular version, and Stigler's framing as positive economics (rather than normative critique) changes how you deploy the concept analytically.

**The revolving door between industry and regulator** — The institutional pipeline by which staff move between regulated industries and the agencies that oversee them, producing structural rather than personal alignment. Worth a deeper look because the mechanism is more subtle than corruption — it operates through career incentives, technical expertise, and social networks, and understanding it changes what reform proposals could plausibly work.

**International regime theory and its limits** — The body of international relations scholarship analysing why agreements without enforcement mechanisms produce predictable patterns of compliance and defection. Worth deeper treatment because it explains the structural similarity between voluntary corporate commitments and international treaties, and gives you a precise vocabulary for distinguishing soft from hard regulatory instruments.

**The IMF and World Bank conditionality problem** — The mechanism by which international financial institutions tie access to capital to regulatory and fiscal conditions that systematically weaken environmental and labour protection in borrower countries. Worth deeper treatment because it reveals a layer of structural pressure operating above the nation-state, which most domestic regulatory analysis cannot see.

**The historical conditions for successful regulation** — The pattern, visible across the labour, environmental, and financial regulatory waves, in which binding rules emerge only when diffuse interests temporarily concentrate through mass mobilisation. Worth deeper treatment because it gives you a positive theory of regulatory success to set against the negative theory of capture, and clarifies what kind of action is and is not commensurate with the problem.
