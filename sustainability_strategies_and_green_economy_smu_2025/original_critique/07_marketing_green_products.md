## 2. Key Takeaways & Learning Points

*   **The Attitude-Behaviour Gap and the MAO Framework**: Consumers frequently express pro-environmental attitudes but fail to translate them into purchasing decisions. The **Motivation, Ability, and Opportunity (MAO)** framework is used to diagnose and bridge this gap.
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: This framework is an attempt to address the *structural constraints on individual agency within a market-dominated society*. It recognizes that individual intent is consistently subordinated to systemic barriers such as time poverty, cognitive overload, and financial inequality.
*   **Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)**: A systematic, quantitative four-stage process (Goal Definition, Inventory Analysis, Impact Assessment, and Interpretation) used to measure the total environmental footprint of a product.
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: LCA addresses the *thermodynamic accounting of material throughput*. It is an attempt to measure the absolute physical toll of commodity production on a finite biosphere, independent of market pricing.
*   **Cradle-to-Cradle (C2C) Design**: A design philosophy that models human industry on nature’s processes, viewing materials as biological or technical nutrients that flow in continuous closed loops.
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: C2C addresses the *physical conservation of matter within a closed system*. It recognizes that in a finite biosphere, waste is a structural design failure resulting from linear production models.
*   **Benefit-Led Positioning**: The practice of marketing green products based on their primary functional benefits (e.g., cost savings, health, effectiveness) rather than their ecological credentials alone, to avoid negative consumer inferences.
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: This concept addresses *the structural requirement of capital accumulation to subordinate ecological values to exchange value*. To survive in a market, a green commodity must first compete on the terms of private utility.
*   **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) Pricing**: A pricing strategy that educates consumers to calculate the long-term operational, maintenance, and disposal costs of a product rather than focusing solely on the initial purchase price.
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: TCO pricing is an attempt to address *the structural externalization of ecological costs*. It seeks to bring the hidden, long-term material costs of resource consumption into the immediate financial calculations of the consumer.
*   **Reverse Distribution and Closed-Loop Logistics**: The design of distribution channels to facilitate the return of post-consumer waste, packaging, or obsolete products back to the manufacturer for recycling or reuse.
    *   *First-Principle Restatement*: This addresses *the physical and economic friction of reclaiming dispersed waste*. It is an attempt to reverse a physical supply chain designed for one-way throughput, grappling with the thermodynamic reality that dispersing matter is easy, while concentrating and reclaiming it is highly energy- and labor-intensive.

## 4. Internal Contradictions & Stress Points

### The Efficiency-Consumption Paradox
The lecture suffers from a profound internal contradiction regarding the ultimate goal of green marketing. In the final modules, the speaker admits that the "ultimate green behaviour... is to not consume at all," and notes that widespread adoption of "reduce, reuse, and recycle" behaviors would lead to "a slowing down of economies and the reduction of job opportunities." Yet, the entire preceding curriculum is dedicated to teaching marketers how to increase the sales, market share, and consumption of "green" commodities. 

The proposed mechanism—shifting the economy to "personalized services" to absorb displaced labor—fails on its own terms. The lecture notes that digital service giants like Google and Facebook require "tremendous amounts of energy" to power their physical server infrastructures. Thus, the lecture's claim that service-sector growth can decouple economic expansion from ecological degradation is contradicted by its own admission of the material reality of digital infrastructure. 
*   *Underlying First Principle*: *The biophysical limits of decoupling economic growth from material throughput*. The framework struggles with the thermodynamic reality that capital accumulation requires physical energy and resource expenditure, regardless of how "green" or "intangible" the commodity is branded.

### The Individualization of Structural Infrastructure
The lecture utilizes the Three-Factor Model (MAO) to explain why consumers fail to act sustainably, placing the analytical weight on individual "motivation" and "ability." However, when discussing "opportunity," the lecture repeatedly reveals that the true bottlenecks are structural and infrastructural—such as the lack of municipal recycling facilities, the time-poverty of urban workers, and the high cost of green alternatives. 

The lecture tasks individual competitive firms with solving these structural issues by making recycling "convenient" or offering reverse logistics. This is a logical leap: a single firm operating under competitive market pressures cannot build or fund public waste-management infrastructure at scale without destroying its own margins.
*   *Underlying First Principle*: *The structural failure of private market actors to provision public goods*. A competitive market system naturally penalizes individual firms that internalize the massive infrastructure costs required for genuine closed-loop recycling, while rewarding competitors that continue to externalize their waste.

### The "Win-Win" Profitability Illusion
The speaker asserts that "being sustainable will become the only way to be profitable" because stakeholders will demand it, rendering Milton Friedman's shareholder-primacy trade-off obsolete. However, the lecture's own tactical advice directly contradicts this optimistic claim. Marketers are warned that green products are more expensive to produce, require complex and costly LCAs, and suffer from consumer skepticism and negative quality inferences. 

If sustainability were naturally aligned with profitability, greenwashing would not be, as the lecture admits, a pervasive and highly attractive corporate practice. Greenwashing exists precisely because it is highly profitable to capture the *reputational premium* of sustainability without incurring the *material costs* of actual ecological restructuring.
*   *Underlying First Principle*: *The structural contradiction between short-term capital accumulation and long-term ecological reproduction*. The market price mechanism is inherently blind to long-term ecological health, prioritizing immediate financial returns over biophysical sustainability.

## 5. Ideological Audit

### The Consumer as the Primary Agent of Ecological Governance
The lecture takes for granted that the primary mechanism for ecological preservation is the aggregate of individual consumer choices in the marketplace. It frames ecological survival as a series of purchasing decisions (e.g., choosing cloth diapers over disposable ones, or buying a hybrid car).
*   *Why this is ideological*: This framing naturalizes the market as the only legitimate arena for social decision-making. It redefines the citizen as a "consumer," transforming a collective political problem (ecological collapse) into an individual purchasing problem.
*   *Who benefits*: Large polluting corporations benefit immensely. By shifting the responsibility of ecological stewardship onto the consumer's "motivation" and "ability," corporations escape direct regulatory limits on production while opening up lucrative new "green" market niches.
*   *What it forecloses*: It makes it impossible to think about collective, non-market solutions, such as state-enforced bans on single-use plastics, public investments in mass transit, or democratic planning of resource allocation.
*   *Suppressed First Principle*: *The displacement of collective political responsibility onto individual market transactions*.

### The Neutrality of the Market Mechanism
The lecture presents marketing tools—such as STP, branding, and pricing—as neutral, objective technologies that can be seamlessly redirected from promoting hyper-consumerism to saving the planet.
*   *Why this is ideological*: Marketing is not a neutral tool; it is an institutional apparatus developed specifically to manufacture demand and accelerate the throughput of commodities to prevent crises of capital overproduction. 
*   *Who benefits*: The marketing profession and corporate executives, whose roles are validated as socially necessary and ecologically heroic, shielding them from the critique that their industry is a primary driver of ecological degradation.
*   *What it forecloses*: The recognition that the structural imperative of marketing is to expand consumption, which is fundamentally incompatible with a finite biosphere.
*   *Suppressed First Principle*: *The commodification of survival*, where access to a clean environment and safe products is restricted to those who can afford the "green premium."

### The Infinite Compatibility of Capital Growth and Biophysical Limits
The lecture operates under the assumption that the "win-win-win" scenario is always achievable—that a firm can grow its profits indefinitely while restoring the environment.
*   *Why this is ideological*: It denies the physical laws of thermodynamics. Infinite growth on a finite planet is a physical impossibility. Framing this tension as a "creative design challenge" for marketers is a form of ecological mysticism.
*   *Who benefits*: Financial elites and capital owners, as it legitimizes the continued pursuit of compound quarterly growth under the banner of "sustainable development."
*   *What it forecloses*: Any discussion of degrowth, steady-state economics, or the necessity of placing absolute, non-negotiable limits on private capital accumulation.
*   *Suppressed First Principle*: *The thermodynamic impossibility of infinite capital expansion within a closed biophysical system*.

## 6. Materialist Reframe

### The Material Reality of Green Marketing
To understand why green marketing exists, we must invert the lecture's idealist claim that "better values produce better outcomes." Green marketing is not the result of a moral awakening among consumers and executives. It is a material strategy deployed by capital to navigate two structural pressures: **market saturation** and **regulatory threat**.

```
[Structural Pressures]
  ├── Market Saturation (Falling rate of profit in mature lines)
  └── Regulatory Threat (Anticipated state bans & carbon pricing)
        │
        ▼
[Capitalist Response: Green Marketing]
  ├── Capture "Green Premiums" (LOHAS/LOVOS niche extraction)
  └── Voluntary Self-Regulation (ISO 14000 to preempt binding state laws)
        │
        ▼
[Material Outcome]
  └── Continued capital accumulation and externalization of absolute ecological costs
```

In highly competitive, saturated markets, firms face a falling rate of profit. "Green" branding allows firms to segment the market and extract **premium rents** from affluent, educated consumers (the LOHAS segment) who have the financial capacity to absorb the higher costs. Materially, green marketing is a mechanism for capital self-preservation through niche differentiation.

Furthermore, voluntary frameworks like ISO 14001 and corporate sustainability reports are material instruments used to preempt binding state regulations. By demonstrating "voluntary compliance" and "market-led solutions," corporate coalitions successfully lobby governments to prevent direct state intervention, bans, or carbon taxes that would materially damage their bottom lines.
*   *Underlying First Principle*: *The search for premium rents and regulatory preemption in saturated markets*.

### The Structural Power of Capital over State and Stakeholders
The lecture claims that "stakeholders" (millennials, governments, investors) now hold equal power to shareholders, forcing firms to become sustainable. This ignores the material reality of **ownership and control**. 

Shareholders own the physical means of production and control the allocation of capital. A firm's managers are legally and structurally bound to prioritize the return on this capital. If a manager prioritizes absolute ecological health over profitability, capital will flee—stock prices will drop, credit will tighten, and the manager will be replaced. 

Governments, too, are structurally dependent on private capital investment to maintain employment and tax revenues. This dependency ensures that state regulations are routinely weakened or captured by industry lobbyists, resulting in voluntary, market-friendly certification schemes (like TUV Rheinland) rather than strict, legally enforced limits on resource extraction and waste production.
*   *Underlying First Principle*: *The structural dependency of social and state institutions on private capital accumulation*.

### The Material Friction of Reverse Logistics
The lecture suggests that firms should implement "reverse distribution" to make recycling convenient. In material terms, a capitalist firm is structured as a **linear throughput engine**. It extracts cheap raw materials, processes them using fossil energy, and distributes them to externalize the post-consumer waste onto municipal taxpayers and the biosphere.

Reclaiming, sorting, transporting, and reprocessing post-consumer waste (reverse logistics) is highly labor-intensive and thermodynamically inefficient compared to extracting virgin resources. Under competitive market conditions, a firm that genuinely internalizes these costs will face a severe competitive disadvantage against firms that continue to externalize their waste. Therefore, voluntary reverse logistics will always remain a marginal, public-relations-driven activity unless forced by absolute state mandates.
*   *Underlying First Principle*: *The thermodynamic and economic asymmetry between linear throughput and closed-loop recycling*.

## 7. What This Means for Making Change

If we reject the illusion that consumer choices and corporate marketing plans can resolve the ecological crisis, the path to genuine change shifts from the supermarket aisle to the arena of structural and material power.

### Real Levers of Change
*   **Direct Production-Side Regulation and Bans**: Historically, significant environmental victories have never been won by marketing campaigns or consumer choice. The elimination of chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) to save the ozone layer was achieved through the Montreal Protocol—a binding international treaty that legally banned the production of these chemicals. Change-makers must focus on passing legislation that directly outlaws destructive production practices, rather than trying to persuade consumers to buy "greener" alternatives.
    *   *First-Principle Application*: Addressing the *externalization of costs* requires the state to use its coercive power to shut down the production of ecologically destructive materials entirely, removing them from the market.
*   **De-commodification and Public Provisioning**: To bridge the "opportunity" gap identified in the MAO model, we must remove key ecological infrastructures from the market. This means organizing for massive public investment in municipal waste-management, zero-packaging public distribution systems, and free, electrified public transit. When sustainable choices are publicly provisioned and free, the "ability" and "opportunity" barriers disappear for the working class.
    *   *First-Principle Application*: Resolving the *structural constraints on individual agency* requires replacing private, profit-driven distribution with public, democratic provisioning.
*   **Collective Labor and Workplace Organizing**: The lecture notes that millennials care about the sustainability of the companies they work for. However, individual employees have no power. Real leverage lies in collective organization. Workers inside major corporations can organize to demand veto power over product design, supply chain sourcing, and environmental practices, backed by the threat of collective labor withdrawal (strikes).
    *   *First-Principle Application*: Shifting the *control of production* from private shareholders to the collective workers who operate the physical infrastructure.

### Structural Obstacles to Action
The primary obstacle is the **market imperative of competitive survival**. Activists and professionals must understand that corporate executives are not "bad people" who need better values; they are functionaries of a system that structurally demands cost externalization and continuous growth. Any voluntary initiative that threatens a firm's market share or profitability will be abandoned or reduced to greenwashing. Therefore, appeals to corporate conscience are a waste of political energy.

### Strategic Orientation for Insiders
For students and professionals operating within these systems, the task is not to become "better green marketers" who help corporations sell more products. Instead, they must use their access and technical fluency to:
1.  **Expose and Sabotage Greenwashing**: Use internal data to leak the material reality of corporate greenwashing to regulators, investigative journalists, and activist groups.
2.  **Design for Subversion**: Push for open-source product designs, universal repairability, and standardizations that actively undermine proprietary lock-in and planned obsolescence, even when it conflicts with the firm's desire for repeat purchases.
3.  **Build Coalition with Organized Labor**: Align corporate sustainability committees with unionized workers to demand structural, legally binding environmental covenants in collective bargaining agreements.

## 8. The Knowledge You Actually Need

To gain genuine analytical power over this subject, you must look outside the business school curriculum to disciplines that analyze the material mechanics of power, resources, and social change.

### 1. Ecological Economics
This discipline rejects the mainstream economic assumption that the market can grow infinitely, framing the economy instead as a physical subsystem of a finite, closed biosphere governed by the laws of thermodynamics.
*   *What it answers*: It explains why "green growth" is a physical impossibility and provides the tools to measure absolute material throughput rather than monetary GDP.
*   *Entry Point*: **Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen** (*The Entropy Law and the Economic Process*) or **Kate Raworth** (*Doughnut Economics*).
*   *First Principle*: *The economy as an open thermodynamic subsystem of a closed, finite biophysical system*.

### 2. Political Economy & Regulatory Theory
This field examines how political power and economic systems co-produce each other, focusing on how laws, property rights, and state institutions are structured to serve capital accumulation.
*   *What it answers*: It explains why voluntary corporate initiatives fail and how "regulatory capture" ensures that state agencies protect corporate profits over public and ecological health.
*   *Entry Point*: **Karl Polanyi** (*The Great Transformation*) or **Stigler’s theory of regulatory capture**.
*   *First Principle*: *The structural subordination of state regulatory power to the requirements of private capital accumulation*.

### 3. Labor History & Social Movement Theory
This discipline studies how structural reforms and social transformations have historically been won through collective struggle, institutional disruption, and organized labor.
*   *What it answers*: It dismantles the myth that social progress is driven by consumer demand or enlightened elites, showing instead that change requires organized, collective disruption of economic flows.
*   *Entry Point*: **Erik Olin Wright** (*How to Be an Anti-Capitalist in the Twenty-First Century*) or **Frances Fox Piven** (*Poor People's Movements*).
*   *First Principle*: *The leverage of organized collective disruption over the flow of capital*.

Together, they move the analyst from passive, consumer-blaming despair to active, structurally informed strategic action.

## 9. A Note to the Reader

Sitting in a lecture hall where the catastrophic destruction of our biosphere is discussed as a "marketing opportunity" or a "branding challenge" is a deeply alienating experience. If you feel a sense of frustration, cynicism, or exhaustion, you should recognize that **your frustration is rational**. It is the only sane response of an attentive mind witnessing a profound mismatch between the scale of a crisis and the triviality of the proposed solutions. 

The feeling of helplessness this education produces is not an accidental byproduct; it is functional. A framework that convinces you that the only way to save the planet is through your individual purchasing decisions effectively neutralizes you as a political actor. It isolates you, turns your anxiety inward as consumer guilt, and ensures you will not attempt to organize collective resistance to the structures of ownership and power that drive this destruction. Understanding that this despair is structurally functional is the first step toward reclaiming your power.

The frameworks you are being taught—LCA, C2C, TCO—are not entirely useless. They are corporate-friendly attempts to grapple with real, inescapable physical problems: the conservation of matter, the externalization of costs, and the limits of resource throughput. The failure of these marketing frameworks to solve these problems does not mean the problems do not exist. It means the need for structural, non-market solutions is more urgent than ever. You are not studying something irrelevant; you are studying the official, defensive response of a system trying to preserve itself in the face of its own biophysical limits.

The horizon of human organization is not fixed. The assumption that competitive, market-led capitalism is the only viable operating system for a complex society is historically false and empirically disproven. Around the world, alternative models have successfully addressed these first principles through structural, non-market mechanisms:
*   **The Mondragon Cooperative Network** in Spain demonstrates that worker-owned enterprises can operate at a massive scale across industrial sectors, prioritizing employment stability and democratic governance over shareholder extraction.
*   **The Kerala Development Model** in India has achieved high human development, health, and education outcomes comparable to wealthy Western nations at a fraction of the GDP and material throughput, driven by public investment and intense social mobilization.
*   **Nordic Co-determination Laws** show that worker power can be structurally embedded within firms, giving employees legal board representation and veto power over major corporate decisions.

These examples are not perfect, but they prove that we can organize our economic life around human and ecological survival rather than private capital accumulation.

You are being trained in the language, logic, and mechanics of institutions that wield immense power. This training is a tool. If you accept its ideological assumptions, you will be absorbed by the system, spending your career helping corporations greenwash their throughput. But if you maintain your analytical independence, you can use this training to understand the vulnerabilities of these institutions from the inside. 

Do not look for inspiration; look for orientation. The most honest form of hope is not the optimistic belief that everything will work out, but the clear-eyed understanding of the structure of the problem and the commitment to act collectively to change it. As Karl Marx famously wrote, "The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways; the point is to change it." The tools of analysis are now in your hands. The choice of how to use them is yours.

consciousness of the environment. Also, for green claims, employees, competitors, partners,
suppliers, and regulators may also be part of the audience, whether intended or not. Note the number
of different ways in which sustainability claims may not quite be telling the whole truth. Consequently,
there's almost an automatic reaction of scepticism when a company makes a sustainability claim. This

consumers is extremely important. When all the elements of the communication mix converge on the
sustainability message, the claims become much more believable. These include observable actions
and communications by the top leaders of the firm. Finally, sustainability communications need to go
beyond the typical sender-receiver mechanism. Being an active part of an environmentally-conscious
ecosystem is critical. Employees, partners and even loyal consumers could be part of the ecosystem.
Employees could be encouraged to create content or to be active on social media like LinkedIn and
Twitter, spreading awareness of a green marketer's sustainability initiatives.
Video 10: Distribution as Convenience
The main value addition of the distribution element of the marketing mix is to provide time and place
utility to the consumer. The marketer's key objective is to work on the opportunity part of the three-
part model of consumer behaviour. So, all distribution tactics for a green marketer could be seen as a
way to make it more convenient for a consumer to be a sustainable consumer. The classical view of
distribution is to see its main function as matching buyer and seller and carrying out the logistical
functions of gathering assortments, breaking bulk and facilitating payments and payment transfers.
For green products, a better way to frame distribution may be as focussed on creating convenience at
all touchpoints of consumer search, purchase, use, and, importantly, post-use journey. Brick and
mortar retailing has been severely challenged and is on the verge of breaking down across the world.
Famous legacy retailers, like department store brands, have entered bankruptcy. The fundamental
challenge has come from e-commerce competitors who don't face the cost of real estate and prime
locations and of training and maintaining trained retail staff. They also have a major advantage of
being able to offer large assortments on a 24*7 basis. This convenience of shopping online greatly has
provoked this large shift to e-commerce platforms by both consumers and small and large
manufacturers. Two interesting phenomena of separating the pre-purchase phase with the purchase
phase have become prevalent. One is called webrooming, consumers doing pre-purchase search on
the internet and then buying at a physical store. The other is the opposite, showrooming, doing pre -
purchase search in a physical store and then buying online. In both these cases, the focus is on
convenience, the convenience of search and the convenience of choice. Now, the increase purchase
online has led to a great impact on the environment. Products are shipped in individually packed boxes
across large geographical distances, making for a reality inefficient model in terms of energy use. New
green products first have to force their way into the retail shelf without availability. Of course, there

is no consideration even by the consumer. To compete, some physical stores are now open 24*7,
which means increased use of utilities without a large in-store traffic in off-peak hours. As service
businesses themselves, retailers have a crucial role to play in the marketing of green products. The
way they decide on minimum unit sizes for sale or selling loose products, enforcing sustainability
standards on the suppliers and logistic partners, making the store itself energy efficient. They can all
help in making purchase convenient to consumers while lowering the impact on the environment.
Large scale changes in modes of transport and making changes to the hardware and software used for
logistics can further enhance the sustainability of the distribution operation. Convenience in use is
more a responsibility of the manufacturer than of the distributor. Almost always, what is convenient
to the consumer is not necessarily good for the environment. The consumer's resources are not just
financial ones; often, they are the time available for consumption. For example, many electronics
products are on standby, so they can be switched on quickly, but they consume substantial energy
while on standby. Retailers can play a role in educating consumers about more sustainable behaviours
when the product is in use. For example, only using full loads in washing machines or cutting out the
car engine when idling. Now, this is one of the great challenges of changing behaviours, which have
been habitual for decades. Recycling behaviour is greatly influenced by convenience. The easier it is
to recycle, to sell, to reuse, to repair, the greater will be these consumer tendencies. Setting up
convenient recycling facilities for a variety of products beyond the common paper, plastic, glass
packaging, will go a long way in improving recycling rates. Supermarkets, post offices, petrol stations,
public libraries, schools are all well situated to provide depositories for recycling of a variety of

distribution automated machines, which can take deposits of cans or batteries in return for some

Video 11: Marketing Green Products as a Competitive Strategy
In this course, we have mainly focussed on understanding the marketing of green products in tactical
terms by putting together a marketing plan. We conclude by taking a much longer strategic view.
There are many other major trends that are sweeping the field of business today, which are also
relevant to the green marketer. Many societies in the developed world are ageing as birth rates go
down and lifespans increase. Telecommunications technology will continue to grow rapidly, in reach,
in speed and in capacity, enabling even more advanced ways of communicating. And many parts of
Asia will likely see high rates of growth in this century. The green marketer must also stay on top of
these trends, or else there is risk getting swept away by more alert competitors. Addressing the needs
of the elderly, using the latest available communication tools in an integrated way and understanding
the motivations and abilities, and time pressures of the Asian consumers, especially in megacities, will
be crucial as you develop a marketing strategy. All aspects of the running of a business contribute to
its environmental footprint. Marketing cannot be a lone function focussed on sustainability. Other
aspects of corporate strategy such as office location and design, employment and work practices, for
example, more work from home, reducing the need to commute, choosing suppliers and retailers, all
need to be focussed on reducing the impact on the environment. Nowwithout profits, of course, there
is no business, but without sustainability, there may be no profits. Shareholders, employees, suppliers,
partners and society at large will all demand attention to the environment. Aiming for sustainable
outcomes and outperforming competitors on this dimension will become a strategic goal. The focus
needs to be on designing win-win solutions particular to your circumstances. For example, a hotel in
Singapore discovered that the simple act of removing minibars from rooms and instead selling the

found it just as convenient to buy from the lobby on the way back into the hotel, and the green shop

Video 12: Module Summary
In this course, we covered some key ideas of the marketing of green products. We went through the
basics of consumer behaviour and then the classic marketing planning approach, covering
segmentation, positioning and the marketing mix variables. For each stage in the planning process,
we examined specific issues most relevant to green products. The most important takeaways from
this course are three big ideas. The marketing of green products does not start with the manufacture
and end with the sale. It starts with the production process and the sourcing of raw materials, and the
best case-scenario, it does not end as much as return to the supply chain, so there's a cycle, a life cycle
for every product. Every step in this process needs to consider issues of sustainability, because,
ultimately, the image of the brand as sustainable can be damaged by any one misstep in this process.
The ultimate goal of marketing green products win-win-win situation. The marketer wins by becoming
more profitable. The consumer wins by getting a better-quality product at a good price, accompanied
by the joyful feeling of having been a socially responsible consumer. Society benefits by lowering the
impact on the environment. In some ways, the idea of the marketing of green products can be
improved to the idea of the marketing of greener products, the ultimate green behaviour, after all,
maybe to not consume at all. That said, the relentless search for sustainable improvements in every
step of the marketing process can result in greener products, greener marketing practices, greener
consumer behaviour and a healthier and more sustainable world.
