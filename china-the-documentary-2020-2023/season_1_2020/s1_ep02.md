## Episode Introduction

This episode moves into the Warring States period [战国时期, Zhànguó Shíqī], when the old Zhou [周] order has not just weakened but broken into a hard world of rival states, permanent war, and intense intellectual competition. Through Mozi [墨子, Mòzǐ], Mencius [孟子, Mèngzǐ], and Zhuangzi [庄子, Zhuāngzǐ], it shows three very different answers to the same civilisational emergency: save the world through universal concern, reform it through humane rule, or step back from its madness altogether.

As you watch, keep three questions in mind. When politics becomes structurally violent, is the moral task to resist it, to humanise it, or to refuse its terms? Can ideas still shape history when rulers mainly want military advantage? And why did an age of breakdown and bloodshed produce some of the most enduring confidence in Chinese thought [中国思想, Zhōngguó sīxiǎng]?

## Where We Are and How We Got Here

We are in the 5th to 4th centuries BCE, in the Warring States period [战国, Zhànguó] of the Eastern Zhou [东周, Dōng Zhōu]. The episode ranges across the interstate world of Qi [齐国, Qíguó], Chu [楚国, Chǔguó], Wei [魏国, Wèiguó], Song [宋国, Sòngguó], and the intellectual centre of Jixia Academy [稷下学宫, Jìxià Xuégōng] at Linzi [临淄, Línzī]. Its main figures are Mozi [墨子, Mòzǐ], the anti-war activist and technician; Mencius [孟子, Mèngzǐ], the great Confucian [儒家, Rújiā] heir to Confucius [孔子, Kǒngzǐ]; and Zhuangzi [庄子, Zhuāngzǐ], the brilliant Daoist [道家, Dàojiā] voice of freedom beyond office.

We arrive here directly from the world of Confucius and Laozi [老子, Lǎozǐ]. In the previous episode, the problem was the hollowing-out of the Zhou ritual order [周礼, Zhōulǐ]: titles remained, ceremonies remained, but real power had slipped away from the old moral centre. Confucius responded by trying to restore order through ritual [礼, lǐ], moral cultivation, and proper roles; Laozi answered with suspicion toward striving, artificiality, and coercive rule, pointing instead toward the Way [道, dào]. Neither response resolved the political crisis. That unresolved tension is the starting point here.

What has changed is scale and hardness. The late Spring and Autumn world [春秋, Chūnqiū] still carried memories of a recoverable order. In the Warring States [战国], fragmentation has become systemic. Small states have been swallowed, and a handful of major powers now dominate: Qi [齐], Chu [楚], Yan [燕], Han [韩], Zhao [赵], Wei [魏], and Qin [秦]. Courts recruit talent aggressively, warfare becomes more organised and technical, diplomacy grows more ruthless, and thinkers now speak not only about virtue but about survival. The old question, “How do we restore the world?” becomes a sharper one: “What kind of world is even possible now?”

## The Ideas Underneath

The deepest assumption beneath this episode is that political disorder is also a crisis of human relations. Nobody here treats war as merely military. The struggle is over what people owe one another, what rulers are for, and whether society should be organised around kinship, usefulness, conquest, or inner freedom.

Mozi [墨子, Mòzǐ] begins from the claim that partiality is the root of chaos. If people care only for their own families, their own clans, their own states, then aggression follows naturally. His answer is universal care [兼爱, jiān’ài]: not sentimental love, but impartial moral concern extended beyond kin and status. That is why his opposition to offensive war [非攻, fēigōng] is so central. He is not just condemning cruelty; he is attacking the moral logic by which states excuse violence when it serves their own interest. His movement, Mohism [墨家, Mòjiā], embodies this ethic in disciplined action, technical expertise, and collective service. In him, Chinese thought briefly takes a strikingly practical, almost engineer-moralist form.

Mencius [孟子, Mèngzǐ] inherits Confucius’ belief that politics must be rooted in moral character, but he sharpens it for an age of stronger states and greater suffering. His key conviction is that humane government [仁政, rénzhèng] is not idealistic decoration; it is the real basis of legitimacy and strength. “The people are most important” [民为贵，社稷次之，君为轻, mín wéi guì, shèjì cì zhī, jūn wéi qīng] is radical because it places the welfare of the people above the ruler’s ego. Yet Mencius does not abandon graded human relationships. Against Mohist impartiality, he insists that moral life begins in family feeling and extends outward from there. For him, society is not saved by flattening human bonds but by cultivating them properly and governing in accordance with them.

Zhuangzi [庄子, Zhuāngzǐ], carrying forward the Daoist [道家, Dàojiā] current from Laozi [老子, Lǎozǐ], questions the entire structure within which these arguments occur. He is less interested in designing good government than in exposing how narrow, anxious, and self-trapping the pursuit of status can be. Through dream, parable, and irony, he dissolves rigid distinctions between success and failure, usefulness and uselessness, self and world. His famous images — the butterfly dream [庄周梦蝶, Zhuāng Zhōu mèng dié], the joy of fish [鱼之乐, yú zhī lè], the turtle refusing honour at court — all push toward one civilisational possibility that will never disappear in China: when the public world becomes morally intolerable, retreat is not necessarily cowardice. It can be wisdom.

## What Was At Stake

The central tension of the episode is whether an age built on power can still be redirected by moral vision. Mozi [墨子, Mòzǐ] and Mencius [孟子, Mèngzǐ] both answer yes, but in very different ways; Zhuangzi [庄子, Zhuāngzǐ] increasingly doubts the premise.

The first major pivot is Mozi’s intervention against Chu’s planned attack on Song [楚攻宋, Chǔ gōng Sòng]. This is more than an anecdote about a clever man stopping a war. It stages a crucial possibility in Chinese civilisation: that technical skill, moral courage, and organised conviction might restrain raw state violence. Mozi does not appeal to noble lineage or ritual prestige. He arrives as a commoner thinker with arguments, machines, disciples, and nerve. For a moment, ethical action seems able to interrupt geopolitics. But the larger consequence is bittersweet. Even a victory like this does not stop the age of war itself.

The second pivot is Mencius’ repeated failure at court, especially in Qi [齐国, Qíguó]. He gains prestige, admiration, and access to rulers such as King Xuan of Qi [齐宣王, Qí Xuānwáng], yet cannot convert courtesy into transformation. That gap is decisive. Rulers are willing to honour virtue, but not to let virtue govern strategy. The age wants results faster than benevolent rule [仁政, rénzhèng] can visibly deliver them. This is the point at which the scholar’s role changes again: not effective minister, but enduring conscience.

Zhuangzi’s refusal of office under King Wei of Chu [楚威王, Chǔ Wēiwáng] forms the counter-pivot. Where Mencius continues to knock on the doors of power, Zhuangzi declines entry altogether. That choice sets in motion one of the most durable patterns in Chinese intellectual life: the split between serving the world and preserving oneself from it. From this point onward, Chinese civilisation will repeatedly honour both the upright official and the principled recluse.

## Why This Still Matters

This episode matters because it fixed several of the deepest moral options in Chinese civilisation. Much of later Chinese life unfolds inside the triangle formed here by Confucian commitment, Daoist withdrawal, and the half-lost but unforgettable Mohist respect for practical usefulness and moral action.

The Confucian side is easiest to see. Mencius [孟子, Mèngzǐ] helped define the expectation that government should be paternal in the best sense: responsible for people’s livelihood, morally serious, and judged by whether ordinary people can live decently. The instinct that rulers must possess not just authority but moral legitimacy, and that officials should remonstrate against bad rule, owes much to this world. So too does the immense prestige of teaching. The idea that to educate talent is itself one of life’s great fulfillments — something Mencius states explicitly — still lives in the extraordinary esteem attached to scholarship, exams, teachers, and cultivated speech.

In family and social ethics, the argument against Mohist impartiality also mattered. Chinese culture largely did not choose equal love for all over differentiated obligations. Instead, it kept the Mencian and broader Confucian pattern in which care radiates outward from family ties. That helps explain why kinship duty, generational responsibility, elder respect, and role-based obligation remain so emotionally powerful in Chinese life, even after enormous modern change.

Zhuangzi’s inheritance is equally visible. Whenever Chinese literature, painting, or personal ethics turns toward mountains, rivers, wandering, spontaneity, or the refusal of rank, it is drawing from the imaginative world he helped create. The idea that one may leave office, reject promotion, or disengage from corrupt competition without losing dignity has deep roots here. So does a certain Chinese suspicion that hyper-ambition damages the spirit. Even people who have never read the Zhuangzi [庄子, Zhuāngzǐ] may still recognise its afterlife in the admiration for simplicity, plain friendship [君子之交淡如水, jūnzǐ zhī jiāo dàn rú shuǐ], and a life not wholly measured by success.

Mozi [墨子, Mòzǐ] survives less visibly, but not insignificantly. His esteem for frugality, utility, disciplined organisation, technical competence, and resistance to unjust aggression still resonates whenever Chinese culture admires the person who works for the common good without demanding recognition. He also stands as a reminder that classical Chinese thought was never only elegant moralism or mystical withdrawal; it also contained a hard, rational, almost proto-scientific concern with how things work and how suffering might practically be reduced.

Taken together, these figures show why “Chinese tradition” is never just one thing. It contains the minister and the exile, the teacher and the craftsman, the court debater and the fisherman by the river. That plurality was forged here, under the pressure of war.

战国风云下，孟轲的理念如此稀有珍贵，但显然有些不合时宜。所以尽管在稷下学宫获得了一些声望，当政者齐威王却并没有起用他。孟轲决定离开齐国，为心中的理想寻求安放之地。

## Zhuangzi and Freedom Beyond the World

为了让自己的观点被更多人听见，这个时代的人们选择用讲故事的方式表达思想。其中有一个人，故事讲得最好。

一天，他做了一个奇怪的梦，梦里他变成一只蝴蝶。醒来后，他分不清究竟是自己梦中变成了蝴蝶，还是蝴蝶梦中变成了自己。他的名字叫庄周 [Zhuangzi]。

庄周年轻时曾担任宋国的漆园小吏。后来宋康王发动宫廷政变，朱偃篡位，他便打消从政的念头，辞去职务回乡，靠编织草鞋为生。一个原本籍籍无名的乡野村夫，在天地中窥见了自然的奥秘。

春秋时期，老子的追随者创立道家 [Daoism]，他们用“道”来解释宇宙万物的构成和变化。庄周自成一派，成为道家的重要代表。他思想中的智慧与机锋，他用意象构建的哲学世界，让中国人用成百上千年的时间去猜测、领悟以及误解。

庄周大概是最喜欢鱼的哲学家。他常常钓鱼、吃鱼，也常想象自己就是一条鱼，自由自在地游走于天地之间。庄周说：“相濡以沫，不如相忘于江湖 [better to forget each other in the wide world than cling together in hardship]。”没钱了，他就去卖草鞋；肚子饿了，他就去钓鱼。身无长物，却逍遥快乐。

他西游渭东，游鲁南，有楚北，游赵飞扬。无边的想象力，让他写出了独一无二的文章，鸟兽虫鱼 [birds, beasts, insects, and fish] 都被他讲成了故事。那是一个无拘无束的、有趣的灵魂。

一天，庄周来到魏国看望老朋友惠施 [Huizi]。此时，惠施刚做了魏国的国相。有人对他说，庄周也是来谋取相位的。惠施吓了一跳，忙派人搜捕庄周。搜了三天三夜，庄周却突然出现在他面前。

他给老朋友讲了个故事：南方有种鸟叫鹓鶵 [mythical bird]，你知道吗？这种鸟不是梧桐树就不栖息，不是竹子所结的果实就不吃，不是甘泉就不饮。它从南海飞往北海时，路上遇到一只猫头鹰，嘴里叼着一只腐烂的老鼠。猫头鹰以为鹓鶵是来抢食的，就对着它大叫一声：“现在你也要为了你的魏国相位来吓唬我吗？”

惠施无言以对。即便如此，庄周仍然把惠施当做最好的朋友。这就是庄周，一个喜欢用寓言表达对世界看法的人。

庄周从未造访稷下学宫，也从不参与公开场合的口舌之争，不过他很喜欢与惠施辩论。一天，庄周和惠施来到濠水边，你看河里的鱼游得多么从容，它们真是快乐呀。

“你又不是鱼，你怎么知道它们快乐？”

“你又不是我，你怎么知道我不知道鱼的快乐呢？”

“我不是，你确实不能说了解；但你也不是鱼，那你也一定不了解鱼的快乐。你一开始就问我，我是怎么知道鱼的快乐，那说明你已经默认了我知道鱼是快乐的。”

庄周喜欢辩论，但他却认为辩论的最高境界就是什么都不说。继承老子一脉的庄周，推崇人与人之间平淡质朴的关系。他说：“君子之交淡如水 [the friendship of noble men is as plain as water]。”老子说过“上善若水 [the highest goodness is like water]”，庄周同样喜欢水的虚静恬淡。

山水之间，他畅想着“天地与我并生，而万物与我为一” [all things and I are one] 的逍遥游 [free and easy wandering]。

## Mencius’ Struggle and the Choice Between Retreat and Advance

就在庄周寄情于自然的时候，孟轲正怀揣儒家理想，辗转于一个又一个国君之间。他想尽自己最大的努力改变世界。

孟轲喜欢循循善诱，从浅显的道理入手，一点点把话题引向深处。在卫国，他用杀人做类比，希望能击中魏惠王的心。

“用刀子杀人和用棍子杀人有什么不同吗？”

“没什么不同。”

“那么，用刀子杀人和用政治杀人有什么不同吗？”

“没有不同。”

“厨房里有肥肉，马厩里有间，可是百姓却面带饥色，野外躺着饿死的人。这就是政治杀人啊。当政者还有什么资格做人民的父母？”

孟轲用心良苦，但魏惠王希望听到的并不是这些。当时各国国君希望听到的都不是这些。眼前是弱肉强食的现实环境，是各种迫在眉睫或隐藏于暗处的危险。他们急于找到快速强大的良策，以避免灭国之灾。正义、仁爱、亲民，在国君们看来，那是遥远而虚幻的，并且毫无力量。

这是各国关系最复杂、军事最活跃、局势最莫测的一个时期。战国七雄中，西边的秦国和东边的齐国最为强盛，形成东西对峙之势。于是各国之间形成了两种外交关系：一种是南北向的弱国结盟，合成一条纵线，联合起来抗击齐国或秦国，这被称为合纵 [vertical alliance]；另一种是位于中间的弱国和两大强国之一结盟，西联秦或东联齐，连成一条横线，攻击其他弱国，这被称为连横 [horizontal alliance]。

而那些鼓吹合纵或连横的游说者，如张仪 [Zhang Yi]、苏秦 [Su Qin]、公孙衍 [Gongsun Yan] 等，就有了一个特别的称谓：纵横家 [school of strategists]。纵横家在诸侯混战中如鱼得水，但儒者孟轲不关心异国如何打败另一国，他更同情人民的疾苦。

一次次碰壁后，孟轲再次将目光投向齐国。这个创建了稷下学宫的国家，应该是与众不同的，他觉得有必要再试一次。

这是孟轲第二次来到齐国。跟上一回遭受冷遇不同，这次他似乎来对了。他遇到一个看上去很赏识他的国君——新任国君齐宣王 [King Xuan of Qi] 是个雄心勃勃的年轻人，对孟轲十分尊敬，将他奉为客卿。

孟轲以为，机遇终于到了。他与齐宣王论证直陈社会积弊，大谈仁政主张，场面却有些尴尬。孟轲慷慨陈词，齐宣王有时竟无言以对，只好顾左右而言他。尽管常被问得狼狈不堪，齐宣王对孟轲依然算得上宽容。

在他眼里，孟轲是思想的先驱和贤德的楷模，他希望自己的臣民都以孟轲为榜样。但是，一心称霸的国君不会有兴趣整天谈论仁义道德。另有一个流派的知识和主张更加实用有效，齐宣王视其为振兴齐国的利器，那就是兵家 [military strategists]。

兵甲 [military power]，隐藏在战争背后；运筹帷幄，决胜千里。他们是战国时代不可或缺的狠角色。齐国先后出现过两位著名的兵家代表：一位是春秋晚期的孙武 [Sun Tzu]，其代表作《孙子兵法》被尊为兵学圣典；他的后代孙膑 [Sun Bin] 同样是兵家传奇。孙膑主张以进攻为主的战略，提出以寡胜众、以弱胜强的战法。这才是齐宣王心中可以雄霸天下的力量。

一天，孟轲又来拜见齐宣王，试图继续游说他放弃占领燕国的念头。公元前315年，燕国发生内乱，齐国趁机派兵伐燕，意欲吞并燕国。齐宣王曾请教孟轲的意见，问他能否取胜。孟轲回答：“燕民悦，则取之；燕民不悦，则勿取。”

但齐宣王早已有了自己的决定，他只是希望获得这位贤人的支持之名。孟轲想要再劝，齐宣王不想再听。在齐国群臣看来，孟轲口中的“民贵君轻”“仁者无敌”无疑是可笑而不识时务的。

孟轲意识到自己的期待再次落空，向齐宣王请辞。齐宣王表示挽留，被他谢绝了。孟轲似乎去意已决。走到齐国边境时，他突然停了下来，还停留了三天。

这可能是他一生中最漫长的三天。很难再遇到一个像齐宣王这样信任自己、礼遇自己的国君了。一旦离开，毕生的理想将再无实现的机会。只要齐宣王还有实行仁政的可能，哪怕希望再渺茫，他都愿意等待。

齐宣王的确想把孟轲留在身边，就像其他诸侯一样，他也承认孟轲的主张迂远而阔于事情 [too far removed from practical affairs]。各国都在变法图强，哪有时间留给收效缓慢的仁政呢？

第三天的黑夜降临，孟轲依然没有收到任何消息。睡意恍惚中，他感觉一切似幻似真：曾经的意气风发，与梦想背道而驰的现实，最后的一线希望，以及大失所望。孟轲知道，没必要再做无谓的等待了。

弟子冲由见他闷闷不乐，问道：“夫子不是说过君子不怨天，不尤人吗？”

孟轲轻叹一声，答道：“此一时也，彼一时也 [circumstances change with time]。”

太阳再次升起时，孟轲离开了齐国。他想，也许是天下大治的时机还没有到，那就继续走下去吧。“如果要平治天下，当今之世，舍我其谁 [if not me, then who]？”

孟轲走过的路，悲壮但豪迈。“君子无终食之间违仁，造次必于是，颠沛必于是 [a gentleman never departs from benevolence, even in haste or distress]。”孟轲仿佛看到，他用尽一生去追随的孔子就走在前方。或许先师孔子也走过同样的路。孟轲相信，终将有人继往开来。

“虽千万人，吾往矣 [even against thousands, I go forward]。”

孟轲不幸赶上了一个崇尚武力的时代。在诸侯国纷纷追求霸道而非王道 [rule by virtue] 的背景下，他竭力推行的仁政，还没有可以落地生根的土壤。但孟轲也并非全然不信。在富有创造力的壮年，他发现了自己此生的使命，这是一种莫大的幸事。

二十多年游历，他收获的并非只有挫败。对使命担当的全力以赴，虽然万分艰苦，却也充盈了他的精神世界。此后的岁月，孟轲回到家乡。

孟轲和先师孔子的命运轨迹仿佛一再重叠：同样的生不逢时，理想被现实撞得粉碎。孟轲也保持了和孔子同样的通达——达则兼济天下，穷则独善其身 [serve the world when successful; cultivate oneself when poor]。孔子留下坦荡荡的君子之风，孟轲坚定地修炼浩然正气 [vital moral energy]。他们从未放弃过自我人格的修养。

之后，两千多年里，做一个君子，成为中国读书人的毕生追求。除了继续教书，孟轲和一众弟子把自己关于政治、教育、哲学、伦理的思想观点写成了一本书——《孟子》 [Mencius]。春秋战国之际，由孔子弟子及再传弟子编纂的《论语》已经成书，和《孟子》一起成为后世儒家必修的孔孟之道 [the Confucian tradition of Confucius and Mencius]。

孟轲乐观地说：“父母俱在，兄弟无故，一乐也；仰不愧于天，俯不怍于人，二乐也；得天下英才而教育之，三乐也。”被后人铭记的君子三乐由此而来。

“天将降大任于是人也，必先苦其心志，劳其筋骨，饿其体肤，空乏其身 [Heaven will place great responsibility on a person and first test and temper him]。”这是孟轲留下的千古名言，这也是他跌宕人生的真实写照。

## The Return to Nature and the Enduring Voices

蒲水边，庄周又在钓鱼。两个楚国使者带着珍珠玉帛，奉楚威王之命来请庄周出仕，而且直接许以宰相之位。庄周的肚子饿得咕咕直叫，他看了看一无所获的鱼筐，对楚人讲了个故事：

“我听说楚王有只神龟，已经死了三千多年，楚王仍将它用布包着，用竹盒装着，珍藏于庙堂之内。请问这只龟，是宁愿死了留下骨头让人尊崇呢，还是宁愿活着拖着尾巴，自由自在地在烂泥中爬行呢？”

楚人想了想，回答：“愿意拖着尾巴在烂泥中爬行。”

他们已经知道庄周的答案了。进则仕，退则道 [advance into official life or retreat into the Way]，就这样，庄周成为中国历代知识分子的两重选择。

如果说孟轲教导世人如何在世事沉浮中保持赤子之心，那么庄周则为失意者开辟了一条心灵的归路。他们二人平生是否相见，后人无从得知。唯一可以确定的是，他们穿越庙堂，行过原野，老在江湖，最终都在内心找到了生命的归处。

创立于乱世的稷下学宫，很快就在战火中衰败。当后人凝视诸子百家的舞台，无不被那段时光的丰盈和璀璨所震撼。在那个众声喧哗的年代，中国诞生了一大批不同观念、不同主张的学术流派，波澜起伏，蔚为壮观。广为流传的至少有十家。他们在思辨中创造出令人目眩神迷的言语故事，留下了恒久流传的文化烙印。

由孔子和老子阐发的中国原生思想，沿着星河变幻的轨迹向前演进。在纷繁复杂的社会大潮中，在无休无止的观点论争中，在苦难中，在希望中，许多质朴而深邃的见解逐渐生根发芽，成长壮大，直至结出硕果。

有多少死亡，就有多少新生。那些喷薄而出的哲思和激情，如同新生命的蓬勃律动。一切才刚刚开始，却无比新鲜，无比生动。他们的自由飞扬，为中国文化注入了长久的自信和从容。他们的声音穿过战火纷飞的年代，穿过喧嚣与沉寂，直击中国人的心灵深处。
